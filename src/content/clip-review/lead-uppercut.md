## What a good rep looks like

The uppercut is a hook rotated into the vertical plane — same load, explode,
accelerate, follow through; same accelerating arc; the elbow stays close and the
fist travels a vertical circle instead of a horizontal one. The lead uppercut
shares its **entire loading phase with the lead hook**: lateral load onto the lead
leg, hips **opening**. That shared load is why the two combine so naturally.

## Assess in this order

1. **Lateral load and sink.** Weight loads laterally onto the lead leg exactly as
   for the hook, and the body sinks slightly to create the upward trajectory. The
   cross-body chains stretch during that lateral load.
2. **Hips open, driven from the ground.** The hips open to drive the rotation, the
   same as the hook and the jab. Power comes from hip rotation and upward momentum
   generated by the legs — never from lifting with the arm.
3. **Elbow close, shoulder down.** The elbow stays close to the body throughout,
   never winged out; the tight elbow channels force vertically instead of letting
   it dissipate outward. The cue is **shoulder down, arm up** — the shoulder stays
   neutral in the socket and only transfers power.
4. **Vertical accelerating arc.** Torso rotates around the spine, the chains carry
   that energy into the arm, and the bicep mediates a rapid acceleration as the arc
   radius shrinks — a gradual start with the whole body, then rapid tightening
   through impact. The shape pros produce is a "come here" motion, bringing the arm
   toward themselves.
5. **Impact and reset.** Grab the fist at impact. Instead of a forward lean, the
   body continues moving in the punch's direction — down to up. The posterior
   shoulder muscles brake the arm and bring it back.

## If / then

- **If the elbow flares out** → force dissipates outward instead of travelling
  vertically. Score and name it.
- **If the shoulder lifts or pops** → the arm is disconnected from the cross-body
  chains and it has become an arm punch. This is the fault to watch hardest for on
  this punch.
- **If the fighter squats and pushes straight up** → linear mechanics; body parts
  all contracting at once instead of a circular arc. Recommend the drill of
  swinging the straight arm out, rotating exaggeratedly with the hips to feel
  tension through the whole chain, then reducing the radius.
- **If the arm scoops without hip rotation underneath it** → the power is coming
  from the arm. Score Hip Explosion low.
- **If it looks like a dragon punch — a straight vertical line** → it should be a
  circular motion using the chains throughout the arm.

## Do not flag

- **Loading off a slip, a roll, or the end of a rear hand.** Legitimate entries
  into the load phase — anything that shifts weight laterally onto the lead leg
  primes this punch.
- **The body sinking before the punch.** That is the load, not a balance error.
- **Landing with the palm facing the fighter.** Correct — it recruits the wrist
  flexors.
- **Continuing to move the body up and through** rather than leaning forward.
- **The fist stopping dead at contact on a heavy bag.**
