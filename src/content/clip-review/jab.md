## What a good rep looks like

The jab is powered by the lateral line and the quads — the jab engine. It is not
an elbow extension. The fighter kicks off the back foot to flick the rear hip
*backwards*, which stretches the cross-body chains and slingshots the arm forward
on an accelerating arc. The hips **open**. Lateral movement in a bladed stance is
forward movement toward the target.

## Assess in this order

1. **Load.** Weight starts near 50/50, legs slightly loaded out of the hinged
   athletic stance. Two legitimate ways in: *natural loading* from ongoing weight
   shifting or movement, or *dynamic loading* — a quick relaxation of the rear leg
   so weight drops to it, then a bounce out of it. Look for the lead leg lifting
   and the lead arm/shoulder briefly relaxing as this happens.
2. **Hip flick.** Does the fighter push off the bottom and front-outside edge of
   the rear foot and flick the rear hip backwards? This must begin *before* the
   arm goes anywhere. Track hip markers against fist markers across frames.
3. **Slingshot.** As the body rotates and weight moves forward, does the arm
   launch in sequence — torso first, arm dragged after it? The arm should reach
   full acceleration crossing into the impact zone, not before.
4. **Arc.** The fist travels a slight arc, not a rail. The arc is what keeps
   tension between fist and body.
5. **Impact and reset.** Weight transfers to the lead leg (staying nearer 50/50
   if a rear hand follows), arm and shoulder drive all the way forward, fist grabs
   hard, and the arm rides a slightly downward trajectory into contact for
   shearing force. Then unwind, push off the lead leg, back to stance.

## If / then

- **If the arm extends before the rear hip flicks back** → this is the arm punch,
  the single most common jab fault. Score Hip Explosion low regardless of how the
  punch looks at contact. Recommend the hip rotation drill, 100 reps daily, in
  both orthodox and southpaw.
- **If the fist travels in a dead-straight line** → the arc and the fist-to-body
  tether are gone. Recommend shadowboxing the jab slowly, exaggerating the arc,
  feeling tension stay in the lead arm the whole way.
- **If there is no weight shift at all in the load** → no elastic energy is being
  stored. Score Loading low and fix this before anything downstream.
- **If the torso rotates hard, like a hook** → over-rotation. Say so directly.
- **If the fighter steps long and throws a rear hand from that stretched base**
  → base not recovered. Recommend getting weight back near 50/50 before the two.
- **If the fist is loose at contact** → recommend grabbing the fist hard at impact
  and landing on the last three knuckles.

## Do not flag

- **The lead arm dropping or relaxing slightly before the jab.** This is correct
  and deliberate — it relaxes the pec and arm so the punch can launch. It is not
  telegraphing. Never list it as an improvement.
- **Subtle body rotation.** The jab's rotation is small by design. Only flag
  rotation if there is essentially none, or if it is hook-sized.
- **The fist stopping dead at contact on a heavy bag.** Normal. Judge follow
  through by whether the hips and torso keep rotating and whether the bag moves.
- **Weight finishing near 50/50** when the fighter is loading up a follow-up
  punch. That is the correct end position, not a failure to transfer.
- **A slip, roll, or weight shift used as the load.** Loading out of defensive
  movement is legitimate — read it as Phase 1, not as a separate error.
