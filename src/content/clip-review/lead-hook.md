## What a good rep looks like

The hook is powered by a **lateral** weight load onto the lead leg and a dramatic
**opening** of the hips, driving a horizontal arc whose radius tightens through
impact. Weight transfers laterally past the centre line — and that crossing is the
moment of impact. It is thrown off a **flat foot** using glute-driven hip
extension, never off a pivot.

## Assess in this order

1. **Lateral load.** Weight shifts onto the lead leg, stretching the lateral line
   and the lead-side cross-body chains, with some knee and hip bend to explode
   from. The arm and shoulder relax briefly, the arm hanging so the upper pec
   pre-stretches. The body should look *alive* — a ready athlete, never stiff.
2. **Flat foot, glute drive.** The single most important check. The glute does two
   jobs at once: hip extension and hip rotation. Look for the ready position —
   angle in hip, angle in knee — straightening out and driving the hip forward,
   with the lead foot planted flat. A lead foot that pivots is the fault: the thigh
   just turns in place, no hip extension happens, and ground reaction force is lost.
3. **Hips – torso – arm.** As the body rotates the arm lags back, the shoulder
   girdle retracts, the elbow extends slightly, stretching the whole arm line from
   pec to fist. Then the pec rebounds and the arm snaps across the body with full
   shoulder-blade protraction.
4. **Tightening arc.** The elbow flexes — bicep contracting — to shrink the arc
   radius and add speed at impact, the same principle as pulling your arms in
   during a spin. The path follows a golden spiral: broad at first, then tightening
   fast. Landing should be mid-arc, not perpendicular, so the punch shears.
5. **Impact at the centre line.** The fist grabs exactly as the weight crosses the
   midline — not merely when weight transfers forward. The body keeps rotating
   through impact rather than leaning forward.
6. **Counter-rotation reset.** As the arm passes the centre line the pelvis
   counter-rotates — belt buckle back to pointing forward while the upper body
   finishes turning. This stops over-rotation and pre-stretches the cross-body
   chains, pre-loading a rear hand.

## If / then

- **If the lead foot pivots to generate torque** → say so directly and score Hip
  Explosion accordingly. Weight goes laterally with no foot supporting it, so the
  fighter finishes off balance and has to disengage before following up.
- **If the pelvis keeps rotating after impact with no counter-rotation** → the
  lead leg will pivot to make room and balance goes. Recommend the spaghetti-arms
  or broomstick turning drill.
- **If the arm is dropped or hung far back before the punch** → they are
  manufacturing stretch that hip rotation should be providing. It telegraphs badly.
  Look for whether the hips are actually rotating first.
- **If the shoulder pops early** → protraction must be the last movement of the
  acceleration phase, inside the impact window. An early pop breaks the chain.
- **If the fighter steps so the feet end up in a straight line** → poor stability,
  nothing behind them for support.
- **If they visibly need the bag to catch their balance** → recommend full-power
  hooks in shadowboxing, with nothing to lean on.

## Do not flag

- **Loading off a slip, a roll, or the end of a rear hand.** These are legitimate
  entries into the hook's load phase — the fighter is primed to launch a lead hook
  virtually any time weight shifts to the lead leg. Read defensive movement as
  Phase 1, not as an error or as a separate technique being performed badly.
- **The fist not turning over.** Correct. The hook is already travelling in an arc;
  turning over is only needed on the jab and straight to protect the elbow.
- **Landing with the palm facing the fighter.** That recruits the strong wrist
  flexors; it is the preferred position.
- **Continuing to rotate through impact instead of leaning forward.** Correct for
  hooks.
- **The fist stopping dead at contact on a heavy bag.**
