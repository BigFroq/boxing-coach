# Packaging options — three concrete models

**Status:** draft scaffold. **Form your own POV before the Discord DM.** You don't have to commit to anything — but Alex will want to react to a proposal rather than be asked "what do you think?"

The three models below are bounded by what's realistic given (a) Alex owns the underlying content, (b) you built the product, (c) the prototype is already functional. One-paragraph pitch per model, then tradeoffs.

---

## Model A — Standalone tool, revenue share

Ship the app as its own product under your branding, with Alex credited prominently and receiving a recurring share of revenue. Pricing: freemium (limited queries/month free, paid tier for unlimited + Clip Review + My Coach session history).

**For him:**
- Passive revenue on content already made.
- Brand exposure to users who haven't found YouTube yet.
- No operational burden — you run it.
- Clear path to sunsetting if it underperforms.

**For you:**
- Maximum upside if it takes off.
- Maximum risk: marketing, support, infra, churn are all yours.
- Revenue share tension if growth flattens.

**Split:** 15–25% to Alex feels fair given he's the entire substrate of value. Negotiable upward if he participates in content updates / shows it to his audience. Lower if the tool is white-label/unbranded.

**When this fits:** if Alex treats it as a product he's lending his IP to, not one he's operating. If his priority is revenue without operational responsibility.

---

## Model B — Course add-on (companion tool, not sold standalone)

The app becomes a free, logged-in-only companion to the Power Punching Blueprint PDF + course. Students who buy the Blueprint get a license key / magic-link login. Non-students can't access.

**For him:**
- Turns static content into an active coach — students can ask questions at 11pm about why their jab feels off instead of rewatching a 20-minute video.
- Raises perceived value of the Blueprint → potential price increase or churn reduction on the existing course.
- Clear ownership (it's *his* tool now, not yours) — fewer questions about the data and the brand.
- No separate product to support — it lives downstream of the course.

**For you:**
- No direct revenue, but the work ships + gets real users + is credited.
- Simplest story, least negotiation.
- Natural stepping-stone to Model A if the course add-on succeeds and he wants to expand reach.

**Deal shape:** one-time build fee (covers your time to polish + productionize) + retainer for content updates (~1–2 days/month as he releases new videos). Or pure equity-style arrangement: you both own it, he operates it, you get a % of the course price increase that's attributable to the tool.

**When this fits:** if Alex's priority is improving the *student* experience, not opening a new revenue line. Most likely to make him comfortable fastest since it's unambiguously his.

---

## Model C — License + walk away

Alex buys the code and vault pipeline outright. You hand over the repo, documentation, a short handoff session, and step away. He operates it however he wants.

**For him:**
- Full control, full upside, zero ongoing dependence on you.
- Clear IP position — everything is his, end of story.

**For you:**
- Cleanest exit. You get paid once, retain no ongoing work.
- You lose access to a product you're proud of. No portfolio upside.
- No participation in any success.

**Deal shape:** negotiate a one-time price that covers the build cost + a modest premium. Think 4–6 months of senior developer cost if he were to build this from scratch — in the €25–60k range depending on polish level at handoff.

**When this fits:** if Alex is uneasy about anyone else operating an app built on his content — even with revenue share. Or if you want to move on to the next project. This is the option with the cleanest conscience on both sides.

---

## My recommendation (for you to override)

**Lead with Model B in the DM.** It's the easiest for him to say yes to, the hardest for him to find objection with, and the most natural fit for his existing business. It also protects you from doing operational work you may not want. If he lights up on it and wants more reach, Model A becomes the next conversation. Model C is a fallback if he's fundamentally uncomfortable with any of this — at least you walk away paid instead of hearing "no thanks."

**What to actually say in the DM:** don't list three models. Say "I think this fits best as a companion to the Blueprint course — lets students ask questions and do sessions between your videos. Happy to discuss what that could look like, including revenue, or any model that feels right to you." Leaves him room to counter-propose without locking anyone in.

---

## Things to NOT negotiate in the DM

- Don't get into contracts, percentages, or specific numbers in the first reply. Get him interested → get on a call → negotiate there.
- Don't propose anything that sounds like "I'll own this and give you a cut." Frame it as his IP + your labor.
- Don't offer up code for free as a "just show me what you think" gesture. That collapses your leverage.
