## Part 1 — Maxim cheat sheet

One-liners, grouped thematically. These are the durable quotables that the RAG should be able to reach for at any moment.

### Power generation — the engine

- **Throw, don't push.** The hip and fist must never arrive at the target together.
- **Hips → torso → arm, into and through.** The cascade only works in that order; reverse it and you're muscling.
- **The shoulder transfers, it does not generate.** Same as throwing a baseball.
- **It starts at the toes.** The kinetic chain wraps the heel, runs up the back of the leg, into the glute.
- **Rotating the hip rotates the hip. Pivoting does not.** Separate mechanics; only one creates torque.

### Chain sequencing — why power isn't additive

- **Load and stretch *simultaneously*, not sequentially.** Alex's "secret tip" — don't shift weight, *then* stretch chains. Do both at once.
- **Arm lags behind.** The price you pay for acceleration is stretch in the pec.
- **Stretch before snap.** Every pro who hits hard leverages the stretch-shortening cycle across the cross-body chains.
- **Oblique → serratus → arm.** The bridge that converts rotational torque into linear extension.
- **Each joint faster than the last.** By the time energy reaches the fist, it has been amplified through every link.

### Trajectory & impact — the shape of the punch

- **Arc, not line.** A punch follows a tight spiral, not a bench-press path.
- **Reduce the radius to accelerate.** Figure-skater physics — bend the elbow, tighten the arc, speed goes up.
- **Land with shearing force.** Forward *and slightly down*, not straight in.
- **Last three knuckles, ring-finger first.** They channel force through the ulna straight to the humerus.
- **Straight wrist, fist clenched at impact.** Turns the forearm into a rigid club; takes the wrist out of the equation.
- **Weight transfers through, not to.** Accelerate past the target; don't stop at the surface.

### Style & strategy — the fight above the mechanics

- **Telegraphing is mostly a myth.** Attention and positional readiness decide what lands, not cosmetics.
- **Stay on the edge of the bubble.** Neutral weight, constant micro-adjustment, always one frame from striking.
- **Hold frame, or lose the fight before it starts.** Be the one forcing reactions.
- **Ring IQ can carry mediocre mechanics.** (Usyk.) It cannot replace them against the top.
- **Relax to contract.** A muscle at 60% resting tension can only fire the other 40%.
- **Strategic cheating builds real power.** Momentum and rhythm under load, not robotic isolation.

## Part 2 — The 22 core principles

Each entry: the maxim, Alex's framing, a direct source quote, vault links to where it's taught, fighter exemplars, and the mistake it corrects.

### Power generation

#### 1. Throw, Don't Push

Foundational. A punch is a throw, not a push — like a baseball pitch, golf swing, or tennis serve. Push mechanics put peak energy at the beginning of the movement; throw mechanics put peak acceleration at the middle-to-end. That difference compounds through every joint.

> "A punch, throwing a punch, follows most other types of athletic moves like throwing a baseball, swinging a racket like in tennis. A punch follows those same mechanics." — *How Devin Haney Can Improve His Punch Mechanics*

**Taught in:** [Throw vs Push](../../vault/concepts/throw-vs-push.md), [Throw vs Push Mechanics](../../vault/concepts/throw-vs-push-mechanics.md), [Linear Style Mechanics](../../vault/concepts/linear-style-mechanics.md), [Power Punching Blueprint](../../vault/drills/power-punching-blueprint.md). **Exemplars (+):** [Alex Pereira](../../vault/fighters/alex-pereira.md), [Charles Oliveira](../../vault/fighters/charles-oliveira.md). **Anti-exemplar (−):** [Devin Haney](../../vault/fighters/devin-haney.md) — "his fist and his hip are in lock step and they're landing at the same time." **Corrects:** The lock-step push. Hip and fist arriving together means zero relative acceleration.

#### 2. The Four Phases — Load, Explode, Accelerate, Follow Through

The central framework. Every punch decomposes into four sequential phases that stretch and contract the cross-body chains.

> "I talk about the four phases of the punch: load, explode, accelerate, follow through. Also, hip, torso, arm into and through. And Pereira does all of these things masterfully." — *How Alex Pereira TKO'd Magomed Ankalaev*

**Taught in:** [Four Phases of Punching](../../vault/concepts/four-phases-of-punching.md), [Load Phase](../../vault/phases/load-phase.md), [Explode Phase](../../vault/phases/explode-phase.md), [Accelerate Phase](../../vault/phases/accelerate-phase.md), [Follow Through Phase](../../vault/phases/follow-through-phase.md). **Exemplars:** [Alex Pereira](../../vault/fighters/alex-pereira.md), [Deontay Wilder](../../vault/fighters/deontay-wilder.md). **Corrects:** Treating the punch as one robotic motion; releasing tension too early; leading with the shoulder before the hip.

#### 3. Torque Is Power

Torque is rotational acceleration produced by the sequential stretch-and-contract of the kinetic chains. It is what distinguishes a throw/swing from a push — and it is what creates the peak power at the end of the movement rather than the beginning.

> "In sports where it's important to transfer high speed energy into another object (baseball, golf ball, tennis ball), the mechanics include rotational acceleration, or torque." — *Power Punching Blueprint, What is Power*

**Taught in:** [Torque](../../vault/concepts/torque.md), [Phase 2: Hip Torque](../../vault/phases/phase-2-hip-torque.md), [Kinetic Integrated Mechanics](../../vault/concepts/kinetic-integrated-mechanics.md). **Exemplar (+):** [Gervonta Davis](../../vault/fighters/gervonta-davis.md). **Anti-exemplar (−):** [Dmitry Bivol](../../vault/fighters/dmitry-bivol.md) — "Bivol will occasionally use a back to front or rotating movement… but doesn't create torque." **Corrects:** Linear/planar mechanics where peak energy is front-loaded.

#### 4. Hip Rotation Is the Engine

The hips are where rotational force originates before cascading through the torso and arm. Two distinct hip movements — opening (jab, hook, lead uppercut) and closing (cross, rear uppercut) — power every punch.

> "It starts with the hips. As your body rotates, you create tension in the chains and connective tissue that runs through the body." — *The Hidden Physics Behind Natural Punching Power*

**Taught in:** [Hip Rotation](../../vault/concepts/hip-rotation.md) — the single most-linked concept in the vault (34 inbound). **Exemplars:** [Gervonta Davis](../../vault/fighters/gervonta-davis.md), [Earnie Shavers](../../vault/fighters/earnie-shavers.md), [Mike Tyson](../../vault/fighters/mike-tyson.md). **Corrects:** Barely using any hip rotation (Spence-style); pivoting instead of rotating; the idea of "putting the shoulder into it."

#### 5. Ground Reaction Force, Not Pivoting

Power starts at the ground. GRF follows Newton's third law: push against the floor and the floor launches you. Pivoting merely rotates the femur in the hip socket — it's a consequence of rotation, not a cause.

> "If you want to create hip drive to power a punch, then create hip drive. Pivoting doesn't rotate the hip. Rotating the hip rotates the hip." — *This Mistake Is Making Your Uppercut Weak and Slow*

**Taught in:** [Ground Reaction Force](../../vault/concepts/ground-reaction-force.md), [Lateral Foot Push Drill](../../vault/drills/lateral-foot-push-drill.md), [Bounce Step](../../vault/drills/bounce-step.md). **Exemplars:** [Mike Tyson](../../vault/fighters/mike-tyson.md), [Floyd Mayweather Jr](../../vault/fighters/floyd-mayweather-jr.md). **Corrects:** "Squish the bug." Curling toes to grip the floor. Pivoting on the front foot during hooks.

### Chain sequencing

#### 6. Kinetic Chains — the Anatomy Trains Framework

Alex imports Thomas W. Meyers' *Anatomy Trains* vocabulary wholesale: Superficial Back/Front Lines, Lateral Line, Spiral Line, Front/Back Functional Lines, Arm Lines. Every punch recruits specific chains; arm punches recruit almost none.

> "You create torque with a hip rotation, then that gets your core rotating and as the energy transfers up your kinetic muscle chains to your arm and fist, it goes through the shoulder." — *Power Punching Blueprint, Common Misconceptions*

**Taught in:** [Kinetic Chains](../../vault/concepts/kinetic-chains.md), [Spiral Line](../../vault/concepts/spiral-line.md), [Front Functional Line](../../vault/concepts/front-functional-line.md), [Lateral Hip Muscles](../../vault/concepts/lateral-hip-muscles.md). **Exemplars:** [Alex Pereira](../../vault/fighters/alex-pereira.md), [Deontay Wilder](../../vault/fighters/deontay-wilder.md). **Corrects:** Treating the punch as a bench press; "putting the shoulder into it"; tensing the arm throughout the motion.

#### 7. Cross-Body Chains Transfer Hip Torque

The front functional line, back functional line, and spiral line run diagonally across the body. Hip rotation stretches them; they snap back violently to accelerate the arm. Most boxing instruction ignores them entirely.

> "When you maintain tension with those cross body chains through the entire trajectory, you don't just stop at the target, you're accelerating through it. By accelerating through your target, you can really move your mass through your opponent, exponentially increasing your power." — *Why Ciryl Gane's Punches Are Weak*

**Taught in:** [Cross Body Chains](../../vault/concepts/cross-body-chains.md) (28 inbound — second hub of the vault). **Exemplars:** [Alex Pereira](../../vault/fighters/alex-pereira.md), [Gervonta Davis](../../vault/fighters/gervonta-davis.md). **Corrects:** Leading with the deltoid. Arm-in-lockstep-with-hips. Over-pronating the fist (kills pec connection).

#### 8. The Stretch-Shortening Cycle

A rapid stretch followed by a powerful contraction produces faster, more ballistic movement than contraction alone. It operates not just in one muscle but across entire chains. This is the engine underneath every principle above.

> "If you create a fast stretch in your muscle, you can contract it with more force and get a more powerful movement. This is known as the stretch shortening cycle." — *How Ramon Dekkers Used His Kinetic Chains*

**Taught in:** [Stretch-Shortening Cycle](../../vault/concepts/stretch-shortening-cycle.md). **Exemplars:** [Alex Pereira](../../vault/fighters/alex-pereira.md), [Gervonta Davis](../../vault/fighters/gervonta-davis.md), [Deontay Wilder](../../vault/fighters/deontay-wilder.md). **Corrects:** Muscling the punch (tension cancels the stretch); statue-vs-slingshot strength training; treating load and chain-stretch as separate sequential steps.

#### 9. The Oblique-to-Serratus Bridge

The single most under-taught link in punching. Obliques stretch with hip rotation, feed energy into the serratus anterior, which protracts the scapula and channels rotational power into arm extension.

> "The serratus anterior protracts the scapula, extending the reach and channeling rotational energy into the arm. This is the bridge that converts rotational core energy into linear arm extension — without strong serratus anterior engagement, hip power is lost before it reaches the fist." — *Power Punching Blueprint*

**Taught in:** [Oblique to Serratus Connection](../../vault/concepts/oblique-to-serratus-connection.md), [Phase 3: Energy Transfer](../../vault/phases/phase-3-energy-transfer.md). **Corrects:** The arm-punch. The idea that stronger abs alone will fix the issue (serratus is where the hand-off happens).

#### 10. Kinetic Integrated Mechanics — the Umbrella

KIM is Alex's name for his whole system — SSC + kinetic chains + four phases working together. Not a separate principle so much as the label under which the other nine live.

> "Instead of pivoting and having a linear relationship between the hip and the shoulder… the way I show you how to do it utilizes a rapid stretch shortening cycle in the legs to power the hips in a rotation which creates a cascade of secondary and tertiary stretch shortening cycles in the torso and the arm." — *How ALL "Natural" Punchers Generate Power*

**Taught in:** [Kinetic Integrated Mechanics](../../vault/concepts/kinetic-integrated-mechanics.md). **Exemplars:** [Alex Pereira](../../vault/fighters/alex-pereira.md), [Charles Oliveira](../../vault/fighters/charles-oliveira.md), [Gervonta Davis](../../vault/fighters/gervonta-davis.md). **Corrects:** Moving hip and shoulder in a 1:1 linear ratio. "You are being misled by the huge majority of boxing trainers."

### Trajectory & impact

#### 11. Arc, Not Line

A punch travels in an arc because the kinetic chains wrap around the body in a spiral. An arc maintains tension throughout the trajectory; a straight line doesn't. Arc ≠ loop: a loop is wide and telegraphed, the arc is tight and efficient.

> "A straight line motion doesn't require or utilize continuous tension in your chains. An arc requires tension. You should think about the punch like a swing or a throw, not a bench press." — *Fedor's Legendary Power*

**Taught in:** [Arc Trajectory](../../vault/concepts/arc-trajectory.md). **Exemplars:** [Charles Oliveira](../../vault/fighters/charles-oliveira.md), [James Toney](../../vault/fighters/james-toney.md). **Corrects:** Looping punches. Straight-line bench-press mechanics. Confusing *arc* with *loop*.

#### 12. Reduce the Radius to Accelerate

Alex's figure-skater principle: tighten the arc and angular velocity rises. For hooks, bend the elbow to ~90°; the fist travels a tighter arc and accelerates faster. Same mechanism as pulling your arms in during a spin.

> "The way to create acceleration in the hook — you want to reduce the radius of the arc… and the moment of impact is the moment you pass the center line." — *How Tim Bradley Could Have Fixed His Punch Mechanics*

**Taught in:** [Hook Mechanics](../../vault/techniques/hook-mechanics.md), [Left Hook](../../vault/techniques/left-hook.md), [Arc Trajectory](../../vault/concepts/arc-trajectory.md). **Corrects:** Throwing a wide looping hook with a straight arm.

#### 13. Shearing Force at Impact

Land forward *and slightly down*, not straight in. Shearing force recruits the lats, teres major, obliques, and pecs through the cross-body chains. Axial force recruits the wrist.

> "By landing with shearing force, by throwing the punch in an arc, you're going to be recruiting all those muscles for extra stability." — *James Toney Straight Punch Mechanics*

**Taught in:** [Shearing Force](../../vault/concepts/shearing-force.md). **Exemplar:** [James Toney](../../vault/fighters/james-toney.md). **Corrects:** Axial/straight-in force. Bent wrist at landing. Flat single-plane trajectories.

#### 14. Knuckle Landing Pattern — Last Three First

Alex explicitly rejects the conventional "land on the index and middle knuckles" teaching. The middle, ring, and pinky knuckles are supported by the ulna, which connects directly to the humerus — a stronger skeletal line. Aim the ring-finger knuckle as primary contact.

> "These three knuckles are supported by the ulna, which connects directly to the humerus at the elbow — creating a solid structural line from fist to shoulder." — *Power Punching Blueprint*

**Taught in:** [Knuckle Landing Pattern](../../vault/concepts/knuckle-landing-pattern.md). **Corrects:** The conventional teaching that Alex considers weaker and injury-prone.

#### 15. Straight Wrist, Violent Grab at Impact

The fist stays loose through the trajectory, then clenches violently at contact. A straight wrist turns the forearm into a rigid club. A bent wrist kills weight transfer.

> "You want to grip your fist hard at impact. Why? Well, that creates a very solid platform and in fact it takes away your wrist from the equation and turns your forearm into a very solid and stable club at landing." — *How to Land Your Straight Punches*

**Taught in:** [Wrist Position at Impact](../../vault/concepts/wrist-position-at-impact.md), [Knuckle Landing Pattern](../../vault/concepts/knuckle-landing-pattern.md). **Corrects:** Bent-wrist landing. Pre-clenching the fist (kills speed). Prioritizing knuckle contact over wrist stability.

#### 16. Weight Transfer — Through, Not To

Weight transfer is a consequence of proper chain mechanics, not a deliberate step. Transfer weight *into and through* the opponent. Stopping at the surface wastes the body's full momentum.

> "Transferring your weight forward through your target should be the focus. The step is a consequence of the weight transfer, not the cause of power." — *Power Punching Blueprint, Common Misconceptions*

**Taught in:** [Weight Transfer](../../vault/concepts/weight-transfer.md). **Exemplar (+):** [Gervonta Davis](../../vault/fighters/gervonta-davis.md). **Anti-exemplar (−):** [Canelo Alvarez](../../vault/fighters/canelo-alvarez.md) — "weight too far back" leaves no room for transfer. **Corrects:** Focusing on the step. "Popping the shoulder" for power. Stopping at the target.

### Style & strategy

#### 17. The Telegraphing Myth

The public fear of "telegraphing" is, per Alex, mostly wrong. Pros universally use full kinetic chain mechanics. What determines whether a punch lands isn't cosmetic give-away — it's opponent attention and positional readiness, which you manipulate with feints, setups, and timing.

> "If telegraphing was a real issue, why is anyone getting hit? Why is anyone getting knocked out?" — *How Gerald McClellan Uses His Kinetic Chains*

**Taught in:** [Telegraphing](../../vault/concepts/telegraphing.md). **Exemplar:** [Mike Tyson](../../vault/fighters/mike-tyson.md) — the dinner-table story of manipulating attention. **Corrects:** Minimizing hip rotation out of telegraphing fear. Handicapping your own mechanics.

#### 18. Positional Readiness

Neutral weight balance, pelvis-forward-enough to push off the rear leg without resetting first. The difference between landing in 7 frames and needing 12 is positional readiness at the moment the opening appears.

> "Here's a clear example of what I mean about Canelo's weight being too far back to be able to respond to anything… He needs from this position to move his pelvis forward or move it down and forward in order to launch off that rear leg." — *Canelo vs Crawford Breakdown*

**Taught in:** [Positional Readiness](../../vault/concepts/positional-readiness.md). **Exemplar (+):** [Terence Crawford](../../vault/fighters/terence-crawford.md). **Anti-exemplar (−):** [Canelo Alvarez](../../vault/fighters/canelo-alvarez.md). **Corrects:** 60/40 rear-foot weighting ("amateur recommendation"). Being square and flat-footed. Throwing weight forward with no intent.

#### 19. Edge of the Bubble

The meta-skill of distance management. Live at the boundary of the opponent's effective range, constantly moving in and out, gauging space as active information.

> "One thing you can do while you're watching boxing is look at the space between the fighters. Try to get good at reading where the range is for the guys that you're watching." — *Canelo vs Crawford Breakdown*

**Taught in:** [Edge of the Bubble](../../vault/concepts/edge-of-the-bubble.md). **Exemplars:** [Terence Crawford](../../vault/fighters/terence-crawford.md), [Floyd Mayweather Jr](../../vault/fighters/floyd-mayweather-jr.md). **Corrects:** Static positioning. Not reading the space between fighters.

#### 20. Frame — Biomechanical and Psychological

Alex uses "frame" in two senses and explicitly connects them. Biomechanical: the athletic frame is shoulders + hips + core, balanced and functional. Psychological: who holds the dominant position in the interaction, forcing reactions instead of reacting.

> "Frame control means you're the one setting the tempo, the tone, setting the energy. The other person is responding to you. In one-on-one sports, it's massive. You don't want to be the one reacting. You want to be the one forcing the reactions." — *What Makes Oleksandr Usyk Great*

**Taught in:** [Frame](../../vault/concepts/frame.md), [Athletic Frame](../../vault/injury-prevention/athletic-frame.md). **Exemplar (+):** [Charles Oliveira](../../vault/fighters/charles-oliveira.md) (pre-fight vs Topuria). **Anti-exemplar (−):** [Ilia Topuria](../../vault/fighters/ilia-topuria.md) (same fight, pre-fight body language). **Corrects:** Getting pulled into the opponent's rhythm. Covering up instead of holding presence.

#### 21. Ring IQ

The cognitive/strategic dimension — distance gauging, disrupting, maintaining focus, reading openings. Can carry mediocre mechanics to a certain level; cannot replace them against the top.

> "His ring IQ and his skill level, his meta skills are so good, they essentially carry him throughout the fight with mechanics that leave a lot to be desired. Imagine if he had more integrated full body mechanics with the ring IQ he has. He would be an all-timer." — *What Makes Oleksandr Usyk Great*

**Taught in:** [Ring IQ](../../vault/concepts/ring-iq.md). **Exemplars:** [Terence Crawford](../../vault/fighters/terence-crawford.md). **Corrects:** Failing to adapt mid-fight. Emotional fighting (Oscar-vs-Floyd). Throwing high-commitment punches with no follow-up plan.

### Body & training foundations

#### 22. Relax to Contract + Strategic Cheating

Background tension kills power before the punch starts — a muscle at 60% resting tension can only fire the other 40%. The fix is partly corrective work (pec minor release, hip hinge, anti-extension core) and partly retraining movement patterns through "strategic cheating" — full-body momentum work at ~60% 1RM.

> "If a muscle has 100% capacity to contract and it's already 50-60% tight just from tension it only has that other 40% in theory to contract." — *Biomechanics Behind Big George Foreman's Legendary Punching Power*

> "If your lifts are robotic and segmented, you're training your body to disconnect force instead of transfer it. That's why most traditional strength programs don't build natural power." — *Kinetic Power Training*

**Taught in:** [Background Tension](../../vault/injury-prevention/background-tension.md), [Strategic Cheating](../../vault/concepts/strategic-cheating.md), [Pec Minor Release](../../vault/injury-prevention/pec-minor-release.md), [Hip Hinge Mechanics](../../vault/injury-prevention/hip-hinge-mechanics.md), [Shoulder Integrity](../../vault/injury-prevention/shoulder-integrity.md). **Exemplars:** [Earnie Shavers](../../vault/fighters/earnie-shavers.md) ("loose, relaxed muscles"), [Mike Tyson](../../vault/fighters/mike-tyson.md), [Floyd Mayweather Jr](../../vault/fighters/floyd-mayweather-jr.md) ("great resting muscle tension, which is one of the hallmarks of athleticism"). **Corrects:** Statue-mode strength training. Obsessing over isolated muscles. Plank/crunch programming.

## Part 3 — Teaching moves

The *meta-patterns* Alex uses to teach. None of these are tracked as explicit vault fields, but they surface repeatedly across notes. This is the pedagogical layer of his system — how he makes the mechanics stick.

**1. Myth correction first.** He names the bad model *before* teaching the good one, anchored in specific trainer clichés he hears. Examples: "squish the bug," "60/40 on the back foot," "put your shoulder into the punch," "pivot to rotate the hip," "turn your fist over on the hook," "step with your jab." Each one becomes a ghost he's exorcising. This is why the `## Common Mistakes` section exists on almost every concept note — it's structural to his teaching.

**2. Fighter-as-exemplar (positive and negative).** Every principle is anchored in named fighters. Positive: Pereira for kinetic chains, Davis for explosive hip-torso-arm sequencing, Tyson for glute-powered hip extension, Crawford for positional readiness + ring IQ, Mayweather for bounce step + pull counter, Shavers for hip-driven uppercuts. Negative: Haney (push the punch), Gane (lead with shoulder), Bradley (arm punch), Canelo (positional readiness failures), Jake Paul (tight guard, tricep-only jabs). The negative exemplars are as teaching-essential as the positives — see [Devin Haney](../../vault/fighters/devin-haney.md), [Ciryl Gane](../../vault/fighters/ciryl-gane.md), [Tim Bradley](../../vault/fighters/tim-bradley.md).

**3. Analogy to other throwing sports.** Baseball, golf, tennis — used as bypass around boxing-specific intuition. "A punch follows most other types of athletic moves like throwing a baseball, swinging a racket like in tennis." This does two things: it recruits the reader's existing motor knowledge and it makes "peak acceleration at the end" feel obvious rather than exotic.

**4. Anatomy-first framing.** Thomas W. Meyers' *Anatomy Trains* vocabulary — Spiral Line, Front Functional Line, Superficial Back Line, Lateral Line, Arm Lines — gives every principle a physiological substrate. The vault carries this through: each concept cites the specific chain involved, not a vague "core" or "hip."

**5. Frame-by-frame biomechanics.** Alex literally counts frames in video analysis. "In Bud's punch, from initiation to landing is seven frames. We'll count them. 1 2 3 4 5 6 7. That's 23 hundredths of a second." This does three things: it grounds abstract claims in observable reality, it teaches the viewer to see punches differently, and it justifies small mechanical changes with concrete speed differentials.

**6. Felt-sense self-experiment.** "When I mimic a pushed punch on the bag, imitating Haney's mechanics, it feels uncomfortable because I'm so not used to it." He uses his own body as the demonstration apparatus, and he invites the reader to do the same. This shows up repeatedly in the concept notes — the test for whether you're throwing or pushing isn't a coach's verdict, it's how it feels.

**7. One-inch calibration.** The loading visualization ("imagine a heavy weight pulling your pelvis straight down — sink about one inch") is Alex's calibration signature. Small, subtle, non-telegraphing. Recurs in [Heavy Weight Visualization](../../vault/drills/heavy-weight-visualization.md) and elsewhere. The principle: big cues produce big (telegraphed, wasteful) movements; small cues produce efficient ones.

**8. Paired contrast.** Every concept has a "this, not that." Throw / push. Arc / line. Rotate / pivot. Hips lead / hips lockstep. Relaxed / tense. Shearing / axial. This keeps the teaching precise — the principle is always defined against its most likely failure mode.

**9. Naming as diagnosis.** Specific errors get precise names: "the Dragon Punch uppercut," "lock-step," "side-delt raise hook," "squish the bug," "scrunching toes," "C-spine posture." Naming turns a vague feeling ("my uppercut is weak") into an actionable diagnosis ("you're doing a Dragon Punch").
