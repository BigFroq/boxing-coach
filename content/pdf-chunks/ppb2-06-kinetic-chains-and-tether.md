# PPB 2.0: Kinetic Chains (Plural) and the Kinetic Tether

**Source:** Power Punching Blueprint 2.0 by Alex Wiant DC (rough draft, Pages 8-9)

## You Don't Have One Kinetic Chain — You Have Many

A lot of people reference "the kinetic chain" as one thing. This creates conflicting or incorrect instruction — "almost but not quite," "small picture," incomplete instruction. **You don't have one kinetic chain. You have many**, and they all work in concert to begin, accelerate and deliver the punch.

Alex covers the chains as defined by **Thomas Myers** (a pioneer in mapping the human body / Anatomy Trains) — some as Myers described them, some combined for simplicity — then integrates them into punch mechanics.

## The Kinetic Tether

Your muscles are connected with **fascia**. This fascia forms continuous chains from your pelvis through your torso and into your arm — a connected system that allows force to be **transmitted and shaped** through the body.

**Maintaining continuous elastic tension in this system is what allows you to accelerate the punch, and apply your body mass to the punch.** It's the same underlying principle that allows someone to serve a tennis ball at 120 mph or throw a 100 mph fastball.

### The Tetherball Analogy

Think of tetherball: the rope keeps the ball connected to the pole while allowing it to accelerate in a circular path. **Your fascia and kinetic chains keep your fist connected to your torso while allowing it to accelerate through rotation.**

### Not Rigid, Not Loose — Elastic

- It's not a rigid connection and it's not a loose one. It's an **elastic tether**
- You need to be relaxed enough to utilize the elasticity, but connected enough to maintain and increase tension so you can **shape and accelerate the trajectory** of your punch
- It's the same tension/resistance you feel when throwing a ball, swinging a tennis racquet, or **moving your arm through water**
- Most obvious in an overhand punch (which closely resembles a throw), but applies to all punches

> [callout] The kinetic tether is an elastic system that transmits and amplifies force. Tension through this system allows you to accelerate the punch and connect your body mass to the punch.
