# PPB 2.0: Stance as Athletic Ready Position + Ground Reaction Force

**Source:** Power Punching Blueprint 2.0 by Alex Wiant DC (rough draft, Pages 3-6)

## Primer on Stance

Your stance is your foundation. Everything is built on top of it. It's also your connection into the ground — every athletic movement ultimately starts by pushing off the ground.

Your stance needs to function as an **athletic ready position**, rather than something totally unique to boxing. Think of defending someone in basketball: body slightly lowered, knees and hips slightly bent with a neutral spine, ready to react.

Warning: a lot of people "sit down" in their stance in a way that actually reduces readiness — they sit down with **posterior pelvic tilt and a collapsed or flexed lower back**. This is the opposite of a ready position and prevents free movement.

At its most fundamental level the boxing stance is an athletic ready position adapted to striking — the same "low ready" as defending in basketball, addressing a golf ball, or preparing to run to the next base.

## Ground Reaction Force (GRF)

Virtually every sport uses ground reaction force — pushing off the ground to generate movement:

- **Tennis serve**: player drops body weight by bending hips and knee, then pushes off the ground to begin a hip rotation that cascades through the body, accelerating arm and racquet
- **Baseball pitch**: pitcher loads the rear leg, lowers body over it, then pushes off to begin the hip rotation that cascades through body and arm
- **Golf swing**: golfer addresses with bent hips/knees, loads rear leg on the backswing, pushes off the rear leg to initiate the swing

Different sports, same principle. In each it's a whole-body coordinated movement that turns ground reaction force into a **sequential cascade of acceleration** through the hips, torso and arm, peaking at the moment of impact or release.

With a punch, instead of putting that speed into a ball, you transfer that speed, acceleration, and your body's weight into your opponent. You maximize fist velocity AND apply body mass to the target.

## Why "Linear" Pivoting Mechanics Fail

- Pivoting isn't pushing — it doesn't fully utilize the ground or leverage the posterior chain and glutes to drive the punch
- Linear mechanics rotate the body as a single unit — no meaningful **rotational separation between hips and torso**
- Hip-torso separation is what creates **torque** — rotational tension created by a stretch in the core muscles. This torque is what allows the rest of the body to accelerate
- The linear style treats the punch as an "all at once" movement using straight-line individual muscular effort — "this is simply not how the body is meant to move"

**The hips move first and everything else follows.**

> [callout] Ground reaction force initiates the hip movement. Torque turns that force into acceleration to produce a fast and powerful punch.

Every human movement — walking, swinging a golf club, throwing a punch — is a series of stretches and contractions. Using GRF optimally creates a sequence of stretches and contractions that begins from the ground and ends in the fist.
