# PPB 2.0: The Hip Hinge — Bamboo Spine, Stability, Wall Drill and Exercises

**Source:** Power Punching Blueprint 2.0 by Alex Wiant DC (rough draft, Pages 18-21)

A solid, balanced core is a **pre-requisite** for a solid hinge. The hip hinge is a universal athletic position that lets you fully utilize the posterior chain, core stability, and force transfer.

**Definition refresher:** bending at the hips while keeping a straight or neutral spine. "Straight doesn't mean flat — it means the spine is neutral with its curves intact."

## The Hinge in Forward/Rotational Force Transfer

- Pushing a person forward in football — force off the ground with legs and hips, core stabilized all around
- Pushing a weighted sled — straight spine while legs and hips do the work
- Golf swing at address, batting, tennis — all require a straight/neutral spine to efficiently create accelerating rotation

## Your Spine: Bamboo, Not Steel

Your spine is your **axis of rotation**. Your hips and pelvis turn your spine to accelerate a golf club, bat, racquet — and it's used the same way in a punch (even straights, which combine forward force transfer with spinal rotation).

The spine is 24 bones connected with joints, ligaments and muscles — it has structure AND flexibility. **Think of it like bamboo rather than a steel pole.** The hinge + core balance + intra-abdominal pressure let you use the spine's bamboo-like flexibility to stretch your core muscles and let them snap back to power punches. **If your spine were rigid like steel, you couldn't stretch your core and cross-body chains to create torque or acceleration.**

## Stability: The "Head Past Knee" Rule Has Nuance

"Don't put your head past your knee" is a good rule of thumb — but **elite fighters break this rule all the time** to aggressively transfer weight forward while staying balanced. The key is the hinge:

- When you hinge, your lead leg is in a much better position to support pelvis and torso as you transfer weight forward
- Glutes, low back and the entire posterior chain stabilize the body and arrest forward momentum
- Even if your head moves past your lead knee, the lead leg can still support your entire body
- Crucially: the hinge keeps you balanced **without relying on an impact to stop your forward momentum** — even on a total miss, you stay on balance and can reset

## How to Find and Train the Hinge

**Wall drill:**
- Stand ~6" from a wall, back to the wall
- Spine and core neutral — no excessive lower-back arch; stack ribs over pelvis, knees slightly bent
- Bend at knees and hips letting your butt go backwards to touch your back pockets to the wall
- Think of upper body, leg and lower leg as individual levers
- Don't let the knees travel forward from their starting position
- Weight distributed evenly on the full surface of your feet
- Practice 10 minutes daily until easy, natural, precise, well controlled

**Hip hinge exercises:**
- **Kettlebell swings** — "probably the highest ROI exercise for the hinge"; forced to keep a straight sturdy back; find a weight that takes some explosive hip movement
- **Romanian deadlifts** — dumbbells first for a more natural hinge; reverse direction at full hamstring stretch
- **Deadlift** — start with a trap bar for the most natural hinge; barbell later
- **Good mornings** — excellent but more lower-back focused
- **One-legged Romanian deadlift** — bodyweight or light dumbbell to start; rear leg lifts as counterbalance; feel lower back, glute and hamstring throughout; rear toe can stay down if balance is tough
- **Balancing with a hinge on a bosu ball** — hold the bottom position 30 seconds; partner tosses a tennis ball to challenge balance
