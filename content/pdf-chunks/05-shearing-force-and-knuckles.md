# Shearing Force and Knuckle Alignment

**Source:** Power Punching Blueprint by Alex Wiant DC (Pages 10-13)

## Shearing Force vs. Axial Force

Land with **shearing force, not axial force**. The trajectory of the punch should be **forward and slightly downward** at impact, not straight into the target.

- **Axial force** pushes straight into the target — this compresses the wrist and is more likely to cause injury
- **Shearing force** slides across the target with a forward-and-down trajectory — this distributes impact more safely and creates a more damaging effect on the target

## Fist Mechanics at Impact

- **Tighten the fist with a violent grab at impact** — the sudden clenching contracts all muscles in the forearm and hand simultaneously, creating a rigid structure at the moment of contact
- Keep the fist loose during the entire punch until the instant of impact
- The violent grab turns the loose, fast-moving hand into a solid, heavy weapon at exactly the right moment

## Latissimus Dorsi Role

The **latissimus dorsi** is heavily involved in both stability and power generation during the punch. The arc of the punch naturally engages the lats more than a straight-line pushing motion would. This is one of the strongest muscles in the body and a major reason why the arc trajectory produces more power.

## Knuckle Landing: Middle, Ring, and Pinky

Land with the **middle, ring, and pinky knuckles** — aim specifically for the **ring finger knuckle** as the primary point of contact.

- These three knuckles are supported by the **ulna**, which connects directly to the **humerus** at the elbow — creating a solid structural line from fist to shoulder
- The first two knuckles (index and middle) are supported by the **radius**, which does not attach directly to the humerus — this creates a weaker, less stable connection
- Landing on the last three knuckles channels force through a stronger skeletal pathway

## Arc Yes, Loop No

The punch follows an **arc** — but this is **NOT looping the punch**. A looped punch is a wide, telegraphed swing. The arc is a tight, efficient curve that naturally results from the spiral motion of the kinetic chains. The difference:

- **Arc**: tight, fast, generated by proper body mechanics and chain activation
- **Loop**: wide, slow, generated by swinging the arm in a large circle — this is incorrect and easily countered
