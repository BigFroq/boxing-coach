# Uppercut Mechanics

**Source:** Power Punching Blueprint by Alex Wiant DC (Pages 25-27)

## Lead Uppercut

The lead uppercut shares many loading similarities with the **hook**.

### Loading Phase

- Weight loads laterally to the lead leg, similar to the hook setup
- The body sinks slightly to create an upward trajectory
- Cross-body chains stretch during the lateral load

### Execution

- The hips **open** to drive the rotation (same as hook and jab)
- The **elbow stays close to the body** throughout the punch — do not let it flare out
- The punch drives **upward momentum** from the ground through the hips, core, and into the fist
- The tight elbow position keeps the force channeled vertically rather than dissipating outward
- The fist travels in a tight upward arc, accelerating through each joint in the chain

### Key Similarity to Hook

The lead uppercut and lead hook share the same **loading phase** (lateral weight shift, hip opening). The difference is the direction of the arc — horizontal for the hook, vertical for the uppercut. This means the two punches can be thrown from the same loaded position, making them effective in combination.

## Rear Uppercut

The rear uppercut shares loading similarities with the **straight punch**.

### Loading Phase

- Weight loads onto the **rear leg**, similar to the straight punch setup
- The rear hip is coiled back, pre-stretching the cross-body chains
- The body sinks to prepare for the upward trajectory

### Execution

- The hips **close** to drive the rotation (same as the straight punch)
- The rear hip drives forward and upward simultaneously
- The **elbow stays close** — same principle as the lead uppercut
- **Upward momentum** is generated from the ground through the closing hip rotation
- Energy transfers through the core, up through the shoulder, and into the rising fist

### Key Similarity to Straight Punch

The rear uppercut and straight punch share the same **loading phase** (rear leg weight shift, hip closing). The trajectory changes from horizontal (straight) to vertical (uppercut), but the power generation mechanism is identical. This means the two punches flow naturally in combination.

## Common Principles for Both Uppercuts

- **Elbow stays close to the body** at all times — never wing it out
- Power comes from **upward momentum** generated by the legs and hips, not by scooping with the arm
- The fist should feel like it is being driven upward by the body, not lifted by the shoulder
- The punch rises along a tight vertical arc, with the fist accelerating through impact
