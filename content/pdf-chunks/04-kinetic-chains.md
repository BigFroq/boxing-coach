# Kinetic Chains in Punching

**Source:** Power Punching Blueprint by Alex Wiant DC (Pages 8-10)

## Overview

The kinetic chains involved in punching are explained through the framework of **Anatomy Trains** by Thomas W. Meyers. Different myofascial chains activate during different phases of the punch.

## Phase 1 and 2 Chains (Loading and Hip Torque)

- **Superficial Back Line** — runs from the toes up the back of the body to the brow ridge; involved in the initial loading and weight transfer
- **Lateral Line** — runs along the side of the body; stabilizes during lateral weight shifts
- **Back Functional Line** — connects the latissimus dorsi across the thoracolumbar fascia to the opposite gluteus maximus; critical for cross-body power transfer
- **Front Functional Line** — connects the pectorals across the abdomen to the opposite hip; works in opposition to the back functional line
- **Superficial Front Line** — runs from the tops of the feet up the front of the body to the skull; stretches during the loading phase

## Phase 3 and 4 Chains (Energy Transfer and Follow Through)

- **Lateral Line** — continues to stabilize as energy transfers upward
- **Functional Lines** (front and back) — the primary power transmission chains from hip to opposite shoulder
- **Spiral Line** — wraps around the body in a helix pattern; responsible for the rotational quality of the punch
- **Arm Lines** — four distinct lines (superficial and deep, front and back) that channel energy from the shoulder through the arm to the fist

## Hip Rotation and Cross-Body Chains

When the hips rotate, the **cross-body chains are stretched**, creating a more significant contraction when they snap back. This is the mechanism that transfers hip torque into the upper body. The greater the stretch (within safe range), the more powerful the resulting contraction.

## The Arc of a Punch

A punch follows an **arc, not a straight line**. This is due to the spiral motion created by the kinetic chains wrapping around the body.

- The punch travels in a **spiral motion** from the hip through the core and out through the arm
- **Reducing the radius of the arc increases speed at the point of impact** — the same principle as a figure skater pulling their arms in to spin faster
- At the top of the arc, three things happen simultaneously:
  - **Rapid snap and rotation of the forearm** (pronation)
  - **Clenching of the fist** — sudden muscular contraction
  - **Weight transfer** lands at the peak of the arc for maximum force delivery
