# PPB 2.0: Module 6 — Phase 4: Impact and Follow Through

**Source:** Power Punching Blueprint 2.0 by Alex Wiant DC (rough draft, Pages 31-35)

**Biggest principle: your impact, and trajectory at impact, isn't strictly perpendicular to your target.** The goal is to recruit as many muscles as possible in the upper body and arm to provide a solid platform for weight transfer. At impact you need **rigidity in the entire upper body — from fist to core** — to move your weight through the target.

## Grabbing Your Fist

You have to **grip your fist hard at impact**. Grabbing your fist at impact turns your forearm into **a club** — a very solid object to make impact with.

## The Arc and the Vectors of Movement

The arc provides multiple vectors of movement at impact and enables shearing force:

- **Straight punches — three vectors**: back-to-front movement, rotational movement at the shoulder, and the arc trajectory downwards or towards your body
- **Looping punches — two vectors**: the direction of travel (side-to-side for hooks, down-to-up for uppercuts, oblique downward for the overhand), and the **decreasing radius of the arc** when possible

**The golden spiral:** all punches should loosely follow the "golden spiral" trajectory — starting as a gradual arc, then rapidly tightening/accelerating through impact. More obvious in looping punches, but even in straights the arc tightens dramatically with the fist grab.

## Shearing Force

When your fist lands while arcing, the target applies force in the direction opposite your fist's movement. This shearing force **forces you to recruit all your cross-body kinetic chains** to stabilize and continue the fist's trajectory through the target — this is how the wrist flexors stabilize your body weight at impact.

## Shoulder Protraction (The "Snap" / "Shoulder Pop")

The shoulder should go **all the way forward at impact** — the final component of acceleration through impact. It must come as the **final movement of the acceleration phase, not before the impact window**. People online tend to "pop" the shoulder prematurely, which **"breaks the chain"** during acceleration. Consistent tension in all chains through acceleration, with a final snap at impact via full shoulder protraction. Occurs in both straight and looping punches: shoulder and shoulder blade fully forward maximally recruits pec and serratus anterior to stabilize arm and weight transfer.

## Aggressive Body Lean ("Recoil Management")

Every action has an equal and opposite reaction — your target projects force back at you. Without accounting for it (especially vs a heavy bag), you bounce backwards instead of driving through. Like managing a firearm's recoil, you want slightly aggressive forward body lean (which the hip hinge already gives you) when landing straight punches.

With hooks: instead of forward lean you **continue rotating the body through impact**, often moving the body with the punch's direction (left/right for hooks, down-to-up for uppercuts). This is where the weight behind punches comes from — the continuous tension through arm and chains **connects your fist to your body mass even if you aren't fully transferring weight between your feet**.

## Amplitude

**Amplitude = the distance you transfer your weight into your target** — "punching through your target." Alex thinks "punch at a spot behind the target" is misleading: you must prepare for the point of impact by grabbing your fist — aiming behind the surface risks an unclenched fist at impact, reducing weight transfer. **Make impact at the surface so you have stability to drive your weight through.**

- Less amplitude/weight transfer = less commitment = faster punch, faster reset
- More amplitude = more commitment = slower overall punch and longer reset

## Reset

Just like a pitcher or tennis player unwinds after a throw or swing: at impact you're at maximum tension, then you release and unwind. The muscles opposite the punch engage to slow the arm and bring it back; the body rotates back to the stance ready to punch, defend, or disengage. Or roll straight into another punch — **the final position of one punch is usually the starting position of another** (jab end = rear hand start; rear hand end = hook start). You can also add a "beat" after a punch to further load tissues or move defensively.

## Troubleshooting Impact

- **Lacking power at impact** → usually loss of tension through acceleration. The punch is not "a stretch, contraction and cast with a noodle arm" — maintain upper-body chain tension through the entire arc. Phases 3-4 should feel like swinging a tennis racquet or moving your hand through water; try literally swinging your arm through water to feel full-chain tension from hand to chest
- **Not stabilizing the body / transferring weight at impact** → use an **isometric wall press**: lean into a wall with your fist as if making impact (jab or rear hand); feel the body stabilize the fist. For a hook: face a door frame, press your fist into the side of the wall, moving slightly laterally toward the pressing arm to recruit all leg muscles and cross-body chains
- **Resistance bands**: bands resist toward the end of the movement, forcing the cross-body chains to stabilize the arm. Straights: attach above and behind you. Hooks: attach to the side and very slightly forward (feels a little awkward but gives the full-body tension feeling)
