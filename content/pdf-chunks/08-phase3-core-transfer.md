# Phase 3: Core Energy Transfer and Impact Mechanics

**Source:** Power Punching Blueprint by Alex Wiant DC (Pages 17-20)

## Axis of Rotation

The **spine** serves as the axis of rotation for the entire punching motion. All rotational energy generated by the hips transfers through the core along this axis.

## Cross-Body Chain Activation

The cross-body chains (functional lines) are **stretched** during Phase 2 hip rotation, then **snap back with a powerful contraction**. This stretch-shortening cycle is the primary mechanism that transfers hip torque into the upper body and out through the arm.

## Exercise: The High Five

This exercise combines the bounce from Phase 1 with hip rotation from Phase 2, adding the arm extension of Phase 3.

1. Start with the **bounce** (weight shifting back and forth)
2. Add **hip rotation** as you shift weight
3. As the hips rotate, **throw the hand forward** as if giving someone a high five
4. Let the arm extend naturally from the hip and core rotation — do not muscle the arm forward
5. The hand should feel like it is being launched by the body, not pushed by the shoulder

## Secret Tip: Simultaneous Loading

**Load the cross-body chains simultaneously with the weight shift in Phase 1.** Rather than treating loading and chain stretching as separate sequential events, combine them. As you shift weight and sink, the cross-body chains are already being pre-stretched. This eliminates wasted time between phases and creates a more explosive release.

## The Arc and the Latissimus Dorsi

The arc trajectory of the punch **utilizes the strong latissimus dorsi more than a straight-line push would**. Since the lats are one of the most powerful muscles in the body, the arc path naturally recruits more power than pushing straight forward (which primarily uses the smaller pectoral and deltoid muscles).

## Loose Until Impact

**Keep the arms and hands LOOSE until the moment of impact.** This is critical because:

- Loose muscles can **contract more forcefully** than muscles that are already partially tensed
- Tension in the arm slows the punch down — tight muscles fight against each other
- A relaxed arm moves faster through the arc
- At impact, **squeeze the fist suddenly** — this contracts all arm muscles simultaneously, creating maximum rigidity at the exact moment of contact

## Three Knuckle Landing

Land with the **middle, ring, and pinky knuckles**. This is called the **"power line"** because it channels force through the **ulna** directly to the **humerus**.

### Why these knuckles:

- The ulna connects directly to the humerus at the elbow joint — creating a solid bone-to-bone force pathway
- The radius (which supports the first two knuckles) does **not** attach to the humerus — it creates a less stable, weaker connection

### Punch Stability Exercise

**Lean against a wall using your fist:**
1. First, lean on your **last three knuckles** (middle, ring, pinky) — notice the stability and solid feeling
2. Then lean on your **first two knuckles** (index, middle) — notice the wobble and instability
3. The difference is immediately obvious and demonstrates why the last three knuckles provide a stronger structural base

### Historical Agreement

Both **Bruce Lee** and **Jack Dempsey** independently advocated for landing with the last three knuckles. Despite very different fighting styles and eras, both recognized the biomechanical superiority of this alignment.

## Shearing Force Distribution

Landing with shearing force (forward and slightly downward) is **safer than axial loading** (straight into the target). Shearing distributes the impact force across a broader area of the fist and wrist, reducing the risk of fractures and sprains that are common with direct axial impact.
