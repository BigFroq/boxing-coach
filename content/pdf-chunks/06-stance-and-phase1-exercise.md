# Stance and Phase 1 Exercises

**Source:** Power Punching Blueprint by Alex Wiant DC (Pages 14-15)

## Stance Width

Use a **wider than shoulder width stance**. This is contrary to what many gym trainers teach, but the wider base provides critical advantages:

- **Lower center of gravity** — makes you more stable and harder to move
- Engages the **hip abductors** for powerful weight shifting between legs
- Allows for greater loading potential in Phase 1

**Mike Tyson** had very flexible hips and maintained a low center of gravity throughout his fights — this was a key element of his devastating power despite his shorter stature.

## Phase 1 Exercise: Heavy Weight Visualization

This exercise develops the loading mechanics of Phase 1.

1. Stand in your fighting stance (wider than shoulder width)
2. Imagine a **heavy weight pulling your pelvis straight down** toward the ground
3. **Sink about one inch** — not a deep squat, just a subtle drop
4. Feel the elastic potential energy loading into the muscles of the legs and hips
5. The movement should feel like gravity is pulling you into the loaded position

This trains the body to load naturally and efficiently without excessive movement that would telegraph the punch.

## Phase 1 Exercise: Bounce in Your Step

This exercise develops the rhythmic weight shifting that creates continuous loading and unloading.

1. Stand in your fighting stance
2. Begin **shifting your weight back and forth** between your lead and rear leg
3. Focus on the **loading and unloading of tissues** — feel the muscles stretch and compress with each shift
4. Maintain the bounce as a continuous, relaxed rhythm
5. The weight shift should feel natural and springy, not forced or mechanical

This creates a constant state of readiness where you are always partially loaded and can release into a punch from any point in the rhythm.

## Reference: Peter Ralston

Peter Ralston's book **"Principles of Effortless Power"** explores these concepts of loading and releasing energy through the body with minimum muscular effort and maximum efficiency. The idea is that power comes from proper body alignment and gravity, not from muscular force alone.
