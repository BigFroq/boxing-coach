# PPB 2.0: Punch Module 2 — Rear Hand Punch

**Source:** Power Punching Blueprint 2.0 by Alex Wiant DC (rough draft, Pages 39-41)

**Chains and muscles involved:**
- Phase 1-2: **posterior chain**
- Phase 3-4: **front functional line, spiral line, arm lines**

## Phase 1-2 — Load and Explode

Several ways to load the rear leg depending on the situation:

- **After the jab (easiest)**: the jab finishes with the hips open. From there, **just drive the hips closed** — with a slight hinge the leg is already loaded; use the glute to power the hips closed. Requires some weight still on the rear leg after the jab
- **Lead right**: you'll be a bit more square with the target — use a bounce step or quick load on the rear leg before exploding
- **Slip counter**: the slip itself loads the rear leg. Slip to your strong-side leg with the hinge incorporated to load most effectively
- **Pull counter**: the pull shifts weight to the back leg — just bounce your weight off the rear leg to initiate the explode phase

**You can launch the rear hand virtually any time you have some weight on your rear leg with a hip and knee bend.** If you relax arm and shoulder while adding the load, you stretch the pec for added acceleration in phase 3. Regardless of entry: use glute and posterior chain to push against the ground to create **hip drive and hip rotation** — the hinge puts the glutes in a great position to generate force.

## Phase 3 — Accelerate

**Remember: Hips – Torso – Arm.**

- As the hips begin to rotate, the torso lags a moment → **hip-torso separation generates torque**
- When the torso rebounds, the arm lags slightly → stretches the pec → **slingshot effect launching the arm forward**
- The body reaches its final position in a slightly aggressive forward-leaning hinge to manage the "equal and opposite" force of impact ("recoil management")
- **The lead leg stabilizes the pelvis** to prevent falling forward and acts as an anchor for the cross-body chains to maintain tension
- "You aren't throwing a wet, floppy noodle out there" — continuous tension through the kinetic tether shapes the shot in an arc, like a throw or swing

## Phase 4 — Impact and Follow Through

As the arm nears the impact zone: grab the fist and push arm and shoulder forward all the way. At impact the arm is moving **forward, rotating, and moving downward or towards your body** (three vectors). This forward shoulder position + fist grab + three-vector movement creates **shearing force** at impact, maximally recruiting the cross-body chains for a stable weight-transfer platform. Make impact with full-body tension and drive through with your desired amplitude.

## Reset / Recovery

Two main options after impact:

1. **Roll immediately into your hook** — weight is mostly on the lead leg, hips closed: the natural starting point for the hook
2. **Reset to neutral** — let the arm continue its natural arc after impact, then bring it quickly back. **The arm shouldn't come back the same way it went out** — let the punch release and unwind; the overall path is almost **elliptical** rather than straight out-and-back

## Rear Hand Summary

- Start after the jab or as a lead. After the jab: hips are open, use the glutes to drive them closed
- Extra rear-leg load + momentary rear-arm relaxation = simultaneous cross-body chain loading for extra speed/power
- A slip or pull can load the rear leg for a counter
- Hips turn → torso lags (core stretch) → torso rebounds → arm lags (pec stretch) → slingshot
- Maintain fist-to-body tension by accelerating the arm in an arc
- At impact: grab fist, drive arm/shoulder forward, transfer the majority of weight to the lead leg
- Lead leg supports the pelvis; drive into the target with forward body lean
- Then hook, or reset (release, unwind, back to stance)

## Key Takeaways

- **Sequencing: Hip – Torso – Arm**
- Aggressive forward body lean at impact supports weight transfer
- Driving arm and shoulder forward creates a stable foundation for impact
- Three vectors of movement: forward, rotational, downward/toward your body
- Maintain fist-body tension with the arc
- The lead leg keeps the body supported as long as you have a hinge
