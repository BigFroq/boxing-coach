# PPB 2.0: Module 4 — Phase 3: Acceleration (Spaghetti Arms, Shot Shaping, High Five)

**Source:** Power Punching Blueprint 2.0 by Alex Wiant DC (rough draft, Pages 28-30)

**Concept:** Allowing the rotation of your core to power and accelerate your arms. Your spine is your axis of rotation. As you explode with hip rotation and create stretch in the core through hip-torso separation, you snap back on that stretch to transfer energy through torso, shoulder and arm. Not a conscious contraction — it should feel like **throwing a ball or swinging a tennis racquet**; the stretch and rebound should feel easy and natural. These drills are for *feeling* how acceleration works, not perfect technique.

## Exercise 1: Spaghetti Arms

- Stand straight, shrug shoulders all the way up, hold 10 seconds, let them totally drop. Shake out the arms so they're loose like spaghetti
- Turn the body side to side, **initiating with the hips, leading with the athletic center**. Relaxed arms lag behind and swing freely — hip rotation is the only thing powering the arms
- Feel the upper body muscles stretch toward the end of each turn. Arms should feel attached to the body by **elastic ropes**; let them smack against the body with their momentum
- **Add hip counter-rotation**: just before the hips reach their end point, turn them back to center (belt buckle forward) — right about when the trailing arm passes in front of you. Feel a stretch in the core with the counter rotation; the arms should **accelerate** as you counter rotate. Let the counter rotation begin hip rotation the opposite way and let the arms follow — continuous side-to-side turning
- Then do it in your stance; add a dip at the end (quick hip/knee bend) to begin the counter rotation
- **Goal: feel the kinetic tether** — the fascial connection between fist and body. Fists like the tetherball; fascia keeps the fist connected without "muscling." Feel the arms pulling away from the body

## Exercise 2: Shot Shaping

Same turning motion, but add structure instead of noodle arms:

- Keep both **thumbs pointed up**. Feel a stretch in the pec as you reverse direction at the end of rotation — a gentle stretch, not forced. Now the core AND pec stretch with each hip counter-rotation
- As the arm swings after the counter-rotation, feel elastic tension in pec and bicep. Once used to the feeling, increase tension to begin **accelerating the arm and hand**
- Should feel like **swinging a tennis racquet or moving your arm through water**. There is **no separate shoulder movement** outside this continuous chain of tension
- You should feel like you could slap something with your palm as your hand crosses the center line
- **Taking it to the bag**: do the same drill at the heavy bag — as the arm swings after the stretch, accelerate your palm into the bag and slap it

## Exercise 3: The High Five

Purpose: feel how hips and torso accelerate the arm while maintaining chain tension through an arc.

- Throw a high five in the air to an imaginary person; continue past imaginary impact until the arm is straight out in front. Notice the arc — the arm stays **powered through the entire motion, just like a throw**
- Progressively lower the starting point until the hand starts at face height. Do both hands in stance — this is very similar to the trajectory of your straight punches
- **Combine with phases 1 and 2**: in stance, arms up. Quick dip and bounce off the rear leg (don't close hips fully yet). As you dip, relax arm and shoulder so they dip with the body — this momentary dip **stretches the upper pec fibers**. Then add the full phase-2 hip rotation and let it launch the high five. Keep the hand powered through the whole arc — "a throw doesn't stop halfway through the movement and neither should your high five." Exaggerate the arc if needed
- Also: load the lead arm with the rear leg and throw a lead-hand high five as you bounce forward (don't worry about jab hip-rotation details yet)
