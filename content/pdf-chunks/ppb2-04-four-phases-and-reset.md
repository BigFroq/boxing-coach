# PPB 2.0: The Four Phases of the Punch (+ Reset)

**Source:** Power Punching Blueprint 2.0 by Alex Wiant DC (rough draft, Pages 6-7)

Every swing or throw involves four phases: **load, explode, acceleration, follow through**. Impact or release occurs just between acceleration and follow through.

## Phase 1: Load

Loading of elastic energy into your leg muscles — the muscles connecting hips and pelvis to the ground. These may already be somewhat loaded from your stance (bent knees and hips), however you can add an additional **quick dip** to further bend hips and knees for even more elastic loading, speed and power.

## Phase 2: Explode

A quick push off from the ground to launch your hips and pelvis into rotation. Alex calls this **hip rotation or hip drive** — "we are driving our hips, not just 'pivoting.'" This creates a stretch in your core, elastically separating pelvis and torso — this is what creates **torque**.

## Phase 3: Acceleration

Compounds the first two phases into a **cascade of stretch shortening cycles** to facilitate elastic force transfer through the core and shoulder into the arm — maximizing the velocity of the ball in a pitch, the racquet in a serve, or your fist in a punch. This sequence of elastic loading and unloading (recoil/rebound) is what all other sports use when throwing or swinging.

## Impact / Release

The culmination of the acceleration phase: the point where you release the ball, make contact with bat or racquet, or make impact with your target.

## Phase 4: Follow Through

Your body's unwinding through impact or release. A punch is slightly different from other movements here: **when you make impact you need to stabilize your body weight as you make impact and then transfer your weight into and through your target.**

## Reset

A counter rotation of your hips, braking of your arm with your back and shoulder muscles, and a rapid movement back to your ready position. This phase can be a pure reset **or the beginning of another punch**.

Key KIMs principle: **the final position of one punch is the starting point of another.** This makes it natural to throw a rear hand after a jab, or a hook after your rear hand.
