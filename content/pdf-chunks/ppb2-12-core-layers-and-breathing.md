# PPB 2.0: The Core (Three Layers), Muscle Balance, and Diaphragmatic Breathing

**Source:** Power Punching Blueprint 2.0 by Alex Wiant DC (rough draft, Pages 15-18)

One of the biggest assets for athleticism is a balanced hips-and-core and the hip hinge. The hinge isn't just an "ability" — the loaded athletic posture it gives you needs to be the **default organizational mode of your body. It's your operating system** from which these mechanics are built.

The hinge unlocks: easier posterior chain use, easier movement through hip/leg loading-unloading, improved use of gravity in movement, access to ground reaction force, and an organized "base" athletic operating system. It must be accessible **on demand** — if pulled out of position, you reorganize back to the loaded ready position effortlessly, without conscious thought. (For a ground-up hip/core balancing manual, Alex points to his "Complete Guide to Shoulder and Hip Repair" at thepunchdoctor.com/hip-and-shoulder-repair.)

## The Three Layers of the Core

- **Front**: abs, obliques, transverse abdominus
- **Back**: spinal extensors, multifidus and other small spinal stabilizers
- **Middle** (often omitted, but important): the **hip flexors — psoas and iliacus**. Tight hip flexors and anterior hip muscles create imbalances that make the hinge hard to hold as a default position

## The Antenna / Guy Wires Analogy (Resting Muscle Tension)

A tall antenna is supported by guy wires pulling evenly from all sides. Think of your spine and skeleton like an antenna: **all your muscles should pull evenly on your internal structure**. When one muscle group contracts, opposing muscles stretch; the more balanced these opposing muscles are, the more smoothly you can load and unload them for fluid, athletic, explosive movement.

- Chronically short/tight muscles won't stretch or contract as effectively; their chronically stretched, weak counterparts lose the ability to load and explosively release elastic energy
- The old rubber band analogy: a worn-out rubber band doesn't snap back like a new one
- When muscles are balanced with even tension, they're **more sensitive to stretch** — already "loaded" against each other — so even small movements produce fast, powerful, coordinated contractions
- Perfection isn't the goal; **improving resting muscle tension is the goal**

## The Diaphragm and Intra-Abdominal Pressure

The diaphragm is the "roof" of your core; the core muscles are the sides; pelvis and pelvic floor are the bottom. This creates a **muscular cylinder you can fill with air using your diaphragm to create intra-abdominal pressure**.

- Creating intra-abdominal pressure does NOT mean holding your breath while punching
- **Diaphragmatic breathing** = primarily using the diaphragm to draw air in. You have two mechanisms to breathe: the diaphragm, and accessory muscles (neck, shoulders, chest) — accessory muscles should play only a small complementary role
- Breathing "down" (expanding abdomen and rib cage) rather than "up" into the chest creates internal resistance the core muscles can contract around — turning your core into a **"pressurized canister"** for stability, structure and force transfer

## The Diaphragm Feedforward Mechanism

When diaphragmatic breathing becomes automatic, **your body creates this pressurized canister predictively when you begin to throw a punch** — the "diaphragm feedforward mechanism." This lets you maintain a strong, stable platform for force transfer **no matter where you are in your breath cycle, even if you breathe all the way out** — i.e., punching independent of perfect breath timing.
