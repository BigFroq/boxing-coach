# PPB 2.0: Module 1 — Stance and Base (Triple Threat Stance)

**Source:** Power Punching Blueprint 2.0 by Alex Wiant DC (rough draft, Pages 21-24)

Your base is probably the most important thing to understand — the foundation upon which all mechanics and movements are built.

## Getting Your Stance

Stance is an **individual** thing based on your internal structure and anatomy. Hard guidelines can be counter-productive. You want foot width that **maximizes your ability to move** — the stance needs to be enabling, rather than limiting. A good starting point: about **shoulder width** between the feet.

## Too Narrow vs Too Wide — Gravity as a Movement Engine

Gravity is always pulling you down. When you lift one leg, gravity naturally pulls your body toward the unsupported side:

- **Too narrow**: your center of mass is too close to the support leg — gravity won't pull you down fast enough to assist movement
- **Too wide**: your lateral hip muscles are in a shortened position and can't move you laterally as well
- **Properly balanced**: gravitational pull + lateral hip muscles move you quickly and efficiently where you want to go

To get the **bouncy, mobile rhythm elite fighters have**, think about moving your weight **between your legs**, rather than just walking around. Manipulating your center of mass makes movement a series of loading and unloading of your legs using gravity and ground reaction force.

**The bouncing ball analogy:** a ball is elastic. You can load your legs and hips with elastic muscle energy through loading and unloading to "bounce" yourself between your legs. Moving your weight between your legs becomes a **plyometric movement** — this is how all the most elite fighters manage their movement in stance.

## The Triple Threat Stance

From basketball's "triple threat position" (ready to shoot, drive, or pass): in boxing your stance must be a **"triple threat stance"** — a position that easily allows you to **defend, attack, or move gracefully off the line to disengage**.

An athletic ready position shouldn't feel frozen or rigid — it should feel **alive and ready**. Even standing "still," your body constantly makes tiny adjustments to maintain balance by adjusting muscle tension in the "guy wires" that hold you up. These small tension changes load and unload muscles with every subtle movement — the feeling of a ready athlete. Think: basketball defender, hitter waiting on a pitch, tennis player receiving serve — relaxed but ready to explode.

**Setup:** Feet about shoulder width apart. Step back with one leg. Lead foot pointed in ~30°, rear foot out ~60°. Lower your center of mass about an inch with a slight knee and hip bend, straight back — a "loaded" position using the hip hinge in your stance. It's a rule of thumb; experiment with slightly wider or narrower.

## "Sitting Down on Your Punches" — Clarified

The "idea" of sitting down can cause undesirable things. The big problem: **lowering your body by posteriorly tilting your pelvis and flexing/rounding the lower back — this wrecks spinal neutrality.** Rounding the low back "bends" your axis of rotation and inhibits your ability to generate and transfer force. Lower yourself with a knee and hip bend while keeping neutrality.

## Exercise: Weight Shifting in Stance

- In stance, spine neutral, lower slightly with knee/hip bend. Imagine a huge weight attached to your **athletic center** pulling you down into the ground. Athletic "ready" position, 50/50 weight
- Feel your weight shift between your legs — load and unload smoothly. Hip and knee bend when loading, straighten slightly when pushing off. Go 50/50 → ~60/40. Practice small shifts (51/49) up to 75/25, both directions
- Progression: walk forward this way — load the rear leg, push off to move weight forward as you step with the lead leg; as the lead lands, bring the rear leg forward; repeat in a line, then backwards
