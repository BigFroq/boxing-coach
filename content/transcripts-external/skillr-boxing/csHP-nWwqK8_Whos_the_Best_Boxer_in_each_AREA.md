He'd dig an uppercut to the body, then follow up with the same hand to the head. His compact frame and explosive hips gave the punch crushing force in close quarters.

**9. Naseem Hamed** — Hamed's uppercut was wild, flashy, and devastating when it landed. He threw it from awkward angles, sometimes leaping in, which made it unpredictable. His reflexes allowed him to get away with risky delivery, stunning opponents who never saw it coming.

**10. Ricardo Lopez** — Lopez's uppercut was sharp, clean, and technically flawless. He often used his lead uppercut to counter opponents' shots coming straight through the middle. He could also use it effectively from the outside, landing from far away. It was one of his key knockout weapons.

### Solid Guard

A solid guard is the boxer's ability to effectively absorb and block punches by covering up. It involves adjusting the guard dynamically to cover all vulnerable areas and maintaining composure. A strong guard not only protects but also minimizes the impact of incoming punches without compromising stability or leaving openings.

**1. Arthur Abraham** — Abraham's style revolved around his high guard. He shelled up like a fortress, letting punches bounce off his gloves and arms until he found openings to explode. While sometimes criticized for being passive, his guard was extremely hard to crack. His calm demeanor and patience made his defense remarkably solid.

**2. Gennady Golovkin** — Golovkin's guard is subtle but highly effective in pressure fighting. He keeps a compact guard that blocks punches while he inches forward. His composure and distance control allow him to absorb shots without overreacting. Always defensively responsible, he keeps his chin down and maintains a crouched posture, adjusting just enough to block or parry incoming punches.

**3. James Toney** — Toney's guard wasn't a classic high guard, but a slick shoulder roll style defense. While often praised for his head movement, his solid guard was equally vital. He absorbed shots with subtle rolls and blocked the rest with relaxed composure. What made his guard so effective was his ability to catch punches on his arms and parry them with his gloves, staying calm and comfortable under fire without ever panicking.

**4. Joshua Clottey** — Clottey had one of the most frustrating guards of his era. He kept his gloves glued to his head and his elbows tightly tucked in a crouched position. His stability and composure behind the guard gave him underrated durability and frustrated even elite opponents.

**5. Julio Cesar Chavez** — Chavez used a tight, disciplined high guard. He blocked shots on his arms and gloves while subtly shifting his head off the center line. He often kept his hands protecting his body, moving just enough to block incoming punches. He was also great at parrying at mid-range. His ability to absorb heavy punches without overreacting made his guard exceptionally strong.

**6. Ken Norton** — Norton often employed a cross-arm guard, using it to smother and deflect punches. While he could be wild at times, when composed, he had one of the most solid cross-guards in history. He would close tightly and use his arms to block and parry shots, proving especially effective against head-hunting opponents.

**7. Marlon Starling** — Starling was famous for his airtight high guard. He could shut down combinations by tucking in tightly, leaving almost no target to hit. His dynamic high guard kept his elbows tucked and moved from side to side to parry punches. His compact, fluid defense frustrated opponents who couldn't break through it.

**8. Marvin Hagler** — Hagler was solid as a rock and excellent at blocking. He absorbed shots up close while applying pressure, rarely leaving himself open. His strong chin and stoic approach amplified his guard's effectiveness. He was also highly responsible while punching, maintaining balance between offense and defense. Hagler's guard was fundamentally sound and perfectly suited for walking opponents down safely.

**9. Tim Witherspoon** — Witherspoon was a master of the cross-arm guard, a defensive style many fighters struggle to figure out. He used it to parry most punches with ease, constantly adjusting his arm positions to cover openings. His dynamic guard, adapting to every situation, was extremely difficult to penetrate.

**10. Winky Wright** — Winky Wright had one of the most iconic high guards in boxing. He held a tight, disciplined shell that absorbed punishment on his arms and gloves with almost no openings. Even heavy hitters couldn't break through, often landing on elbows and forearms instead of cleanly connecting. Winky could effortlessly defend simply by raising his guard and blocking any incoming attack.

### Best Combinations

Good combinations will be sequences of punches thrown in succession where one punch naturally follows the next without hesitation or conscious thought. They are measured by how effectively they land on openings and how creative and unpredictable the patterns are.

**1. Alexis Arguello** — Arguello's combinations were built on perfect accuracy and a mix of head and body attacks. His gift wasn't speed, but precision. He often paused briefly between punches to calculate and target vulnerable areas. He would frequently mix in his trademark bolo punch to the body, forcing opponents to drop their guard so he could strike the head.

**2. Canelo Alvarez** — Canelo's combinations are compact and calculated, seamlessly blending head and body shots. Especially early in his career, his combinations were picture perfect — creative, unpredictable, and technically flawless. He would often feint before or even during combinations, making him even harder to read. His ability to vary rhythm, double up punches, and mix levels made his combinations truly exceptional.

**3. Dmitry Bivol** — Bivol's combinations are disciplined and efficient, built around straight punches. While Bivol often head-hunts, what makes his work remarkable is the smooth fluidity of his sequences. With relaxed shoulders, he throws long bursts of punches effortlessly without wasting energy. His control and precision allow him to throw in rapid sequences without overcommitting.

**4. Juan Manuel Marquez** — Marquez was a master combination puncher with some of the cleanest technique in boxing. He mixed his signature uppercuts with looping shots in seamless fluid combinations. His attacks flowed endlessly — every punch precise, sharp, and delivered with real power.

**5. Manny Pacquiao** — Pacquiao's combinations were explosive and relentless. He would launch one barrage after another without pause, overwhelming defenses with sheer speed and awkward angles. He didn't just throw combinations, he stormed through them, turning every exchange into chaos for his opponents.

**6. Marco Antonio Barrera** — Barrera's combinations blended aggression with surgical accuracy. Devastating at mid-range, he strung together hooks and uppercuts to systematically break down opponents. His seamless mix of body and head attacks within the same flurry made him both unpredictable and brutally effective.

**7. Muhammad Ali** — Ali's combinations were fast, fluid, and precise. Though he often head-hunted like Bivol, his accuracy and speed were extraordinary for a heavyweight. He mixed hooks, uppercuts, and his sharp right cross beautifully, overwhelming opponents with long, flowing sequences.

**8. Naoya Inoue** — Inoue's combinations are sharp, fast, and punishing. He often sets them up with a stiff jab before unleashing explosive three- or four-punch sequences. He mixes body and head shots perfectly with every punch carrying knockout intent. His timing, speed, and unpredictability make his combinations deadly.

**9. Sugar Ray Robinson** — Robinson's combinations set the gold standard — lightning fast, fluid, and devastating. He committed his entire body into every punch without losing rhythm or speed. Only one, hard-hitting and clever, he's got everything. He could unleash five or six punch flurries that combined grace, precision, and fight-ending power.

**10. Roman Gonzalez** — Chocolatito's combinations are endless — short, sharp punches delivered in relentless sequences. He blends hooks, uppercuts, and straight shots with perfect rhythm, especially on the inside. His ability to switch targets mid combination keeps opponents guessing. A true master of pressure fighting, he combines volume with surgical precision.

### Most Powerful Punches

Powerful punches will be defined as the ability to generate high impact force through a punch with minimal physical effort. This refers to the raw mechanical power behind each punch. It should not be confused with knockout ability, which can be influenced by other factors such as punch accuracy, timing, or speed. Powerful punches focus solely on the force generated — not whether the punch ends the fight, but how much potential damage is packed into each shot.

**1. Artur Beterbiev** — Beterbiev's power is overwhelming and consistent. Every shot breaks the opponent down little by little. Contrary to other nominees, Beterbiev is not a one-punch knockout artist. Instead, he throws short punches that, due to his heavy hands, still snap his opponent's head. Regardless of his style, his power is undeniable. When he commits to a punch, it's inevitable for his opponents to fall.

**2. David Tua** — Often considered the greatest non-world champion, David Tua had brutal punches. His stocky frame and leg strength gave him natural knockout power. He was a threat until the very last round because his power never faded. As we've seen, his hooks were especially devastating.

**3. Deontay Wilder** — Wilder's right hand is one of the most destructive punches in boxing history. While he may not be the most skilled and sometimes throws wild shots, his power makes up for it. He can end a fight with a single punch, even when behind on the scorecards. Every opponent respects his equalizer, no matter the situation.

**4. Earnie Shavers** — Shavers is often called the hardest puncher ever by those who faced him. His right hand could end the fight instantly, even if he was losing every round. Opponents described his shots as feeling like being hit with a hammer. Despite flaws in stamina and defense, his power alone made him an incredibly tough opponent.

**5. Gennady Golovkin** — Golovkin's power is heavy-handed, wearing opponents down until they break. He had effortless power and could even knock down opponents with shots to the arm. Every shot in his arsenal was heavy. And what made him so dangerous was his ability to generate power without loading up. He could throw devastating punches with little space, making him lethal at any range.

**6. George Foreman** — Foreman's punches carried raw, thudding force that looked slow but crushed anyone they touched. He didn't need speed — his sheer strength turned glancing blows into knockdowns. Later in his career, he would often drop opponents with what looked like arm punches. In both his primes, fighters described his power as unnatural, like being hit with a brick wall.

**7. Joe Louis** — Louis's power came from perfect fundamentals and precision. His punches were short, compact, and technically flawless, delivering maximum force with minimum space. His knockouts were clinical, and he consistently hurt much bigger opponents, showing just how big of a punch he packed.

**8. Julian Jackson** — Jackson's knockout power was pure explosiveness. He could shut the lights out with a single punch at any moment. His highlight reel is full of sudden, devastating one-punch KOs. He had unnatural power and would often predict the end of a fight when he connected clean, already pointing to the opponent before they hit the canvas.

**9. Mike Tyson** — Tyson's power was a blend of raw explosiveness and perfect technique. His uppercuts and hooks carried shocking force because of his ability to generate torque with his whole body. He could knock out heavyweights with short, compact punches. Combined with his speed and aggressive style, his power was terrifying.

**10. Sonny Liston** — Liston is often regarded as the most intimidating heavyweight in history. A big part of that was his raw power. His sheer physical strength translated into frightening punching force in every shot. His hooks and uppercuts carried bone-crunching power that few heavyweights could withstand. Facing Liston meant bracing for punishment from the very first exchange.
