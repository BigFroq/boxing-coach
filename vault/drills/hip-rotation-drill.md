# Hip Rotation Drill

## Summary
The Hip Rotation Drill is Alex's Phase 2 ("Explode") exercise — the opening and closing of the hips while pivoting on the **back** foot, done 100 reps per day in both orthodox and southpaw stances. Opening the hips powers your lead hand punches (jab, hook, lead uppercut); closing the hips powers your rear hand punches (cross, rear uppercut). It's the drill that loosens the hips, ingrains the correct mechanics into your neurology and muscle memory, and teaches you to use your glutes — not a front-foot pivot — to power the punch.

## What Alex Teaches

The explosion phase is what powers your punch, and it consists of two things: the rebound after the dip, and hip rotation. As Alex puts it, you load your tissues and then look for "an explosive rotation of the pelvis" — and that rotation is an opening and a closing of the hips. When you open the hips, you're moving your legs apart; when you close them, you're bringing them together. Opening the hips powers the **jab, hook, and lead uppercut**. Closing the hips powers the **straight punches (cross/right hand) and rear hand uppercut**. Forward pivot = closing hips = straight punch power. Backward pivot = opening hips = jab, hook, uppercut power.

Before he shows the drill, Alex dispels a myth you hear a lot in the gyms: pivoting on the front foot. He doesn't agree with it — "you punch like that, look where you are. You're not in a position to throw again. In fact, you're in a weird, off balance, awkward position." The greatest fighters keep that front foot more or less pointing forward, slightly to the side, and they **pivot with the back foot**. The pivot on the back foot is the engine — this is where the rotation initiates. This connects to his broader point that pivoting is secondary to hip drive: a strict pivot has no hip extension, you're just rotating your leg rather than pushing it back against the ground and using ground reaction force to drive the hip forward. If you drive the hip, you'll have to pivot — but the leg will pivot by itself.

To perform it: get your stance, maintain a slight hinge throughout, and while pivoting on the back foot, close the hips — bring them together — then open them. In the **closed position**, your belt buckle points all the way forward, weight is supported mostly by the lead leg (roughly 60:40), body angled slightly forward, both femurs internally rotated all the way. In the **open position**, you push off your lead leg and rotate as far as you can to the side while pivoting on the back foot, lead foot planted with the knee pointed in the direction of the toes, belt buckle ending up pointing about 90° to the side, weight fairly even or slightly rear, both femurs externally rotated all the way. Alex credits this exercise to **Peter Ralston, founder of Cheng Hsin**. Using a flat lead leg for the open phase utilizes ground reaction force and is necessary for a sequentially accelerating lead hook or uppercut; the closing phase with slight forward body lean is what you need later for stable impact and weight transfer.

Alex is explicit about the muscle doing the work: "the muscles that are powering this — you got your big glute muscle, which is responsible for the hip rotation. That's the primary one, and that's a big strong muscle." It's the same muscle responsible for people jumping and exploding off the sprinting blocks. The volume prescription is **100 reps per day in each stance, orthodox and southpaw** — "that hundred every day that you practice will turn to a couple thousand," and after a few thousand repetitions it becomes natural. Train both stances to develop both sides equally. Close them all the way, find and feel what that position feels like; open them, find what that position feels like. Once you get proficient at it, you don't really need to pivot that much — everything will come from the hips immediately.

The drill progresses. Add the dip-and-bounce: practice dipping on the back foot, bouncing off, then closing the hips all the way with some aggressive body lean supported by the lead leg (the lead leg uses the ground to stabilize the body and arrest forward movement); then from closed, dip and bounce off the lead leg and complete the opening movement. Combine into one seamless fluid movement. Throughout, **focus on leading with your athletic center** — the spot about two inches below your belly button in the center of your pelvis — because that's what creates the hip/torso separation necessary for generating torque and turning that torque into acceleration. When you're actually punching, the movement may be much more subtle; the exaggeration now is training you to use your glutes to power your punches. Alex also warns that some students rotate too far laterally over the lead leg and lose balance — the closed position should feel **very stable**, and the fix is almost a one-legged "good morning" with a dumbbell, kettlebell or light barbell, feeling the low back and especially the lead-leg glute engage. That closed position is where you'll be after throwing your rear hand punches.

## Common Mistakes
- **Pivoting on the front foot to power the punch.** It leaves you off balance and out of position to throw again. Pivot on the back foot instead; the greatest fighters keep the front foot more or less pointed forward.
- **Treating the pivot as the power source.** "The pivot is secondary. It's a symptom of the cause." Drive the hip; the leg will pivot by itself.
- **Rotating too far laterally over the lead leg in the closed position and losing balance.** The closed position should feel very stable — fix it with the weighted one-legged "good morning" variation.
- **Not doing enough volume, or only training one stance.** Build to 100 reps daily in *both* orthodox and southpaw; the mechanics only become second nature after a few thousand reps.
- **Rounding the lower back / tucking the pelvis instead of maintaining a slight hinge** throughout the movement.
