# Wall Hip Hinge Drill

## Summary
The wall hip hinge drill is Alex's foundational baseline exercise for finding and training a proper hip hinge — standing about six inches from a wall with your back to it, bending at the knees and hips until your back pockets touch the wall without letting your knees travel forward. It is the beginning and fundamental baseline that must be in place before other things fall into place, because the hinge is what loads the glutes, keeps the spine neutral as your axis of rotation, and lets the lead leg support you when you transfer weight forward. Alex is explicit that it's harder than it looks and takes time to master — he practices it with people in his office and says it takes a little while to really get it.

## What Alex Teaches
The setup is precise. Stand about shoulder width with your feet roughly six inches away from a wall, back to the wall, with a little bit of a bend in the knees. Spine and core stay neutral — no excessive lower-back arch. As Alex puts it, "You don't want to be too archy, right? Cuz that's going to stretch your abs. They're not going to be as responsive." Ribs stack over hips. Then you bend at the knees and hips and let your butt go backwards until your back pockets touch the wall, without moving your knees forward from where they started and without your knees traveling over your toes. Think of your upper body, leg and lower leg as individual levers. Weight stays distributed evenly across the full surface of your feet. Alex recommends videotaping yourself to make sure the spine stays nice and tight, and practicing roughly ten minutes daily until the movement is easy, natural, precise and well controlled.

The reason this drill matters is that a hip hinge means bending at the hips while keeping a straight or neutral spine — and "straight doesn't mean flat — it means the spine is neutral with its curves intact." Alex frames the hinge as the loaded position from his load, explode, accelerate, follow through sequence: "load is slightly lowering your tissues just like you do before you jump. That's the exact position that you want to be in. It's that position where you drop down just an inch, keeping your core nice and neutral with some glute stretch so you can jump. It's the same sort of position you might be in if you are guarding someone in basketball." Lowering your body with a hinge puts the glutes in a loaded and slightly stretched position so you're ready to explode for rotational power and back-to-front power. Keeping a straight spine gives you your axis of rotation. And it prevents excessive shearing forces and load on your discs, protecting the lower back.

Alex contrasts this against the failure mode constantly through fight footage. Floyd against Philip Ndou shows a traditional hinge position with a nice straight neutral lower back that Floyd then uses to explode with forward and rotational power; Ndou has the C-shaped curve with lumbar flexion that limits his mobility, explosive speed and power. Same comparison with Bud versus Canelo — Canelo walks in with that C-shaped posture and posterior pelvic tilt, and because the pelvis is rotated posteriorly, that bone structure limits how far the back leg can extend behind him. Canelo's back leg sits under his hip, he's frequently put off balance, and when he needs to launch he first has to move his leg back — an extra hundredths of a second that could have made the punch land. "You simply can't generate forward explosive power with lumbar flexion and a tucked pelvis."

The hinge is also what makes you stable. "Don't put your head past your knee" is a good rule of thumb, but elite fighters break it all the time to aggressively transfer weight forward while staying balanced. When you hinge, the lead leg is in a much better position to support the pelvis and torso as weight transfers forward, and the glutes, low back and entire posterior chain arrest forward momentum. Crucially, the hinge keeps you balanced without relying on an impact to stop your forward momentum — even on a total miss you stay on balance and can reset. Alex adds a loaded feel drill: hold a dumbbell or kettlebell (or put a barbell on your shoulders), get into your stance, lean forward with a hinge and feel the lead leg support your body — lower back engaged, glute engaged, even quad and hamstring. Do it in both stances.

You don't have to be in a super hinged position all the time — "obviously, that's not reasonable" — but you want the muscle memory down so you can get there on demand. No matter what position you're in, even bending at the waist to get out of the way of a punch, you can find that neutral spine again and launch an offensive punch. Alex connects the wall drill outward to the whole training system: hip hinge exercises like kettlebell swings ("probably the highest ROI exercise for the hinge"), Romanian deadlifts, trap-bar deadlifts, good mornings, one-legged Romanian deadlifts, and hinging on a bosu ball. He also points to his ready to strike video series for the small deep postural muscles — the multifidus and other stabilizers — that are often atrophied or dysfunctional and can prevent you from hinging easily.

## Common Mistakes
- **Letting the knees travel forward** — the knees must not move from their starting position or go over the toes; the movement comes from the hips going backwards, not the knees going forward.
- **Excessive lumbar arch** — being "too archy" stretches the abs so they're not as responsive. Ribs stay stacked over the pelvis.
- **Bending at the waist with a rounded lower back and tucked pelvis** — the C-shaped posture with lumbar flexion and posterior pelvic tilt. This kills forward explosive power and limits how far the rear leg can extend behind you.
- **Uneven weight in the feet** — weight should be distributed evenly on the full surface of the feet.
- **Not verifying the position** — Alex recommends videotaping yourself, because it's hard to feel whether the spine stayed neutral.
