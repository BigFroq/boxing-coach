# Ready to Strike Postural Series

## Summary
The Ready to Strike series is Alex's video set for waking up and strengthening the small, deep postural muscles — the multifidus and other deep stabilizers — that are atrophied or dysfunctional in most people. Its purpose is balance and postural readiness: the ability to stand loaded and centered so you can fire at any moment, the way Floyd and Bud do. Alex points to it as the prerequisite work behind his posture and "sitting down on your punches" teaching, and as the back-side half of the core pillar.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex introduces the Ready to Strike videos as a remedial series aimed at muscles almost nobody trains directly. In his words, they "help you strengthen up the small deep postural muscles that are often atrophied or dysfunctional in a lot of people, including the multifidus and other small stabilizing muscles." The stated payoff is not raw strength — it's balance and readiness: "They will help you get more balance and be like Floyd and Bud, always ready to strike." The series is his answer to fighters who look stiff, off-center, or unprepared to fire from where they're standing.

He introduces the series directly out of the posterior-pelvic-tilt hinge drill. There, the instruction is to hinge backwards without letting the knees travel over the toes — "the hinge point is right about here" — keeping the ribs stacked over the hips and hinging back until the back pockets touch the wall. He warns against over-arching: "You don't want to be too archy, right? Cuz that's going to stretch your abs. They're not going to be as responsive." The Ready to Strike work is what makes that position hold — the deep stabilizers keep it "nice and tight" rather than the fighter faking it with lumbar extension.

In his athletic-frame breakdown, Alex names Ready to Strike as one of the two videos where he covers the low back stabilizers in more depth. His framing there: "You also need your low back stabilizers to be providing structural integrity to your core from the back side. These muscles need to be balanced with the abs to provide a pulley system that's pulling evenly across your entire core." The target state is a core that behaves like "a pressurized container where all these muscles are engaged evenly so you can transfer force between your hips and your shoulders." Without that back-side integrity, hip drive leaks before it reaches the shoulder.

For entry points into the spinal extensors, he names the bird dog exercise, plus glute work that incorporates a hip hinge — "a lot of the exercises that you see chicks do to get a bigger butt. Generally speaking, these are glute exercises while incorporating a hip hinge. So, Romanian deadlifts, one-legged deadlifts, things like that." This sits inside the third of his three pillars of the athletic frame — the shoulder, the hips, and the core — where the core instruction is to balance the abs and obliques with the deep stabilizers.

The role of this series in the wider system is structural, not stylistic. Alex's punch mechanics depend on hips leading and cross-body chains stretching and releasing through the torso into the arm; a core that isn't evenly pressurized from front and back can't transmit that. Ready to Strike is the postural foundation that lets the loaded position hold and the chain transfer arrive intact.

## Common Mistakes
- Being "too archy" in the hinge — excessive lumbar extension stretches the abs so "they're not going to be as responsive."
- Letting the knees travel over the toes instead of hinging back from the hip.
- Losing rib-over-hip stacking; Alex has you videotape yourself to confirm the position "stays nice and tight."
- Training the abs and obliques while neglecting the low back stabilizers, so the core pulley system pulls unevenly instead of pressurizing.
