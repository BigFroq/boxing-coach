# Squat Depth Stretch

## Summary
The squat depth stretch is a unique, comfortable variation that flips traditional hip stretches upside down to improve squat range of motion while maintaining a neutral spine. By lying on your back with feet against a wall and optionally using an abmat and strap, you can deeply stretch the adductors, glutes, and hamstrings without compromising core integrity, making the flexibility directly transferable to deep, powerful squats. Alex discovered this after struggling with tight adductors that prevented glute activation and full squat depth.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex emphasizes that squatting through a full range of motion is critical for developing explosive power, speed, and injury resilience, especially for boxers who need to launch into punches or evade quickly from any depth. The bottom of a squat puts a stretch on the glutes, recruiting more muscle fibers and training strength through the entire movement—not just a quarter squat. Many athletes, however, have hip flexibility restrictions (particularly tight adductors) that limit depth and cause them to compensate by rounding the lower back or taking a narrow stance. This not only reduces glute activation but also introduces unsafe spinal flexion.

The key insight Alex drives home is that you must stretch your hips in context with a neutral, stable spine. When people stretch their hips by bending forward and grabbing their legs, they round the lower back, losing the lumbar extension required for safe, strong lifting. That defeats the purpose because flexibility gained with a flexed spine won’t carry over to a movement like a squat where you need a braced, neutral core. To solve this, you literally flip the stretch: do it on your back. This keeps the spine neutral (or even in slight extension with an abmat) while the hips are moved through their range. Alex calls this “stretching your hips in context with a neutral core,” which directly boosts squat depth, lift safety, and glute recruitment.

The stretch itself: lie on your back close to a wall, door frame, or barbell, and place your feet flat against the surface. Position yourself close enough and spread your legs to a width that gives you a comfortable stretch in the adductors, and possibly the glutes and hamstrings. Once you find that sweet spot, just relax for at least two minutes. To supercharge it, place an abmat under your lower back to reinforce the natural lumbar curve—mimicking the posture you need at the bottom of a squat. If letting your knees fall outward is uncomfortable, loop a strap around them so they can relax fully without straining. Alex notes that with this setup you can “seriously stay here like forever,” making it easy to do while watching something.

From the same lying position, Alex demonstrates single-leg variations. Extend one leg straight out while the other remains bent to stretch the anterior hip and hip flexors. Turn the bent leg out into a quasi figure-four to target the glute and lateral hip. You can also use a stretch assist device (a PVC pipe with a bungee cord) to pull the straight leg toward you without bending at the waist, again preserving a neutral spine. Holding these for two minutes each allows the muscles to relax and adapt to a new length. Alex credits this approach with fixing his own squat depth, allowing him to squat barefoot without heels coming up and feeling his glutes actually fire and get sore from lifting—before, tight adductors had forced a narrow stance that shut off the glutes.

## Common Mistakes
- Compromising the neutral spine by bending at the waist to grab the feet while stretching hips—this builds flexibility that won’t transfer to a squat or deadlift where you need a braced core.
- Not holding the stretch long enough; Alex insists on at least two minutes to let the muscles relax and adapt.
- Using a narrow squat stance because of tight adductors, which prevents the glutes from being properly stretched and recruited at depth.
