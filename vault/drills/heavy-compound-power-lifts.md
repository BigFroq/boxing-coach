# Heavy Compound Power Lifts

## Summary
A focused gym block of compound lifts (trap-bar deadlift, front squat, push press) performed at heavy load with low reps and full rest, intended to build the underlying strength platform that the bounce step and kinetic-power drills *express*. This is not bodybuilding volume work — it's neuromuscular force-production training. Alex frames strength training for boxers as building the engine; the punch-specific drills then teach the engine to fire in the right pattern.

## What Alex Teaches

Alex separates *strength* (force production capacity in the gym) from *power* (rate of force expression in punches). Heavy compound lifts build strength; plyometric drills (medicine-ball throws, bounce-step entries, kinetic-power training) teach the body to express that strength explosively. Without the strength base, the plyometric drills have less to express; without the plyometric work, the strength stays trapped in the gym and never reaches the fist.

Pattern for a heavy lift session:
- **Movement selection**: pick 1-2 compound lifts per session. Trap-bar deadlift, front squat, and push press are Alex's go-to triad — full-body, posterior-chain heavy, shoulder-friendly.
- **Loading**: 80-90% 1RM. Reps in the 3-5 range. Sets 3-5. Full rest between sets (2-3 minutes minimum).
- **Tempo**: lift the bar with intent on the concentric — fast as possible. Eccentric controlled, not loaded.
- **Frequency**: 2-3 times per week, separated from skill work by at least 4 hours. Heavy lifting tires the central nervous system, so skill work after lifting is technically degraded.
- **Cycle**: alternate heavy weeks (3-5 reps at 85-90%) with moderate weeks (5-8 reps at 75-80%) every 4-6 weeks. Boxers don't need to peak for a one-rep max; the goal is sustained strength that converts to punching power.

The output of this session is muscle stiffness and high-threshold motor unit recruitment, not size. You should leave the gym feeling primed but not cooked. If you can't shadowbox without your hips dragging the next morning, you went too heavy.

## Common Mistakes

- **Bodybuilding volume**: 4 sets of 10-12 reps trains hypertrophy and endurance, not the explosive strength a fighter needs. Keep reps low.
- **Skill work too soon after lifting**: heavy lifts down-regulate fine motor control for several hours. Lift in the morning, train skill in the evening — not back-to-back.
- **Skipping the eccentric control**: dropping the bar fast on the way down spikes joint stress without building anything. Lower deliberately.
- **Going to failure**: every set should leave 1-2 reps in the tank. Failure trains failure.
- **No plyometric companion**: heavy lifting alone makes you slow. Pair with [[Medicine Ball Plyometric Throw]] or [[Kinetic Power Training]] in the same training week, not the same session.
