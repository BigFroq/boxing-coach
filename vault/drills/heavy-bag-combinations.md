# Heavy Bag Combinations

## Summary
Alex teaches heavy bag work as the stage where all the individual punch mechanics get assembled into flowing combinations — "this is where the fun really begins, 'cause it allows you to see what sort of power you're able to generate." The organizing principle is that combinations are not separate punches strung together but one continuous chain of hip rotations, where each punch's follow-through naturally loads the next. Before hitting, he sets up the equipment (water-core bag, 16oz gloves, proper hand wraps) and the weight control exercise that teaches the sensation of true weight transfer.

## What Alex Teaches

**The flow logic.** Combinations emerge from the hip mechanics of each punch. The jab opens the hips; the reset — closing the hips — naturally loads the straight. The straight closes the hips; the reset — opening the hips — naturally loads the hook. Each punch creates the loading position for the next one. Alex's listed combinations follow this alternating cycle: Jab–Straight (1-2), Jab–Jab–Straight (1-1-2), Jab–Straight–Hook (1-2-3), and the full Jab–Straight–Hook–Straight–Jab (1-2-3-2-1), which flows through alternating hip open and close cycles the whole way through. The key principle: do not think of combinations as separate punches strung together — think of them as one continuous chain of hip rotations where each opening and closing naturally produces the next punch in the sequence.

**Punch by punch on the bag.** For the jab, you're rotating your axis with a back-to-front motion. Practice the opening of the hips, incorporate the back-and-front into it, practice it low too, and snap your wrist — grab your hand right at the moment of impact. You can put two jabs together, though "it's hard to get two real powerful jabs one right after another" — use one as a throwaway just to get the guy to move. Always practice both stances, orthodox and southpaw, and practice high and low. The step comes in because you're transferring your weight — you've got to catch yourself so you can come right back. For the straight right, follow the load–explode sequence: get loaded on your mark, explode forward, remember the fundamentals and the moment of impact where you grab your fist and transfer your weight. The beauty of a slightly wider base is that the weight transfer occurs very rapidly and you'll be ready for another shot. Then put them together — the one-two, which Alex notes is "basically the knockout move" in the Prince Naseem Hamed / Augie Sanchez case study.

**The hook and the flat front foot.** The hook follows the same format as the jab — loading and exploding, rotating, opening the hips. You're loading laterally on that front leg, exploding with the glute, that powerful muscle, rotating the hip, taking those cross body chains, and finally shooting that arm out with the accelerating arc. At the moment of impact, grab your fist and follow through. Practice both arms, both sides. Alex is emphatic about the front foot: instead of pivoting on the front foot the way some trainers teach, keep it flat. "In my opinion, you're able to reset faster." When you pivot on the back foot, you're always ready to push back off of that left foot; if you pivot with the left, you've got to turn all the way back around — a wasted movement. That quick reset is how guys like Floyd Mayweather Jr. and Roy Jones Jr. are able to put those three-hook excessive combos together. The Sugar Ray Robinson perfect left hook on Gene Fullmer follows exactly this format — he drops his weight to the side, his arm snaps, "just a little flick of the hips," almost imperceptible, the camera not fast enough to catch it.

**The uppercut.** Same fundamentals as the hook, except instead of across the body, we're going up. The body is still rotating around its axis and you're staying put — you're not trying to push it up. At the same time you're moving and letting that acceleration transfer up into the arm with a little bit of vertical snap. With the rear hand, your strong hand, it's a closing of the hips instead of an opening: you're closing, bringing that right across, keeping your weight low, center of gravity low. That's Zab Judah's favorite go-to punch. Alex acknowledges practicing this on the bag lands a bit low — "it's gonna be a low blow, but you can practice the mechanics anyway," your impact would just be a little bit shorter on the mark.

**Setup and the weight control exercise.** Alex recommends a water-core bag with foam on the outside (Powair, Title) over conventional stuffing — his own is 109–110 pounds. With a conventionally stuffed heavy bag, the stuffing condenses at the bottom over time and you can't punch it with all your power because it hurts your hands. "What does that do to your muscle memory? What does that do to your neurology? If you don't practice it, you are not gonna have it when you need it." He uses 16oz gloves at about 195–220 pounds bodyweight and calls 16oz the sweet spot. He does not agree with shadowboxing with hand weights — it changes the mechanics of the punch, your effort goes into getting the weight moving rather than transferring energy into the punch contact zone, and it's tough on the shoulders. Before hitting, wrap your hands with a knuckle pad rolled back and forth over the knuckles, locked in place, thumb locked, wrist finished — this keeps the palm free and unencumbered so you can grab down on something at impact while still getting protection. Then the weight control exercise: get a good stance, push the bag away with your body weight behind the fist, and absorb it when it comes back. "You should feel just like when you're managing recoil with a firearm" — tension developing in your torso and leg. Get your center of gravity low, power coming through the back foot to keep you from falling back. Do it with both arms, both stances. That loaded, compressed position is the same position you explode from. This fixes the common problem of pushing yourself away from the bag, which comes from a narrow stance and a high center of gravity.

**Breathing and head movement.** It does not matter where you are in the breathing cycle when you punch — you always have enough air available to create intra-abdominal pressure. Hit the bag while inhaling, exhaling, at the top of a full inhale, at the bottom of a full exhale, and while holding your breath, and notice you generate solid power at every stage. This eliminates the anxiety of timing breath to punches. Alex also wants head movement added to bag combinations: with a lowered center of gravity and weight shifting side to side, moving your head puts you in a perfect position to push off with your hips and begin the punch. Be conscious of where your weight is and what options you have from that position — weight on the lead foot gives you a lead uppercut, lead hook, even a jab; weight on the back foot gives you an uppercut, hook to the body, straight cross. Finally, "you put 'em together, you dance around the bag, whatever you want. Have fun with it."

## Common Mistakes
- **Pivoting on the front foot for the hook.** It forces you to turn all the way back around — a wasted movement that kills your ability to chain shots. Keep the front foot flat and pivot on the back foot so you're instantly reset.
- **Pushing yourself away from the bag.** Comes from a narrow stance and a high center of gravity. Get wide, get low, and drill the weight control exercise.
- **Shadowboxing or bag work with hand weights.** Changes the mechanics of the punch — your effort goes into moving the weight instead of accelerating and snapping through the target, and it's hard on the shoulders.
- **Using a conventionally stuffed heavy bag.** The stuffing condenses at the bottom over time so you can't punch full power without discomfort — which means you never build the muscle memory for real power.
- **Timing punches to the breath.** Unnecessary. There is always enough air available to create intra-abdominal pressure at any point in the cycle.
- **Thinking of combinations as separate punches strung together.** They're one continuous chain of hip rotations where each follow-through loads the next punch.
