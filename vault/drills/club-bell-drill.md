# Club Bell Drill

## Summary
The club bell drill is Alex's shoulder-stability and kinetic-chain activation tool — a weighted club (he uses 10, 15, and 20 pound versions) swung from behind the head to overhead and back, or through the windmill and reverse windmill. Alex credits Scott Sonnon with bringing the clubbell to the martial arts and alternative fitness community, and calls it "the next step beyond kettlebells." He uses it primarily as a pre-workout warm-up to tie all the shoulder muscles together before boxing training.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex introduces the club bell as the fourth installment of his shoulder stability series for fighters, punchers, boxers, and martial artists. The basic movement goes from behind the head to overhead and back. What that does, in his words, is stretch and activate the muscles of two chains: the spiral line that goes across the body, and the lateral line that goes into the hip. Both of those chains are used for the straight punch and the hook. When you put the clubbell behind your head, it naturally pulls in all those muscles, and when it pulls, it elicits the stretch reflex, which contracts the muscles when you pull the arm back down. He prescribes 10 reps each side, just to feel those muscles activate, using only a 10 pound weight. He notes it's very similar to a kettlebell snatch.

The windmill — and reverse windmill — is Alex's favorite clubbell exercise. He points out you can't really do that many swings like this with a kettlebell, which is part of what makes the clubbell "a little bit cooler." The swing trains your entire shoulder, all the muscles in the shoulder, and it really ties them all together. He owns a 10, a 15, and a 20 pounder, and says the heavier ones are more geared toward developing strength, while the light one is for warm-up work.

The use case Alex describes is specific: before he works out, especially in his garage where he has a bag, he'll use the clubbell to warm up his shoulders and tie all the muscles together before starting the boxing workout. He doesn't bring it to and from the boxing gym. He says you'll be surprised how much more stability you have when you go to work out after warming up with the clubbell.

Alex is clear that the windmill is an advanced skill you have to build up to. A lot of the stuff you'll do first is swings and similar movements around the head. He admits he bonked himself in the head with it before and it scared him because it's heavy — he actually started wearing a rollerblading helmet, "as stupid as that sounds," reasoning that if you hit your head with a helmet on you're probably going to be okay. Now he says he's got these exercises down and can do them with his eyes closed.

This fits into Alex's broader shoulder stability framework, which exists to prevent "power leak" — movement in the shoulder at impact, like a shock absorber, that reduces your power. Where the rotator cuff exercises isolate and strengthen the small stabilizers, the clubbell does the opposite job: it ties everything together as an integrated chain. He points repeatedly to Scott Sonnon's site RMAX.tv and his book on clubbell training ("I think the book's called Ultimate Guide to Clubbell or something") as the resource to check out.

## Common Mistakes
- Jumping straight to the windmill. Alex: "The windmill is an advanced skill. You are gonna have to build up to it." Do swings and simpler movements around the head first.
- Starting too heavy. He uses a 10 pound clubbell for the activation and warm-up work; the 15 and 20 pounders are "more geared towards developing strength."
- Not respecting the head-strike risk — Alex hit himself with it and started wearing a rollerblading helmet until he had the movement down.
- Going too fast at the start. "So start off slow."
