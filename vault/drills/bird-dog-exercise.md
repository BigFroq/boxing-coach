# Bird Dog Exercise

## Summary
The bird dog exercise is a foundational, daily drill to rebuild the deep multifidus muscles of the lower back, which are often atrophied or dysfunctional in modern fighters and athletes. Alex Wiant prescribes 10 reps per side, performed without heavy load, to restore trunk stability and posterior chain integrity. This simple movement is the first step in a sequence that allows the glutes and hamstrings to work properly, ultimately enabling explosive power and balance.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex teaches that the bird dog is not a random core exercise—it is a targeted intervention for the multifidus, a deep postural muscle that connects each vertebra to its neighbors. In his anatomy breakdown, he explains that the multifidus is frequently dysfunctional or lacks tone in many people, largely due to hours of sitting and accumulated imbalances. Because this muscle is packed with proprioceptive cells (muscle spindles and mechanoreceptors) that feed balance information to the brain, a weak multifidus corrupts your entire sense of position and movement. The bird dog directly activates and strengthens this muscle, bringing back the neural drive and resting tension needed for spinal integrity.

When you perform the bird dog, you are not chasing fatigue or a pump. Alex insists on light, bodyweight-only movement, 10 reps each side, every day. He often pairs it with bridges (15-second holds) as the two non-negotiable trunk rebuilding exercises. He explicitly warns against planks and crunches, claiming they can contribute to multifidus atrophy, while the bird dog does the opposite—it re-teaches the lower back to stabilize without excessive compression. The goal is to feel that deep, segmental muscle work without letting the spine buckle or the pelvis rotate out of alignment.

This drill sits at the very base of Alex’s balance and power pyramid. He stresses that the glutes—the primary engines for hip extension and punching power—cannot fire effectively if the multifidus and spinal erectors are weak or asleep. The bird dog is therefore the prerequisite for all posterior chain work. Only after you’ve restored multifidus tone with daily bird dogs and bridges should you move on to good mornings, deadlifts, kettlebell swings, and explosive sprints. Attempting those compound movements without this foundation, Alex says, will simply reinforce the imbalances.

Alex’s phrasing reflects his belief that this work seems too simple to be effective. He often preempts the student’s skepticism: “it sounds silly like the resistance is not tremendous,” but then reiterates that it is vital. The bird dog, in his system, is not an add-on—it is the first concrete step to being “ready to strike,” with a spine that can sense, stabilize, and transfer force from the ground to the fist.
