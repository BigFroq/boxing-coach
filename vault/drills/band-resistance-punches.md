# Band Resistance Punches

## Summary
The band resistance punch exercise uses a rubber band attached above head height to create downward resistance, reinforcing proper punch mechanics while specifically targeting the strong latissimus dorsi and muscles that connect the shoulder to the torso. You perform a few repetitions with each arm, and it can be thrown as a jab as well. The resistance is designed to pull you down, not push you up, which recruits the back and shoulder stabilizers crucial for powerful, body-driven punches.

## What Alex Teaches
The drill begins by securing a rubber band at a point slightly higher than your head. This height is deliberate—the band should resist you as you draw your punch downward, not as you push upward. You step into the band, letting it tension against your arm, then you simply execute your standard punch mechanics against that resistance. This isolates and overloads the lats and the connecting tissue between the shoulder and torso, exactly the big, strong muscles that link the arm to the back and drive arc-based power.  

There is no fancy variation: you just throw the punch with the band adding load, paying attention to the path and the pull of the shoulder. After a few repetitions on one side, you switch hands. Alex emphasizes that you can do this same motion as a jab, too—the same lat-driven, downward-resisted action works for the lead hand. The key is not to let the band pull you out of your normal movement pattern; the band should challenge the exact muscles that are supposed to be firing during a real punch.  

Because the band pulls from above, you are essentially training the eccentric and concentric phases of the pulling motion that happen when you “pull” the punch into the target using your back and core, rather than pushing with the chest and triceps. This maps directly onto Alex’s overall framework of using cross-body chains and lat engagement to generate force, though he does not break down the kinetic chains in this particular video—he simply points out that it hits “all the muscles that tie your shoulder to your torso.”
