# Weight Shift Drill

## Summary
The weight shift drill is Alex's foundational Phase 1 exercise for developing continuous loading and unloading of the tissues between the lead and rear leg. From a wider-than-shoulder-width stance, you shift your weight back and forth in a relaxed, springy rhythm so that you're always partially loaded and can release into a punch from any point in the rhythm. It's the "always be moving" baseline that every power punch in the Blueprint launches from.

## What Alex Teaches
The drill starts with stance. Alex teaches a **wider than shoulder width stance**, which is contrary to what many gym trainers teach, but the wider base gives you a lower center of gravity — making you more stable and harder to move — and engages the **hip abductors** for powerful weight shifting between legs. It also allows for greater loading potential in Phase 1. Mike Tyson had very flexible hips and maintained a low center of gravity throughout his fights, and Alex identifies this as a key element of his devastating power despite his shorter stature.

There are two Phase 1 exercises that build the weight shift. The first is the **heavy weight visualization**: stand in your fighting stance, imagine a heavy weight pulling your pelvis straight down toward the ground, and **sink about one inch** — not a deep squat, just a subtle drop. You feel the elastic potential energy loading into the muscles of the legs and hips, and the movement should feel like gravity is pulling you into the loaded position. This trains the body to load naturally and efficiently without excessive movement that would telegraph the punch. The second is the **bounce in your step**: from your fighting stance, begin shifting your weight back and forth between your lead and rear leg, focusing on the loading and unloading of tissues — feeling the muscles stretch and compress with each shift. Maintain the bounce as a continuous, relaxed rhythm. The weight shift should feel natural and springy, not forced or mechanical. Alex's stated purpose: this creates a constant state of readiness where you are always partially loaded and can release into a punch from any point in the rhythm.

The drill feeds directly into the punches. On the straight, you're perpetually moving — always moving your weight, always moving your feet — and you shift your weight back and load it into your back foot. Alex calls this **being on your mark**: "just like a sprinter — on your mark, set, go." You're on your mark here and ready to explode with power. He uses this to correct a common gym cue: "power comes from the heel." It doesn't really come from the heel — when you load your tissues back like that, the posterior chain goes through your calf, around your heel, into your foot, into the ground. That's where the power comes from. It comes from shifting your weight back and exploding your hips forward. On the jab, the same rhythm applies: "you should always be moving, always be moving" — and when you transfer your weight back during one of those times when you have your rhythm, you kick off with the back foot to rotate the posterior hip backward, opening the hips and stretching the cross-body chains (the spiral line and front functional line).

The weight shift also governs what happens *after* the punch. At the moment of impact your weight has shifted forward onto the front foot — and since that weight is forward and that front foot is loaded, you can just bring it right back. That's the reset: "you're flicking your hips. Once you're forward and you follow through, you just flick them right back." Alex says you can do that all day, just this exercise right here.

Alex extends the weight shift into positioning and head movement. With a somewhat lowered center of gravity and shifting your weight from side to side, when you move your head you're in a perfect position to push off with your hips, to rotate your hips, to begin the punch. Because you're moving your weight around and loading your tissues — loading and unloading the entire time you're moving — you're always in a position to throw. He wants you conscious of where your weight is and what options you have from that position: if your weight is on your lead foot, you've got a lead uppercut, a lead hook, even a jab, or you can rotate your body and get off the line. If your weight's on your back foot, you have an uppercut, hook to the body, straight punch, cross — a lot of options. As you're manipulating your weight, you can always be in position to throw because you're just a little hip movement away.

## Common Mistakes
- Using a shoulder-width or narrower stance because that's what gym trainers teach — you lose the lower center of gravity, the hip abductor engagement, and the loading potential of Phase 1.
- Dropping into a deep squat instead of sinking about one inch — the load is subtle, and excessive movement telegraphs the punch.
- Making the bounce forced or mechanical rather than natural and springy.
- Standing still between punches instead of perpetually moving — if you're not shifting your weight, you're not partially loaded and have nothing to release from.
- Thinking the power comes from the heel rather than from shifting the weight back into the loaded posterior chain and exploding the hips forward.
