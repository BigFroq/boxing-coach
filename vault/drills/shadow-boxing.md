# Shadow Boxing

## Summary
Shadow boxing is where you build and own your mechanics before impact ever enters the picture. Alex teaches three distinct objectives — practicing and *feeling* the mechanics, observing your form in the mirror, and visualizing an opponent — and says most people only ever do the second one. It's also where you prove you can throw a full-power shot on balance without a bag or an opponent to catch you.

## What Alex Teaches
There are three things you can do in shadow boxing, and Alex thinks most people only do one. "You'll see people looking in the mirror and really admiring themselves and their mechanics." That mirror work is objective number two — observing yourself and engaging your form based on what you see. Objective number one is really *feeling* and practicing the mechanics taught in the Power Punching Blueprint, and Alex says you should always spend at least that much time, maybe more, on the mechanics themselves. Objective number three is visualizing fighting an opponent, which he says a lot of people don't really do. Visualization is a very important part of success in any sport — he points to the old free-throw study where the group with the best results practiced *and* did specific visualization of shooting baskets successfully. Apply that to your shadow boxing. Every little bit counts, and it'll go a long way in helping you relate with another opponent whether you're competing or practicing.

The working format Alex uses is walking in a line. "Just walk out of line, practicing the mechanics." Practice each punch individually, and once you've practiced all of them, put them together, incorporate some head movement, practice what your trainer teaches you, and practice both orthodox and southpaw. Once you've warmed up and shadow boxed adequately, you move on to the bag. He notes you don't *have* to do it in a line — that's just how he does it, because "it helps me put my mind on it and just kind of zone me in."

Shadow boxing is where the shoulder blade work gets drilled. During the acceleration phase your shoulder blade might not be all the way pinned back — it might be floating a little, in the process of moving — but right at impact there's an emphatic movement of the shoulder blade to the fully protracted position. That's the last little link, the key to landing and maximizing stability at impact: with the shoulder blade all the way forward, the serratus anterior can contract all the way, along with the obliques, the spiral line, and the front functional line, because the pec can be fully engaged in that position. When Alex walks the line he's feeling his tissues drop down and feeling the shoulder blade fully retract and "really bounce off of my spine" — down and back, which stretches the cross-body chains and initiates the movement forward. Then he practices the shoulder blade going all the way forward, contracting those muscles as if making impact. Do it in every punch — the jab, the straight, the hook (where the shoulder bounces back harder than in the jab). The only punch that doesn't really do it is the uppercut: if you're dropping your tissues, that shoulder comes down rather than back, but you still get that beat, that loading of the tissues to explode from with the hip rotation. Dedicate one of your shadow boxing rounds to just that focus.

Shadow boxing is also the only place you can prove your balance honestly. Alex says you want to be able to throw a powerful shot in your shadow boxing without relying on making impact with something to hold your balance. His analogy: baseball players who swing really hard and lose their balance versus a guy like Josh Hamilton with a very compact, on-balance swing who doesn't lose his balance afterward. As a boxer you should learn how to throw a full-power shot without relying on something to support you — the bag or your opponent. So get used to throwing big punches in your shadow boxing just to make sure you have appropriate balance and can handle that type of movement without falling over, so you're ready to throw another shot afterward. The hip hinge and the counter-rotation drill (turning your pelvis back forward, belt buckle pointing forward, in the last half of the movement) are what let you do that.

Beyond mechanics, Alex uses shadow boxing for rhythm work: shadow box to a metronome and then break the rhythm the metronome gives you, which gives you a feel for what it feels like to break rhythm. This ties into the broader goal of making your movements automatic. You need to move with no mind, no conscious thought — and it still has to be effective. If you're worried about where your elbow is, or focused on gripping the floor rather than acting without thought based on what your opponent is doing, you're not going to make it. You want all your movement second nature so you can respond to your opponent with no mind, no intellectual thought. Small percentages matter here: "small percentages across a large amount of techniques adds up to a large percentage overall," which is why you should be practicing them perfectly in training.

## Common Mistakes
- Only doing the mirror objective — admiring your own mechanics instead of also feeling the mechanics and visualizing an opponent.
- Skipping visualization entirely. Alex says a lot of people don't really do this, despite it being a very important part of success in any sport.
- Relying on the bag or an opponent to catch your balance on a big shot, instead of proving you can throw full power on balance in the air.
- Practicing sloppily or half-attentively — small percentages across many techniques add up, so they should be practiced perfectly in training.
- Staying conscious of your own mechanics too long — worrying about where your elbow is rather than making the movement second nature so you can respond with no mind.
