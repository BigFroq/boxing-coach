# Bounce Step Drill

## Summary
The bounce step is Alex's foundational Phase 1 (Load) drill for teaching you to launch punches off the ground rather than muscle them out. You step back, drop your weight about an inch to load the rear leg, then bounce off the ball of that foot to propel your athletic center forward into the punch. Alex describes it as "what you might do if you're walking and then want to launch into a sprint" — the same reflexive mechanism you use skipping rope or stepping up a curb.

## What Alex Teaches
The bounce step lives inside the Load phase of Alex's four-phase framework — load, explode, accelerate, follow through. Load phase means rapidly lowering your athletic center and loading your muscles. It's very fast, only a dip of around **one inch**, and it's mediated by a fast **relaxation** of the legs to slightly increase knee and hip bend — not a muscular squat. You keep the same neutral core, spine and pelvis from the hinge; the dip comes strictly from the legs. Alex's imagery: imagine your pelvis is connected to a long chain and a heavy weight underground, and that weight is pulling your pelvis down at "freefall" speed using gravity. It primarily uses the posterior chain and lateral line, plus quads for the jab.

Alex distinguishes **natural loading** from **dynamic loading**. Natural loading is created automatically by your movement — whenever your weight shifts onto a leg, the muscles and connective tissues briefly develop elastic energy as they accept your body weight. Launch immediately from that weight shift ("touch and go") and you're using natural loading. Dynamic loading is an additional, intentional increase: if you've gone stationary on a leg and the initial elastic energy has faded, add a quick dip to recreate it, then immediately rebound into the punch. The big takeaway is that **a smooth weight transfer will usually create enough elastic loading to launch a punch on its own** — the dip is what you add when you want extra speed and power on demand.

There are two on-your-feet exercises Alex gives for the bounce step in the video. First: stand with your feet about shoulder width apart, take a step back, drop your weight down, and push off — and you want to push off **the ball of your foot**. You should feel like it's propelling you forward enough that your weight naturally wants to go forward. Do it on both sides. That's how you launch off your back foot to throw a lead right hand "just like Ali did, just like Floyd did, Roy Jones Jr." You end up in a position just like you would be if you were going to launch into a sprint. Second: the **mid-gate bounce step**. You're walking, and as your weight starts to transfer forward to where "more of your weight is forward than back" — somewhat more forward of your natural center — you drop your weight very quickly, bounce off that back foot, and power right into a punch. Alex compares it directly to stepping up a stair or stepping up a curb.

In PPB 2.0, Alex breaks this into four progressive versions under "A Bounce in Your Step." There are no fixed reps — the more you do it, the more natural it feels. **Version 1**: in stance, drop about an inch and bounce up. No jump — drop, catch, rebound to your original stance. **Version 2**: from an even weight distribution, shift weight back slightly, hold a second, then dip on the back leg with a quick relaxation and bounce your weight forward onto the front leg. Return to even and repeat on the front leg. **Version 3**: bounce weight continuously between legs with no return to neutral, relaxing arms and shoulders so they drop slightly while the legs load — the leg dip lets the arm and shoulder muscles stretch simultaneously, preparing you for simultaneous cross-body chain and leg loading later. Feel the lateral leg muscles load and unload; the lateral line is what moves you forward in stance. **Version 4 — The Bounce Step**: feet on the same line about shoulder width, step back, dip as the rear leg contacts the ground to load it, then bounce off the rear leg to launch your athletic center forward to the starting position. Both legs.

Alex uses Floyd Mayweather as the model. On the pull counter, Floyd lowers his weight, loads his tissues, and drops his center of gravity — "look how he's on his mark like a sprinter." Then the hips go from back to front before his torso and arm move at all, then torso, then arm. On the bounce step specifically: "You're going to see Floyd start like walking forward and then quickly relax, drop his tissues, push off of that back leg, and launch the punch." Alex also shows Floyd baiting with a pull counter — pull, weight comes back, drop down, and launch right into a punch from southpaw. Same idea. Once the bounce step is in place, it feeds into the later phases: the High Five exercise starts with the bounce (weight shifting back and forth), adds hip rotation, then throws the hand forward as if giving someone a high five — the hand should feel like it's being launched by the body, not pushed by the shoulder. In Phase 2, you add the dip-and-bounce to the opening and closing of the hips, focusing on **leading with your athletic center** — the spot about two inches below your belly button in the center of your pelvis.

## Common Mistakes
- **Scrunching or curling your toes.** "You want to push off the ball of your foot. And you can't do this if you're trying to scrunch up your toes." Alex checks this against Floyd on the pull counter: "Do you think his toes are curled there? Absolutely not. Just like a sprinter in the sprinting blocks, they are likely splayed out to assist him in launching his whole body forward."
- **Dipping too deep.** The load is only about a one-inch dip. Bigger isn't better — it telegraphs. "You don't want to telegraph, of course, and this is not that you're loading up to get a huge swing. It's an instantaneous shift of the weight."
- **Bending at the spine or pelvis instead of the legs.** Keep the same neutral core, spine and pelvis from the hinge — the dip comes strictly from the legs, bending only at hip and knee.
- **Muscling the dip instead of relaxing into it.** The drop is mediated by a very fast *relaxation* of the legs, and should feel like the imaginary underground weight is pulling you down at freefall speed.
- **Jumping instead of bouncing.** In Version 1, there's no jump — you drop, catch, and rebound to your original stance.
- **Bracing and linearly muscling the punch.** Alex names this as the alternative you've "probably seen on YouTube" — trying to muscle the punch linearly instead of using the elastic loading of your muscles, which doesn't utilize the benefits you already intrinsically have.
