# Medicine Ball Throw Drill

## Summary
The medicine ball throw drill is a plyometric exercise that trains your kinetic chains to explode with power just like a punch. Instead of catching the ball and pausing, you catch it on the fly and immediately use the ball’s momentum pushing you back to load your tissues and throw it back explosively. Alex teaches it with alternating roles, both stance sides, and an emphasis on rhythm and full-body integration.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex distinguishes between the common “wrong way” and the correct plyometric execution. Most people in the gym catch the medicine ball, pause, reset, and then throw it back. This static approach, he says, “is okay, but you’re gonna get real limited results.” The proper method treats the drill as a dynamic catch-and-throw cycle: one person throws, the receiver catches on the fly and immediately returns it. There is no pause; the ball’s incoming momentum pushes you backward onto your rear foot, loading your muscles and connective tissues. You then explode forward to throw the ball back, using that stretch-shortening cycle just like when you drop into the load phase of a punch and then drive off.

Alex emphasizes that this drill “trains all those kinetic chains” and “shows you really how to use all the chains to explode.” He wants you to feel the chain reaction from hips to torso to arm, much like a baseball pitch or a tennis serve. After each exchange, you and your partner switch roles—one throws several times while the other does the plyometric catch-and-return, then you swap. You also change stances: practice in your orthodox stance, then switch to southpaw (or your weaker stance) to develop both sides equally. Alex encourages going even faster once you get the rhythm, and he says to “take the momentum of the ball and how it’s pushing you back and use that to load your tissues to throw it back to your partner.” The goal is to feel the elastic loading and the immediate explosive release, mirroring the kinetic sequence of a powerful punch.

## Common Mistakes
- Catching the ball, holding it, and pausing before throwing (static, non-plyometric rhythm).
- Standing too upright and not allowing the catch to push you back onto the rear foot to pre-load the tissues.
- Failing to switch roles with your partner so both people get plyometric reps.
- Only practicing from your dominant stance, neglecting the weaker side.
- Throwing the ball back without using the body’s kinetic chains—arm-only pushing instead of full-body explosion.
