# Heavy Weight Visualization

## Summary
Heavy Weight Visualization is Alex's Phase 1 loading exercise: you imagine your pelvis is connected to a long chain and a heavy weight buried underground — like a bowling ball six feet down — pulling your pelvis straight toward the ground. You sink about one inch, relaxing your tissues and lowering your center of gravity, which loads the elastic properties of the leg and hip muscles so they can explode into hip rotation. The drill trains the body to load naturally through gravity rather than through a big telegraphing movement.

## What Alex Teaches
The exercise exists to solve a specific problem in Phase 1, the load phase. Alex is explicit that loading is not a windup: "you don't want a telegraph, of course, and this is not, that you're not loading up to get a huge swing. It's an instantaneous shift of the weight." Loading happens one of two ways — disguised inside your movement as you shift your weight around, or through "a tiny use of gravity" to drop the pelvis. Heavy Weight Visualization is the tool for finding that second path.

The mechanism is the elastic property of muscle. "You're loading the elastic properties of the muscle by stretching it a bit. If you stretch your muscle slightly, you're gonna be able to utilize it and contract it more violently." Alex's analogy is the jump: nobody jumps from a stationary position — "they dip down a little bit to get that elastic property of muscle functioning, to stretch it a bit so they can explode from it." That dip is what you're reproducing in your stance. He calls it "sort of a plyometric exercise."

The visualization itself is precise. Imagine your pelvis connected to a long chain with a heavy weight underground — Alex specifies a bowling ball roughly six feet down — and let that weight pull your pelvis down. You want to be in a **neutral position and slightly lowered**, sinking about **one inch**, not a deep squat. Relax all your tissues as you drop. Think about your center of gravity being all the way underground. Feel the elastic potential energy loading into the muscles of the legs and hips. The movement should feel like gravity is pulling you into the loaded position, not like you're squatting into it.

The drill has two stages of practice. First, "just walk around and be conscious of that" — carry the image of the underground center of gravity with you as you move. Then get into your stance and practice lowering the pelvis somewhat and shifting your weight around in that position with a low center of gravity. That is the position you punch from: "when you're in the stance and you do that, that's the position that you're punching from." You can hold it all the time if you're fighting in close; at mid to longer range, it's the position you shift your weight or use gravity to catch you into, in order to explode with your power shots. From there you "load your weight in a different direction from which you want to explode."

The exercise depends on the stance that precedes it. Alex teaches a **wider than shoulder width stance**, contrary to what gyms told him — "they always wanted me to make my stance narrower." A wider base lowers your center of gravity, gives better balance, engages the hip abductors for powerful weight shifting, allows more freedom to move laterally, and creates greater loading potential in Phase 1. He points to **Mike Tyson**, who had very flexible hips and kept a low center of gravity throughout his fights — a key element of his devastating power despite his shorter stature. Heavy Weight Visualization is paired with the **Bounce in Your Step** exercise, which trains the same loading and unloading of tissues through continuous weight shifting, so you're always partially loaded and can release into a punch from any point in the rhythm. The underlying principle traces to **Peter Ralston's "Principles of Effortless Power"** — power comes from proper body alignment and gravity, not from muscular force alone.

## Common Mistakes
- **Loading up into a big windup.** "You're not loading up to get a huge swing" — the load is instantaneous, or disguised through movement, so you don't telegraph.
- **Dropping too far.** Sink about one inch. It's a subtle drop, not a deep squat; excessive movement telegraphs the punch.
- **Staying tense while dropping.** Alex tells you to relax all your tissues as the weight pulls the pelvis down — the drop should feel like gravity catching you, not a muscular effort.
- **Using a narrow, shoulder-width stance.** Gym coaches told Alex to narrow his stance; a narrow base puts your center of gravity "way higher" and kills the loading potential this exercise depends on.
