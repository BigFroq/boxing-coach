# Weight Control Bag Exercise

## Summary
The Weight Control Bag Exercise is a foundational drill that teaches you how to transfer your body weight into a heavy bag and manage the resulting recoil without being pushed backward. By pushing the bag, catching it as it swings back, and redirecting its momentum, you learn to load your tissues, drop your center of gravity, and feel the full-body stability needed to land powerful punches with authority and balance.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex introduces the Weight Control Bag Exercise as a way to internalize the sensation of supporting the weight you put into your target. He recommends using a heavy bag — ideally a water-filled bag weighing over 100 pounds — because traditional stuffed bags become dense at the bottom over time and punish your hands when you punch at full power. Wearing 16-ounce gloves, you stand in a wide, athletic stance and push your fist into the bag with your entire body weight, leaning forward so the bag supports you. This creates a direct feel for having your mass behind the fist, not just your arm.

Once you push the bag away, you let it swing back and catch it with the same hand, absorbing its weight as it compresses your tissues. Alex compares this sensation to managing recoil with a firearm: you should feel tension build through your torso, legs, and especially your rear foot, which must drive into the floor to prevent you from falling backward. Practicing this with both hands and alternating stances ingrains the lower center of gravity and the “loaded” feeling you need for real punching power. Even if you only adopt a slightly more aggressive forward posture (by just an inch or so), the exercise trains your nervous system to handle force and prevents the common error of pushing yourself away from the bag when you punch.

Alex emphasizes that this is not meant to replicate the exact position of a punch at impact, but it gives you a clear physical reference for what it means to transfer weight and remain stable. He suggests doing the exercise briefly before or after every bag session — or even as a standalone drill — to “bounce that bag, juggle that bag” and build the muscle memory for loading and exploding from the loaded foot.

## Common Mistakes
- **Narrow stance and high center of gravity** — causes the puncher to push themselves away from the bag instead of absorbing and redirecting force.
- **Using a densely stuffed bag** — conventional heavy bags compact over time, hurting the hands and preventing full-power practice, which robs you of realistic neurological training.
- **Shadow boxing with hand weights** — this alters punch mechanics, forcing you to decelerate the weight rather than snapping through the target, and can strain the shoulders.
