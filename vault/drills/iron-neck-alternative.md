# Iron Neck Alternative Drill

## Summary
Alex teaches two DIY alternatives to the Iron Neck: one using a weight plate with padding for yes/no movements, and another using resistance bands anchored to an upright to create rotational resistance. Both are cheap, effective ways to train the neck without the expensive device, but he stresses starting light and avoiding overdoing it, especially with the head strap rotation.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex first discovered the Iron Neck as a cool device that uses bands to resist head rotation from multiple angles, but he wasn’t ready to spend the money. So he experimented with a simple band setup: attach a resistance band to a sturdy upright at head height, loop it around your head, and then rotate your head against the pull. You can position yourself to target the front, back, and sides—covering virtually every angle. This DIY band version runs maybe ten percent of the cost of the real Iron Neck, and because bands don’t tend to make you sore, you can do it almost daily to build neck resilience and conditioning.

His second alternative skips bands entirely and uses a weight plate and some padding. He recommends pipe insulation from the hardware store or just a towel wrapped around the edge of the plate. Lie face-up on a bench, place the padded plate on your forehead, and perform “yeses” (chin to chest) and “nos” (side-to-side rotation). Alex finds this plate method even more effective than the headband approach for these flexion and lateral movements. Start with 10 pounds—even that is plenty—and just 10 to 20 reps to get the feel. You really don’t need much weight to train the neck.

For the back of the neck, Alex keeps a head strap in the mix. Attach a light weight (he uses 10 pounds) to the strap, get into the usual posterior neck training position, and then turn your head side to side. This rotation under load hits the posterior chain in a way that’s easy to overdo—he warns that the first time he tried it, he came away with a surprisingly sore neck, more discomfort than expected. So with any of these, the golden rule is: start light, feel it out, and don’t push through pain.

These alternatives look a little silly, Alex admits, but the effectiveness is immediate. He emphasizes that neck training for boxing and martial arts doesn’t require expensive gear or heavy loads; it’s about consistent, controlled resistance that integrates with your overall kinetic chain awareness.

## Common Mistakes
- Starting with too heavy a plate or too much band resistance instead of beginning with 10 lb or light tension.
- Skipping the padding (towel or pipe insulation) on the weight plate, leading to discomfort.
- Overdoing reps or intensity on the first session, particularly with the head-strap rotation, which can leave the neck excessively sore.
- Not securing the band anchor properly for the DIY Iron Neck, risking sudden snaps or uneven loading.
