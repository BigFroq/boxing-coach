# Shadowboxing Both Stances

## Summary
Shadowboxing both stances is a foundational drill Alex uses to rewire the nervous system and strengthen your dominant side by training the mirroring mechanics of your off-hand. Even if you never fight southpaw, practicing all punches in an opposite stance builds neural pathways that improve balance, coordination, and power on your natural side. This work should be integrated into every shadowboxing session—right alongside the three core objectives of feeling mechanics, observing form, and visualizing an opponent.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex treats training the opposite stance as a non‑negotiable part of a complete shadowboxing practice. He draws an analogy from other sports: a right‑handed baseball pitcher doesn’t plan to throw left‑handed in a game, but training the left side still sharpens the neuromuscular patterns that make the right‑arm pitch better. The same principle applies to boxing. When you practice your jab, straight, hook, and uppercut in a southpaw stance as an orthodox fighter, you create fresh neural pathways that, in turn, strengthen the movements of your dominant arm. This carries over not only to cleaner mechanics but also to better balance and the ability to throw powerful shots without depending on a bag or opponent to catch you.

In the “Putting It All Together” shadowboxing walkthrough, Alex explicitly tells students to “walk outta line” while drilling the mechanics of every punch, and to do it on both sides. The process is simple: after you’ve broken down each punch’s phases—load, explode, accelerate, follow through—you flow them together moving forward in a line, first orthodox, then southpaw. He wants you to feel the weight transfer, the shoulder‑blade protraction at impact, and the recoil management, all while switching stances so your body learns to stabilize and generate force from either base.

This cross‑stance drilling dovetails with his three objectives for shadowboxing. Most people only use the mirror to admire their form, but Alex outlines three distinct tasks: 1) practicing and feeling mechanics, 2) observing yourself in the mirror to verify what you see matches what you feel, and 3) visualizing a live opponent. He says you should spend at least a round—maybe more—on mechanics alone, and that’s exactly when you cycle through both stances. You can also bring the same dual‑stance focus into the visualization round, imagining how you’d set up and land counters from either orientation.

Beyond mere repetition, Alex teaches specific balancing drills to embed both sides. For instance, he wants you to throw full‑power shots in the air without anything to support you—just like a compact baseball swing that stays on balance—and you must do it in both orthodox and southpaw so you aren’t relying on impact to hold you steady. Another quick drill is the instant gravity drop: by fully relaxing the leg muscles you drop an inch or two into a low center of gravity, and you practice this rapid dip in both stances to prepare for explosive launches from any position. All of this work means that when you eventually hit the bag, your nervous system has already built the foundation to switch stances seamlessly and punch with integrity.

## Key Quotes
> “Even if you're Orthodox, you want to practice at Southpaw … practicing at Southpaw will help you throw it in an Orthodox stance that much better. … getting those mechanics down on the other side of the body is just gonna help create new neural pathways in the brain and it'll help strengthen the movement of your dominant arm.”
> — Power Punching Blueprint – The Jab

> “I want you to walk outta line, practicing, practicing the mechanics. And once you practiced all of 'em, put 'em together, incorporate some head movement, practice what your trainer teaches you, and of course, practice orthodox and southpaw.”
> — Power Punching Blueprint – Putting It All Together (Shadowboxing)

> “You want to be able to practice throwing a powerful shot in your shadow boxing without relying on making impact with something to hold your balance. … get used to throwing big punches with your shadow boxing just to make sure you have appropriate balance and can handle that type of movement without falling over.”
> — How To Throw Power Shots While Staying On Balance

> “Practice doing that both in, both in southpaw and in orthodox stance … just by relaxing, fully relaxing your tissues.”
> — Power Punching Blueprint – Finding Low Center of Gravity Fast

## Common Mistakes
- Shadowboxing only in their orthodox stance, believing practicing the opposite side is irrelevant.
- Using the mirror to check aesthetics but skipping the dedicated mechanics round that ingrains both stances.
- Forgetting that training the off‑stance directly strengthens the dominant side through new neural pathways—not because they plan to fight southpaw.
- Attempting to throw full‑power shots in shadowboxing without first building balance in both stances, causing them to stumble or over‑rotate.
