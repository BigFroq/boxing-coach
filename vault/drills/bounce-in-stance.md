# Bounce in Stance

## Summary
The bounce in stance is Alex's foundational Phase 1 (Load) drill — a continuous, rhythmic shifting of weight between the legs that keeps you in a constant state of loading and unloading. Rather than walking around the ring, you think about moving your weight *between* your legs, turning movement into a plyometric event powered by gravity and ground reaction force. This is how elite fighters get their bouncy, mobile rhythm, and it's what lets you release into a punch from any point in the rhythm.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex frames the bounce around two types of loading used throughout the KIMs system. **Natural loading** is created automatically by your movement — whenever your weight shifts onto a leg, the muscles and connective tissues briefly develop elastic energy as they accept your body weight. If you launch immediately from that weight shift ("touch and go"), you're using natural loading. **Dynamic loading** is an additional, intentional increase in elastic loading: if you've become stationary on a leg and the initial elastic energy has faded, you add a **quick dip** — briefly relax the leg to allow a rapid bend at the hip and knee, then immediately rebound into the punch. Add dynamic loading any time you want extra speed and power. His big takeaway: "A smooth weight transfer will usually create enough elastic loading to launch a punch on its own."

The dip itself is very specific. It's a drop of **around one inch** — not a squat — mediated by a very fast **relaxation** of the legs to slightly increase knee and hip bend. You keep the same neutral core, spine and pelvis from the hinge; **the dip comes strictly from the legs**. It should feel like an imaginary underground weight attached to your athletic center pulls you down at "freefall" speed using gravity. It's the same quick drop before sprinting forward, or the dip before a jump. It primarily uses the posterior chain and lateral line, plus the quads for the jab. This is also why Alex clarifies "sitting down on your punches": the big problem is lowering your body by posteriorly tilting your pelvis and flexing/rounding the lower back, which wrecks spinal neutrality and "bends" your axis of rotation, inhibiting your ability to generate and transfer force. Lower yourself with a knee and hip bend while keeping neutrality.

The **bouncing ball analogy** underpins the whole drill: a ball is elastic, and you can load your legs and hips with elastic muscle energy through loading and unloading to "bounce" yourself between your legs. Moving your weight between your legs becomes a **plyometric movement** — this is how all the most elite fighters manage their movement in stance. Stance width is what makes this possible. Too narrow and your center of mass sits too close to the support leg, so gravity won't pull you down fast enough to assist movement. Too wide and your lateral hip muscles are in a shortened position and can't move you laterally as well. Properly balanced, gravitational pull plus the lateral hip muscles move you quickly and efficiently where you want to go. This lives inside the **triple threat stance** — borrowed from basketball's triple threat position (ready to shoot, drive, or pass) — a position that easily allows you to defend, attack, or move gracefully off the line to disengage. An athletic ready position shouldn't feel frozen or rigid; it should feel **alive and ready**, like a basketball defender, a hitter waiting on a pitch, or a tennis player receiving serve — relaxed but ready to explode.

Alex teaches the exercise "A Bounce in Your Step" in **four versions**, with no fixed reps — the more you do it the more natural it feels. **Version 1:** in stance, drop about an inch and bounce up. No jump — drop, catch, rebound to your original stance. **Version 2:** from 50:50, shift weight back slightly to ~40:60, hold a second, then dip on the back leg with a quick relaxation and bounce your weight forward to ~60:40 front. Return to 50:50 and repeat on the front leg. **Version 3:** bounce weight continuously between legs with no return to neutral — relax arms and shoulders so they drop slightly while loading the legs, because the leg dip lets the arm and shoulder muscles stretch simultaneously, preparing you for simultaneous cross-body chain and leg loading later. The rhythm is 50:50 → quick rear-leg dip to 40:60 → bounce off, move athletic center forward, load lead leg 60:40 → bounce back. Feel the lateral leg muscles load and unload; the lateral line is what moves you forward in stance. **Version 4 — The Bounce Step:** feet on the same line about shoulder width, step back, dip as the rear leg contacts the ground to load it, then bounce off the rear leg to launch your athletic center forward to the starting position. Both legs.

On video Alex adds the practical feel and the analogies. You push off the **ball of your foot** — and "you can't do this if you're trying to scrunch up your toes." He compares it directly to stepping up a stair or a curb, and to skipping rope off the balls of your feet: "It's the same type of reflexive mechanism. Now, are you scrunching your toes while you jump rope? I certainly hope not." He also teaches the **mid gate bounce step**: as you walk and your weight starts to transfer forward — once you've reached the point where more of your weight is forward than back, somewhat more forward of your natural center — you drop your weight very quickly and bounce right off that back foot, powering straight into a punch. After the bounce step you should be in a position "just like you would be if you were going to launch into a sprint." The case studies are Floyd Mayweather — pull countering, baiting, dropping down and launching into a punch from southpaw; loading his tissues, dropping his center of gravity, pushing off from back to front with a rotational component in the hips that stretches the cross body chains for a snappier jab — plus Ali and Roy Jones Jr. launching the lead right hand off the back foot.

The bounce also feeds directly into Phase 2 (Explode), which is the rebound after the dip plus hip rotation. Alex has you dip on the back foot, bounce off, then close the hips all the way; then from closed, dip and bounce off the lead leg and complete the opening movement — combined into one seamless fluid movement. Focus on **leading with your athletic center**, the spot about two inches below your belly button in the center of your pelvis. He's explicit that sometimes you won't dip at all before a punch (e.g. linking the 1-2 or 2-3), but the dip is there when you want extra speed and power, want to wait a beat, or slip defensively before following up.

## Common Mistakes
- **Scrunching your toes.** You push off the ball of the foot — you can't bounce if you're gripping with your toes, the same way you can't skip rope that way.
- **Dipping too deep.** It's around one inch, mediated by a fast relaxation of the legs, not a squat. Excessive movement also telegraphs the punch.
- **Losing spinal neutrality.** Interpreting "sitting down on your punches" as posteriorly tilting the pelvis and rounding the lower back bends your axis of rotation and inhibits force generation and transfer. Lower with a knee and hip bend instead.
- **Bending anywhere but the hip and knee.** Keep spine, core and pelvis neutral — the dip comes strictly from the legs.
- **A stance too narrow or too wide.** Too narrow and gravity can't pull you off the support leg fast enough; too wide and the lateral hip muscles are shortened and can't move you laterally.
- **Standing frozen or rigid.** The ready position should feel alive and ready — a basketball defender, a hitter waiting on a pitch, a tennis player receiving serve — not stationary, where the elastic energy has already faded.
