# Kettlebell Swings, Cleans, Snatches

## Summary
Alex prescribes the kettlebell swing, clean, and snatch as full-body kinetic chain exercises for punching power — not isolation lifts. The swing is his top pick for grooving the hip hinge ("probably the highest ROI exercise for the hinge"), while the snatch works "all these muscles in front, which you've used in the punch." All three train the body to work together, which is the whole premise of his system.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex introduces the kettlebell swing, clean, and snatch as a linked progression inside the Power Punching Blueprint. His instruction is deliberately simple: find a kettlebell that's appropriate for you, and if you're familiar with swings, it's just like that. From the swing he builds to the clean, then to the snatch, and he tells you to switch and do both arms. The reason these earn a place in a punching program is that "this works all of, you know, many, many kinetic chains, and that's what we want. We want to train your body to work together." The snatch in particular he singles out because it "works all these muscles in front, which you've used in the punch."

The swing is where the hip hinge lives. Alex calls kettlebell swings "probably the highest ROI exercise for the hinge" — you're forced to keep a straight, sturdy back, and you should find a weight that takes some explosive hip movement to move. The hinge itself is bending at the hips while keeping a straight or neutral spine, where "straight doesn't mean flat — it means the spine is neutral with its curves intact." When he programs the swing for posterior chain work, the cues are: keep tension in your lower back, really focus on hinging at the hips, use a light weight to start — it doesn't have to be a lot, and you can increase weight very quickly once you have the movement down with the hip hinge.

Alex frames the swing as one of his examples of putting rhythm and momentum into your lifting — what he calls strategic cheating. "You're gonna get a nice stretch in your posterior chain and you're going to be able to really explode with hip drive to power the entire swing. So, you get a weight and you want to use the hip hinge, right? Keep your core nice and neutral. Let the weight come down, stretch your posterior chain, and then explode back up." This is the stretch shortening cycle trained in context with other muscles in the chain, not in isolation. It fits his larger argument that power is "not all at once like an isolated lift. It's not even almost all at once like a squat or a deadlift. It's a sequence, a ripple, a cascade of acceleration." Traditional strength programs "train your body to be a statue when it needs to be a slingshot."

He also connects the kettlebell snatch directly to his shoulder stability work. Teaching the clubbell overhead-and-back movement for kinetic chain activation, he notes it "stretches and activates all these muscles — we've got two chains here, got a spiral line that goes across the body and we've got the lateral line which goes into the hip here, and both those chains are used both for your straight punch and your hook." He then says outright: "it's very similar to a kettlebell snatch." The mechanism is the same — putting the weight behind your head naturally pulls in all those muscles and elicits the stretch reflex, which contracts them.

Alex places the kettlebell swing in his compound posterior chain block alongside good mornings, deadlifts, squats, split squats, and single-leg deadlifts — but only after the foundational work is in. He is explicit that these compound weight-bearing exercises need a strong, sound lower back first, meaning a working multifidus and the other postural support muscles, and that everything is done with a neutral spine, even muscular tension, and a starting weight that's light enough not to compromise form. He also notes a kettlebell can be used for hip flexor work — hook your foot under it and flex your hip up.

## Common Mistakes
- Not hinging at the hips — Alex repeatedly cues "really try to focus on hinging at the hips" and keeping tension in the lower back rather than turning the swing into something else
- Starting too heavy — "use a light weight for this, it doesn't have to be a lot; you can increase in weight very quickly after you get the movement down with the hip hinge"
- Losing the neutral spine — the swing forces "a straight sturdy back"; core stays neutral, spine keeps its curves
- Jumping to compound kettlebell work before the foundation is there — Alex insists on a strong lower back and working multifidus before moving on to the more complex weight-bearing compound exercises
- Training the movement as an isolated lift instead of a sequence — "if your lifts are robotic and segmented, you're training your body to disconnect force instead of transfer it"
