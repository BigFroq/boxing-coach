# Bag Bounce / Juggle Drill

## Summary
The Bag Bounce / Juggle Drill (also called the Weight Control Exercise) teaches you to transfer your full body weight into the heavy bag and then absorb its return without getting pushed off balance. By pushing, catching, and redirecting the bag’s swing, you learn to load your tissues, lower your center of gravity, and maintain a stable, aggressive stance—sensing what true weight transfer feels like rather than just arm punching.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex introduces this drill as a foundational exercise for handling a heavy bag—especially a 100+ pound water bag—without recoiling or pushing yourself away. The problem he identifies is that many fighters use a narrow stance and high center of gravity, causing them to bounce off the bag instead of driving through it. The fix is to get wide, drop your weight, and practice bouncing or juggling the bag.

The drill itself goes like this: stand in your regular fighting stance, then push your fist into the bag with your whole body—lean in, let the bag support you, and feel your weight behind the punch. As the bag swings back toward you, catch it and absorb its energy, letting it compress all your tissues. You’ll feel tension build through your torso and legs, much like managing the recoil of a firearm. Then push it right back and repeat. Do it with both arms, then switch your stance and practice both sides. This is not about punching with velocity; it’s a slow, deliberate exercise to feel the loading sensation and understand how your back foot and low center of gravity anchor you.

Alex explains that the compressed position you feel when catching the bag closely mimics the loading phase of a punch. Letting the bag push all your tissues back is exactly where you want to be loaded from when you explode forward. It also teaches the “push and plant” concept—you can push off the back leg to drive the punch while keeping that leg planted for stability at impact. By practicing this, you train your nervous system and muscle memory to support the weight you’re putting into a target, eliminating the habit of being pushed backward.

The drill is meant to be done frequently—before or after workouts, or even as a standalone exercise. It doesn’t require throwing full-speed punches; instead, you focus on the transfer of weight, the absorption, and the redirect. Once you’ve wired that feeling, you can then integrate the same loading sensation into your actual punches, staying on balance and delivering power without recoil.

## Common Mistakes
- Standing too narrow with a high center of gravity, causing you to be pushed away from the bag instead of absorbing its return.
- Trying to punch with full velocity during the drill instead of slowly absorbing and redirecting—this is a loading exercise, not a speed drill.
- Not getting low enough to engage the back foot and posterior chain; you must drop your center of gravity to stabilize a swinging heavy bag.
- Only practicing with one hand or ignoring the opposite stance; you need to drill both sides to build balanced weight transfer.
