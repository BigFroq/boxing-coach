# Strategic Cheating in Lifting

## Summary
Strategic cheating is a training method where you intentionally loosen strict lifting form with moderate, controllable weights to incorporate momentum, rhythm, and full-body integration. It trains the stretch shortening cycle and neuromuscular sequencing—the same cascading force transfer used in a punch—so you move like a natural puncher rather than a rigid, isolated bodybuilder.

## What Alex Teaches
Traditional weightlifting advice—slow tempo, perfect form, isolating muscles, training to failure—may build muscle, but it fights against the speed and power needed in the ring. Combat athletes don’t move like bodybuilders; they use momentum, rhythm, and full-body integration. Strict, robotic lifts train your muscles to fire all at once and disconnect force rather than transfer it. That turns you into a statue when you need to be a slingshot. The alternative is to deliberately add a “cheat” component to select lifts, using weight you can already handle with strict form (around 60% of your one-rep max), so you turn a strength exercise into a timing, rhythm, and coordination drill.

Strategic cheating does two things for a fighter. First, it trains the stretch shortening cycle not just in the target muscle but in context with other muscles in the chain and the rest of the body. A quick stretch loads elastic energy; the release produces a rapid, powerful contraction—just like dipping down before a jump. Second, it builds neuromuscular timing by teaching you to sequence force so one part of your body loads while the next part is already transferring energy. That’s the cascade of a punch, a throw, a swing. When you watch a natural puncher on Alex’s breakdowns, you see a smooth, flowing chain of events from legs through the body and into the target. Strategic cheating gives you a similar feel in the gym.

Practical applications include movements like the standing bicep curl or the pull-up. For the bicep curl, you can add a slight body sway or hip rhythm to involve the legs and core, turning an isolation lift into a full-body chain reaction. For pull-ups, once you can do at least five to ten strict reps, you can add a kip—swinging the legs forward then back to create a whip through the body that elevates you by transferring momentum from the lower body through the core into the arms. These exercises are not replacements for foundational strength; they are complementary drills that let you feel how to connect chains of muscle and accelerate your body the way a natural puncher does.

This is a practiced technique, not a resort when you’re tired or using weight you can’t handle. Sloppy lifting with heavy loads is dangerous. Alex stresses that if you’re a beginner, you must first build a solid foundation of control and strict form. Once you can lift well, strategic cheating is meant to peel away the robotic patterns that get trained into you, reminding your body how it naturally wants to move with speed, rhythm, and coordination. The same principle can be applied to any standing or hanging exercise where your whole body can contribute; seated movements won’t work.

## Common Mistakes
- Using weights that are too heavy to control—strategic cheating is for loads you can already lift strictly, not max-effort grinds.
- Confusing deliberate, rhythmic cheating with sloppy, uncontrolled form.
- Skipping the foundation: beginners should first develop strict strength and brain-body connection before attempting this.
- Treating it as a replacement for strict lifting; it is a complementary practice to build timing and integration.
- Attempting to apply it to seated exercises where the whole body cannot participate in the kinetic chain.
