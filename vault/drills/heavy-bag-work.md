# Heavy Bag Work

## Summary
Heavy bag work is where Alex has you take the mechanics you've drilled in shadowboxing and put them together to see what power you can actually generate. He's specific about equipment — a water core with foam on the outside, over 100 pounds, and 16 ounce bag gloves — because a bag you can't hit with full power teaches your neurology the wrong thing. Before punching, he has you do the weight control exercise to learn what supporting your own weight at impact feels like.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex is opinionated about the bag itself. He uses a water core bag with foam on the outside, weighed at 109-110 pounds before he hung it. His reasoning is mechanical: conventional stuffed heavy bags condense over time — "the stuffing gets really dense down at the bottom over time and you can't punch it with all your punching power 'cause it hurts your hands." And if you can't punch it with all your punching power, "what does that do to your muscle memory? What does that do to your neurology? If you don't practice it, you are not gonna have it when you need it." A water bag is resilient, gives good feedback, and lets you have a heavy bag that isn't painful to punch. In the course material he names Powair and Title as recommended brands. For gloves, 16 ounces is the sweet spot — he weighs about 195 and finds 16oz perfect; lighter fighters might go a little smaller, but he recommends no more than 16 for bag work. He also rejects shadowboxing with hand weights: it changes the mechanics, "now you're not able to really accelerate through your target because you have a weight in your hand," your effort goes into moving the weight rather than into the punch contact zone, and it's tough on the shoulders.

Before you throw a punch, Alex has you do the **weight control exercise**. The problem it solves is the one you see in the gym, in commercials, on TV — someone punches the bag and pushes themselves away. That comes from a narrow stance and a high center of gravity. So: get in your regular stance, get wide, push the bag away, then catch and absorb it as it swings back. "If you stabilize that hundred pound bag coming at you, you're gonna have to get your center of gravity low. You're gonna have to get power coming through your back foot to keep you from falling back." He compares the feeling directly to managing recoil with a firearm — tension developing in your torso, tension developing in your leg. When you catch that bag, "all those tissues are being compressed and you should be able to feel them tense up." It's not exactly the position you'll be in when you land, but it teaches you what it feels like to support the weight you're putting into your target. Practice both hands, then switch stances. Do it every time you practice — before your workout, after, or on its own.

Hand wrapping comes before the bag. Alex's method: across the wrist, around the wrist one more time, around the thumb, bring it up across the hand. Then build a knuckle pad by rolling the wrap back and forth over the knuckles — a few loops, one two three four five, and there's six. Lock it in place by bringing it right across the hand between the knuckles, lock the thumb one more time, then finish wrapping the wrist. "What this does, it keeps your palm free and it allows you to grab down on something so you can really get a nice — you get a lot of protection and you're also unencumbered, your palm's unencumbered." Same procedure for both hands.

On the bag, the punches carry over exactly as drilled. The jab rotates your axis with a back-to-front motion and opens the hips — practice that opening, incorporate the back-and-front, practice it low, and snap the wrist, grabbing the hand right at the moment of impact. You can double the jab, using one as a throwaway to get the guy to move. The straight is the closing of the hips — remember the fundamentals and at the moment of impact, grab your fist and transfer your weight. The hook loads and explodes with the hips opening; Alex teaches pivoting on the *back* foot rather than the front, because keeping the lead foot flat lets you reset faster — "if you're pivoting with your left, you gotta turn all the way back around, it's a wasted movement." That quick reset is how Floyd Mayweather Jr. and Roy Jones Jr. string those three-hook combos together. The uppercut is the same fundamentals as the hook but vertical — the body still rotating around its axis, staying put, not trying to push it up, letting acceleration transfer up into the arm with a little vertical snap. The rear uppercut is a closing of the hips, weight low, center of gravity low — Zab Judah's favorite go-to punch.

Combinations flow from the hip mechanics: each punch's follow-through naturally loads the next. Jab opens the hips, the reset closes them into the straight; the straight closes, the reset opens into the hook. Don't think of combinations as separate punches strung together — think of them as one continuous chain of hip rotations. On breathing, Alex is clear that it does not matter where you are in the breath cycle when you punch: you always have enough air to create intra-abdominal pressure. He has you prove it by hitting the bag while inhaling, while exhaling, at the top of a full inhale, at the bottom, and while holding — you'll generate solid power at every stage, which removes the anxiety of timing breath to punches.

Two more things Alex adds on the bag. First, a slightly aggressive posture — even by an inch — dramatically improves your ability to manage the force you're putting into the bag without it pushing you backwards. Push versus plant isn't either/or: "push your hips into rotation and allow that back leg to really stay planted to offer you stability and to give you a good weight transfer at impact." He notes that when he moves forward at 220 pounds against a 100 pound bag, he knows the weight and power he's generated exceed the resistance the bag will offer — "and that just comes with feel." Second, from the Fedor breakdown: after you make impact and the bag has moved, hold it out there for a split second before you reset. "That'll really train your body to manage your weight at impact." He also wants you adding head movement on the bag — because with a lowered center of gravity and weight shifting side to side, every head movement leaves you loaded and in position to push off with your hips and start the next punch.

## Common Mistakes
- **Pushing yourself away from the bag.** "A lot of times I'll see kids punching a bag, or you'll see someone in a commercial or TV show punching a bag, and they kind of push themselves away from it. Well, that comes from a narrow stance, a high center of gravity." Fix: get wide, get your center of gravity down, and do the weight control exercise.
- **Using a dense, condensed-stuffing bag.** It hurts your hands, so you never practice at full power — and what you don't practice you won't have when you need it.
- **Shadowboxing with hand weights.** Changes your punch mechanics, prevents acceleration through the target, puts effort into moving the weight instead of the contact zone, and is tough on the shoulders.
- **Fighting from a permanent rear bias on the bag.** Limits weight transfer, causes poor recoil management and bouncing off the target, and reduces the leverage you need to get the most out of the acceleration phase.
- **Pivoting on the lead foot for the hook.** Keeping the front foot flat lets you reset faster; pivoting the lead foot means turning all the way back around — a wasted movement.
- **Timing your punches to your breath.** Unnecessary. There's always enough air available to create intra-abdominal pressure at any point in the cycle.
