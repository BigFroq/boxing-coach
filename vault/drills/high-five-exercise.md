# High Five Exercise

## Summary
The High Five is Alex's Phase 3 acceleration drill: you throw a high five in the air to an imaginary person and continue past imaginary impact until the arm is straight out in front, feeling how the hips and torso — not the shoulder — launch the hand along an arc. Progressively lowered from overhead to face height, the motion becomes very similar to the trajectory of a straight punch. The point is to feel the arm stay powered through the entire arc "just like a throw," never pushed forward by the shoulder.

## What Alex Teaches
The High Five sits in Phase 3: Acceleration, and it is the drill where the first three phases get stacked together. Phase 1 supplies the bounce — the weight shifting back and forth, the quick dip and bounce off the rear leg. Phase 2 supplies hip rotation, which for straight punches means closing the hips (rear hip driving forward) and for the jab, hook and lead uppercut means opening them. Phase 3 adds the arm extension. As the hips rotate, you throw the hand forward as if giving someone a high five, and the arm extends naturally from the hip and core rotation — you do not muscle the arm forward. The hand should feel like it is being launched by the body, not pushed by the shoulder.

The mechanism underneath is the spine as the axis of rotation and the cross-body chains (functional lines) being stretched during hip rotation and then snapping back with a powerful contraction. Alex frames this as not a conscious contraction: it should feel like throwing a ball or swinging a tennis racquet, where the stretch and rebound feel easy and natural. In the combined version, you start in stance with arms up, take a quick dip and bounce off the rear leg without closing the hips fully yet, and as you dip you relax the arm and shoulder so they dip with the body — that momentary dip stretches the upper pec fibers. Then the full Phase 2 hip rotation launches the high five. He also has you load the lead arm with the rear leg and throw a lead-hand high five as you bounce forward, without worrying about the jab's hip-rotation details yet.

The arc is the whole point, and Alex is emphatic that the hand stays powered through all of it: "a throw doesn't stop halfway through the movement and neither should your high five." Exaggerate the arc if you need to feel it. The arc trajectory matters because it utilizes the strong latissimus dorsi more than a straight-line push would — the lats are one of the most powerful muscles in the body, and they connect to the pelvis and interface with the external oblique, while the long head of the tricep connects to the lats. Pushing straight forward primarily uses the smaller pectoral and deltoid muscles. As Alex puts it in the exercises intro: "The way I teach is to punch in little arcs. It uses these strong muscles for power. You simply can't generate the kind of power you want with straight line punches."

The progression matters. You start by throwing the high five in the air overhead, then progressively lower the starting point until the hand starts at face height, doing both hands in stance — and at that height it is very similar to the trajectory of your straight punches. The High Five follows Spaghetti Arms (turning the body with totally relaxed arms, initiating with the hips, feeling the kinetic tether) and Shot Shaping (the same turning motion with structure added — thumbs pointed up, feeling the stretch in the pec, feeling like you could slap something with your palm as your hand crosses the center line). Alex is explicit that these drills are for *feeling* how acceleration works, not for perfect technique.

There is no independent shoulder movement anywhere in this chain. As Alex says of shot shaping and the same principle carries into the high five: "a lot of people they'll try to like do funny things with the shoulder, but you got to feel like this whole chain is connected... The shoulder helps facilitate movement that is initiated from the ground by your legs and your hips which then cascades through the body." He also teaches a secret tip for the phase: load the cross-body chains simultaneously with the weight shift in Phase 1 rather than treating loading and chain stretching as separate sequential events — this eliminates wasted time between phases and creates a more explosive release. And the arms and hands stay loose until the moment of impact, because loose muscles can contract more forcefully than muscles already partially tensed; at impact you squeeze the fist suddenly.

## Common Mistakes
- **Stopping the hand at imaginary impact.** The arm must continue past impact until it is straight out in front — a throw doesn't stop halfway through.
- **Muscling the arm forward.** The arm should extend naturally from the hip and core rotation; the body launches the hand.
- **Independent shoulder movement.** Doing "funny things with the shoulder" breaks the chain that is initiated from the ground by the legs and hips.
- **Straight-line trajectory instead of an arc.** A straight-line push uses the smaller pec and deltoid; the arc recruits the lats. Exaggerate the arc if needed to feel it.
- **Closing the hips fully during the dip.** In the combined version, the quick dip and bounce off the rear leg comes first — don't close the hips yet.
- **Tension in the arm before impact.** Keep arms and hands loose until the moment of impact; tension slows the punch down.
