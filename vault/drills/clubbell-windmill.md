# Clubbell Windmill

## Summary
The clubbell windmill is Alex's favorite shoulder exercise — a swing that takes the club from the front, around and behind the head, and back, training every muscle in the shoulder and tying them all together. He uses it as a warm-up before boxing workouts, especially in his garage, because of how much shoulder stability it gives you afterward. It's an advanced skill you must build up to, and Alex is candid that he's bonked himself in the head with it hard enough that he started wearing a rollerblading helmet.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex credits the clubbell's popularity to coach Scott Sonnon, who introduced it to the martial arts and alternative fitness community several years ago. He frames it as "the next step beyond kettlebells" — kettlebells are great, he trains with them and loves them, but "you can't really do that many swings like this with a kettlebell." He points people to Sonnon's site RMAX.tv and his book on clubbell training. Alex trains with a 10-pound clubbell for the movement he demonstrates, and also owns a 15 and a 20, noting that "the heavier ones are, you know, a little bit more geared towards developing strength."

The windmill — and its counterpart the reverse windmill — is his favorite thing to do with the club. The value is integrative rather than isolating: "this swing, as you can imagine, trains your entire shoulder, all the muscles in your shoulder, and it really ties 'em all together." He uses it as a pre-workout warm-up, particularly when training at home where he has a bag in the garage, though he doesn't carry a clubbell to the boxing gym. The payoff he promises is stability: "you'll be surprised how much more stability you have when you go to work out, uh, after warming up with the clubbell."

In the shoulder stability series, Alex breaks down the mechanism as kinetic chain activation. The movement he demonstrates goes "from this position right here to overhead and back," and what that does is stretch and activate the chains that run through the shoulder. He names two specifically: "we've got a spiral line that goes across the body and we've got the lateral line which goes into the hip here and both those chains are used both for your straight punch and your hook." When the clubbell goes behind the head, it "naturally pulls in all these muscles," and that pull "elicits that stretch reflex which will contract the muscles when you pull the arm back down." His prescription there is simple: 10 reps each side, both sides, with a 10-pound weight — "just to feel those muscles activate is what we want to do."

He explicitly relates it to kettlebell work: "it's very similar to a kettlebell snatch." That sits alongside his other kinetic-chain tools — the kettlebell swing, clean and snatch, which work "many, many kinetic chains" and train "your body to work together," with the snatch in particular working "all these muscles in front, which you've used in the punch."

Alex is direct about the risk and the progression. "This is an advanced skill. The windmill is an advanced skill. You are gonna have to build up to it." Before you get there, you do "a lot of stuff you'll do first" — basic swings, movements around the head. He admits he hit himself in the head with the club and it scared him because the weight is real, which is why he started wearing a rollerblading helmet: "as stupid as that sounds and you hit your head with a helmet on, you're probably gonna be okay." Now he says he's got the exercises down well enough to do them with his eyes closed. The instruction that follows is: start off slow.

## Common Mistakes
- Jumping straight to the windmill instead of building up to it — it's an advanced skill, and you should do basic swings and around-the-head movements first.
- Going too fast too soon. Alex bonked himself in the head with the club and started wearing a rollerblading helmet; his instruction is to start off slow.
- Starting with a heavy club. Alex demonstrates with a 10-pounder and reserves the 15 and 20 for strength development.
- Overdoing the reps in a warm-up context — 10 reps each side is enough to feel the muscles activate.
