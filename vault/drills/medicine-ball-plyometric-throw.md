# Medicine Ball Plyometric Throw

## Summary
The medicine ball plyometric throw is a drill for developing explosive punching power by training the body’s kinetic chains to use a stretch-shortening cycle. Alex teaches you to catch the ball on the fly and immediately explode back to your partner, using the incoming momentum to load your tissues exactly like the load phase of a punch. This turns a common gym exercise into a true plyometric that ingrains how to sequence the hips, torso, and arm.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex distinguishes sharply between the wrong way and the right way to perform the medicine ball throw. The wrong way—which he sees often in boxing gyms—is catching the ball, pausing while holding it, and then turning to throw it. He says this is “okay” but yields limited results because you aren’t tapping into the stretch-shortening cycle. The right way is to treat it as a plyometric exercise: catch the ball on the fly and immediately throw it back to your partner. The ball’s momentum pushes you back, which loads the tissues of your legs and torso, and you then explode forward using that loaded stretch just like you do when dropping into the load phase of a punch and exploding into the delivery.

The drill trains all the kinetic chains in a plyometric fashion. Alex emphasizes that it shows you how to use all the chains to explode, mirroring the mechanics of a straight punch or cross. He demonstrates with a partner: one person throws the ball a few times while the other catches and returns it instantly, then they switch roles. You can also vary your stance—for example, working from a southpaw or weaker stance—to build balanced power. The goal is to make the catch-and-throw sequence as fast as possible: “Do it when it comes down and immediately explode.” Alex likens the feeling to “throw a fireball, the street fight,” capturing the raw, whole-body acceleration.

The same plyometric principle appears in Alex’s barbell punch exercise, where you let the weight push you back onto your rear foot and then explode forward. He also references the medicine ball throw in his “cheating” video as an example of using momentum to initiate the stretch-shortening cycle, reinforcing that you let the ball push you to your back leg and use that stretch to launch the ball back. The medicine ball smash (overhead slam to the ground) is a separate core and back exercise, not a plyometric throw drill.

## Common Mistakes
- Catching the ball, pausing, and then throwing—treating it as a strength movement instead of a plyometric
- Not using the ball’s momentum to load the tissues; failing to connect the catch directly into the explosive throw
- Throwing with only the arms instead of letting the hips lead and the whole kinetic chain fire
- Working only from one stance; neglecting to train the weaker (e.g., southpaw) side
