# Rotator Cuff Exercises

## Summary
Rotator cuff exercises build shoulder stability to prevent "power leak" — that shock-absorber effect where energy is lost into a loose joint at impact. Alex teaches a layered approach: first warm the cuff to wake up the brain-body connection, then on separate days strengthen it with specific holds and movements, always balancing internal and external rotators to avoid the gorilla‑posture imbalance.

## What Alex Teaches
The shoulder is a loose, mobile joint, so unless the rotator cuff muscles actively center the humeral head in the socket, impact energy bleeds backward instead of driving into the target. Alex frames this as a “power leak,” a term he repeats often. The cuff comprises the subscapularis (internal rotator), infraspinatus and teres minor (external rotators), and supraspinatus (abductor). Most people have tight internal rotators and weak, stretched external rotators — visible as thumbs pointing inward at rest. Correcting this demands first loosening the tight muscles (especially the subscapularis) with long‑hold stretches, then strengthening the cuff in a precise order: two sets of external‑rotation work for every one set of internal rotation, finishing with abduction exercises for the supraspinatus.

The warm‑up is strictly for creating a mind‑muscle connection — not for building strength. Alex uses a PVC pipe held at 90‑90 (elbow and shoulder at 90°) and does external rotations while either pulling the bar apart or pushing it together; this recruits the scapular muscles to better isolate the external rotators. Then he switches to a light band for the subscapularis, doing internal rotation while pushing in and pulling out, again at low volume (10‑15 reps). The key rule: never fatigue the rotator cuff before a boxing workout, because a tired cuff can’t stabilize against the pounding of bag work.

Strengthening happens on a separate day — “like on a Sunday or an off‑day.” The top exercises are external rotation on a bench (elbow pinned, 90°, using a 5‑lb plate or dumbbell, 1‑2 sets of 10‑15) and a side‑lying row or bench exercise for the supraspinatus: arm at 100° of abduction, thumb up (Alex explicitly corrected an earlier video where he used thumbs down; thumbs up maximally activates the supraspinatus). He also shows band or dumbbell variations in the “30‑degree plane” — about 30° forward of true horizontal — to work the supraspinatus in its anatomically neutral position. Alex frequently recommends the “shoulder horn” tool to stabilize the arm so the cuff muscles can be isolated without the body having to stabilize itself.

Between sets of external‑rotation work, Alex inserts a stretch for the internal rotators (subscapularis) because the external rotators are naturally inhibited while internal rotators are tight; stretching the tight side super‑charges the strengthening effect. He also shows wall stretches for the internal and external rotators, using a doorway or band to hold 60‑second stretches. The overall sequence: external rotation (2×), internal rotation (1×), then supraspinatus (1×), all with light resistance, no sets to failure, and always both sides.

## Common Mistakes
- Using a thumbs‑down position for supraspinatus work (corrected by Alex; thumbs‑up is the proper orientation).
- Strengthening the rotator cuff immediately before or after a heavy boxing workout, which fatigues the cuff and increases injury risk.
- Skipping the internal‑rotator stretch between external‑rotation sets, missing a chance to improve muscle balance.
- Starting with too much weight instead of a light plate (5 lbs) and allowing the elbow to drift away from the side.
- Only training the external rotators or neglecting the internal rotators and supraspinatus, which fails to create full‑spectrum stability.
