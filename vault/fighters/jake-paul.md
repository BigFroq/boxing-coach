# Jake Paul

## Summary
Alex uses Jake Paul as a case study in partial mechanics — a fighter who can generate real torque and use his kinetic chains effectively, but only when he has room to take a big step. Up close, Jake reverts to linear-style mechanics: pushing and muscling punches, swinging his arm without connection to his hips or upper body. He is the illustration of why creating torque *subtly and on demand* matters more than being able to create it at all.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex's central point about Jake Paul is that his good mechanics are conditional. In the Tyron Woodley knockout, Jake creates "a very small amount of torque," but he needs a huge big step to do it — stepping, pushing off of his back foot. On that punch he does it correctly: his upper body lags behind his hip by a frame or so, his arm follows behind the torso, which loads up those muscles with elastic muscle energy — "just like you might dip down very slightly to stretch your glutes in your legs in order to jump." His hips finish rotating while his hand and arm keep moving, so he creates acceleration, and his whole upper body ends up behind his fist providing support for the impact and the follow through. In another clip Alex points at the pec specifically: the pec stays largely still while the hips move, then the upper body follows — successful stretch between hips and upper body that he can contract on, landing with a stable platform.

But Alex is explicit that this isn't world-class torque: "he's not creating worldclass torque like Artur Beterbiev like Alex Pereira." It's enough to get the body moving. The failure is the on-demand part — "he's not able to do it up close, he's not able to do it subtly on demand." On one right hand, Jake's hip and arm move in lock step, so no torque at all; instead he uses jumping mechanics of the kind Alex attributes to Dmitry Bivol, springing up so there may be momentum but the punch lands at the same moment the hip reaches its forward point. He also lands without good integrity in his cross body chains, or contraction in them to stabilize the punch, so there's no real weight transfer through the target. On that same punch his elbow is very high at landing, which inherently creates a weaker position for the shoulder because he loses tension in his lats and teres major.

The hooks are where Alex is harshest. One left hook is "more like a swinging swinging punch with his entire arm extended" — it lands, but there's no torque or acceleration, "just a swing of his arm." On another, the arm isn't connected to the rest of the body at all: "is that strictly an arm punch." Alex notes Jake doesn't have the room to take a huge step to power the punch when he's closer, and that this pattern persists — "I've seen this actually recently in his training him throwing a hook like this so we know he's still doing it." On a pivoting hook, Jake's body moves in lock step with his hips and his arm ends up all the way behind his body in a very weak position, fully extended, not connected, not creating acceleration.

Alex also flags a right hand to the body against Nate Diaz where Jake lands with the palm and is "just pushing it with his arm — he's not using torque or acceleration to power the punch." His read on the Nate fight is the tactical thesis: Nate forced Jake to fight close to him, which is where the mechanics break down. Those pushed shots "can accumulate — I mean, it's not that comfortable but it's also not really going to bother someone like Mike." Where Alex does credit Jake, he's precise about the anatomy: on one halfway decent torque example against Nate, Jake creates the pelvis rotation but powers the punch with his deltoid rather than the arm being truly connected to the pec and then into the cross body chains that go into the hips — effective against Nate, but not the mechanism Alex teaches.

Against Anthony Joshua, Alex's prescription follows directly from the diagnosis: Jake would have to stay out of Joshua's range, "really use his body as much as he can to stretch with it, to turn his body and reach to close that distance from out of range to in range and then fire intentional shots behind his setups." He allows the possibility of landing something big, but adds the general rule: "in situations where someone might have a puncher's chance, virtually every time the better boxer wins."

## Common Mistakes
- **Needing a huge step to create torque.** Jake can only generate rotation with a big committed step, so the moment an opponent closes distance he has no mechanism left.
- **Hip and arm moving in lock step.** When the hip finishes at the same instant the punch lands, no torque has been created — there's no stretch in the cross body chains to contract on.
- **Swinging the arm from full extension.** Hooks thrown as a whole-arm swing with the arm behind the body, disconnected from hips and torso, land in a weak position with no acceleration.
- **Pushing and muscling punches up close.** Linear-style mechanics — pushing the punch with the arm instead of powering it with torque and acceleration.
- **Landing with a very high elbow.** This inherently creates a weaker shoulder position because tension is lost in the lats and teres major.
- **Powering the punch with the deltoid instead of the pec.** Even when the pelvis creates torque, using the anterior deltoid rather than a true connection from arm to pec to cross body chains to hips leaves power on the table.
