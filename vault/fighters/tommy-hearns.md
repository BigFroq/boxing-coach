# Tommy Hearns

## Summary
Alex uses Tommy "The Hitman" Hearns, the Motor City Cobra, as a case study in how physical gifts combine with sound kinetic chain mechanics to produce knockout power. Hearns had two inherent advantages — good joint integrity and long levers — and he layered elite stretch-shortening cycle usage on top of them, powering even his jab with hip rotation. Alex also uses Hearns as the foil in the Leonard fight, showing that range and power can be beaten by superior meta skills.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex opens the Hearns breakdown with the physical advantages Tommy brought to the table. First is joint integrity: "his joints are tight and stable, not loose or floppy." Tight joints without a lot of slop or play allow the muscles surrounding the joint to have more optimal and more even resting muscle tension. Good muscle balance makes muscles more sensitive to length changes and stretch, so they can contract faster, contract more explosively, and have better stability to manage force transfer at impact. Second is long levers — long limbs give Tommy a mechanical advantage that acts as a force multiplier for movement and power.

On top of those gifts sit the three concepts of kinetic integrated mechanics that Alex teaches. The stretch shortening cycle is a quick stretch prior to a muscle contraction that increases the power of that contraction — the same thing you see in a golf swing or when you dip down before you jump. That quick stretch primes the muscle for a more violent contraction. Power punchers use this first in their legs to rotate their hips, then in their torso and arm, utilizing the crossbody kinetic chains. This creates a cascade of stretch shortening cycles that starts in the hips and allows the body and arm to accelerate to maximize velocity of the fist at impact, directly connecting the hips and arm. Kinetic chains are not one chain but multiple chains of muscle coursing through the body — the same chains used to throw a baseball or swing a golf club — and leveraging them is what makes the difference between *throwing* a punch and *pushing* a punch. Then there are the four phases of the punch: load, explode, accelerate, follow through.

Alex's most emphasized point from the Hearns footage is hip rotation on the jab. He calls out that "I see on YouTube a lot of guys demonstrating it without including hip rotation" — and insists that if you want to flick the jab out there, there's no better way than with a hip flick. In the Cuevas footage you see Tommy lowered down, center of gravity a bit low, ready to explode; he pushes off the back leg and pops the hip into rotation while the arm lags a bit, then the arm comes out. The rotation stretches the crossbody chains including the pec, so Tommy can contract them more emphatically and launch the jab out even faster. Alex points out that on some jabs Tommy straightens out the leg to get rotation, and because he has long arms he doesn't really have to go from back to front to close the gap — strong rotation alone flicks the jab out. Bud Crawford does this same thing, and Alex points to his Crawford video for a parallel example.

For the big right hand that dropped Roberto Durán, Alex walks the four phases frame by frame. It's a short compact punch that crosses Roberto's jab arm. The relaxed arm comes back creating stretch between the arm and the torso while the hip moves forward — "it doesn't have to be much, just a frame," because Tommy's athletic frame and very good resting muscle tension let him use just a little bit of stretch. Then the arm picks up the contraction and everything launches forward. Alex highlights the lateral flexion of the torso along with the rotation to power that cross — traditional cross mechanics — plus good follow through in the arc and landing with shearing force. Critically, Tommy lands in a good aggressive posture with the abs engaged to manage the recoil: even when landing with that kind of power, you have to allow your weight to go into and through your opponent, so you need a stable platform. Alex is honest about the imperfect moments too — Tommy gets a little excited and throws some arm punches with "not a lot of intention on those," and one jab is powered mostly by the deltoid with only a little rotation.

In the Leonard fight, Alex flips the lens: Tommy is the beast with the range, and Leonard beats him with meta skills. Boxing is like high-speed chess, and Alex shows Leonard feint, bait Tommy's counter, then counter the counter — Ray pulls back slightly out of Tommy's range, loading his back leg, then springs off that loaded leg to follow Tommy's arm back and land a jab with perpetual motion, getting out of the way of anything Tommy can respond with. Leonard is always moving in and out of Tommy's range and staying right on the edge. Alex frames this as gauging distance, identifying the limitations of your opponent's range and movement, then exploiting that information — Leonard lands against a guy with much more range than he has. Alex also notes that in the community rankings for the technically perfect boxer, Tommy Hearns sits at the number one spot for best cross.

## Common Mistakes
- Demonstrating the jab without hip rotation — Alex specifically calls out YouTube demonstrations that leave out the hip flick, which is the thing that stretches the crossbody chains and gives the jab its snap.
- Pushing the punch instead of throwing it — the missing link in a lot of weaker punchers is poor utilization of the crossbody chains.
- Throwing arm punches with no intention behind them — Alex flags this even in Hearns' own footage when he gets excited.
- Landing without a stable platform — even with big power you have to let your weight go into and through your opponent, with aggressive body posture and abs engaged to manage the recoil.
