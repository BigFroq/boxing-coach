# Cyril Gane

## Summary
Alex uses Ciryl Gane as the canonical example of a heavyweight athlete whose first two punch phases are fine but who throws away his power in the last two. Gane leads with his shoulder and drives the arm with his deltoid instead of maintaining tension through the cross body kinetic chains from pelvis to fist, so his punches die on contact instead of accelerating through the target. Alex's fix: lead with the hips, keep the palm moving toward the body, don't turn the arm over, and let the stretched chains contract to carry the fist into and through the opponent.

## What Alex Teaches
The framework Alex applies to Gane is the four punch phases — **load, explode, accelerate, follow through** — and the cross body kinetic chains that connect the hips to the fist through the shoulder and arm. As the hip turns first in the explode phase, those chains get stretched; when a muscle is stretched rapidly it can contract faster and more vigorously, starting a kinetic cascade of accelerating movement "just like when you throw a ball." Alex is explicit that Gane's **load and explode are totally fine** — the hip rotation independent of torso rotation is good, the pelvic rotation is good, the aggressive lean is good. The failure is everything after that.

What Gane does instead is lead with the shoulder, turn the arm over, and swing the arm "like dead weight," using primarily the deltoid to move it — "moving his arm up like a deltoid powered side raise," what Alex calls "basically doing a Mr. Armstrong from In Living Color." This creates a stretch shortening cycle in the deltoid rather than in the big cross body chains, and Alex's core rule here is blunt: **"there is no shoulder in punching."** The shoulder doesn't generate power in a throw, it transfers power. It might generate power in a linear movement like a military press, but not in a throw — and a punch is a throw. You throw a punch, you don't muscle or push a punch. Because Gane leads with his deltoid he loses the connection with the pec and the shoulder stabilizers, so he can't maintain tension between arm and body and can't shape the punch — which costs him accuracy on top of power.

The consequence Alex keeps returning to is the missing follow through. When Gane swings the arm that way, the punch ends when the arm runs out of momentum — "it dies on contact," "it runs out of steam." When you maintain tension through those cross body chains for the entire trajectory, you don't stop at the target, you're accelerating through it, moving your mass through the opponent and exponentially increasing power. Alex points to the landing itself as the tell: Gane repeatedly lands with the **back of his hand** or the back of the knuckles — a glancing blow off the ear that's uncomfortable but not consequential, not enough to break the opponent's focus. Alex contrasts this directly with Alex Pereira, who uses tension in all these chains to accelerate his fist like a baseball and accelerates through the target — exactly the shot that dropped Magomed Ankalaev. Gane's overhand, thrown with his body weight and athleticism but with proper mechanics, is "night night for Don'Tale Mayes."

Gane's straighter punches have a subtler version of the same problem. He again leads with the shoulder and moves the arm **bottom-up** instead of creating an arc. Most professional boxers throw from **top down** — the punch starts, goes up, then down, because as the arm straightens the elbow extends, the shoulder turns over and the wrist pronates. That downward motion lets the cross body chains maintain tension and accelerate through the target. Gane going down-to-up produces a punch that's easily deflected or parried; coming top-down is the punch that gets through the guard, and even hitting someone's arm it lands with acceleration and power. The same shoulder-lead shows up in his left hook — shoulder going forward, deltoid driving the punch, not the pec with the cross body chains connected to the pelvis. Alex also notes he keeps the elbow so high on the overhand that when the elbow straightens the shot runs out of steam; a bend in the elbow with tension in the chains would let him accelerate that mass through the opponent.

The fix is specific. When throwing any looping punch — hook, uppercut, overhand — **or even a straight punch — you want your palm moving toward your body.** This lets the wrist flexors stabilize the fist at impact and maintains tension in the whole chain from pelvis through torso, arm, bicep, wrist flexors, fist. Leading with the hips creates the stretch and the tension you can then accelerate on, just like a throw. Gane needs to **not turn his arm over**, but allow the contraction to occur in those chains after they're stretched — letting the fist drag behind rather than leaving with the shoulder. Alex frames the physics behind it: force equals mass times acceleration, and power is force times velocity, so speed at impact and acceleration through the target are what matter. He names Tom Aspinall as making the same mistake and offers to help both fighters' coaches directly.

## Common Mistakes
- **Leading with the shoulder / deltoid instead of the hips and cross body chains.** Creates a stretch shortening cycle in the deltoid only, breaking tension between the arm and the body — the deltoid has nothing to connect to that creates tension or acceleration through the target.
- **Turning the arm over and landing with the back of the hand or back of the knuckles.** A glancing blow off the ear; also leaves the wrist unstable, since it will bend easily at impact without the wrist flexors engaged.
- **Throwing straight punches bottom-up instead of top-down.** A down-to-up arc gets deflected or parried; the top-down arc is the punch that gets through the guard and lands with acceleration even on the arms.
- **No follow through — the punch stops at contact.** Momentum dies instead of transferring mass into and through the opponent.
- **Straightening the elbow too early on the overhand.** Keeping a bend in the elbow with tension in the chains is what lets you carry mass through the target instead of running out of steam.
- **Losing tension so the punch can't be shaped.** Without the pec and shoulder stabilizers connected, those muscles aren't available to shape the shot, which costs accuracy.
