# Deontay Wilder

## Summary
Wilder is Alex's showcase example that elite knockout power comes from the same sequence he teaches everyone — hips rotate first, cross-body kinetic chains stretch, then contract, then the arm follows — not from mystical genetics. Alex is explicit that Wilder "is not the most impressive boxer" and can be wild with his shots, but his athletic frame, joint integrity, height and reach give him a huge advantage, and when he does execute, the four punch phases (load, explode, accelerate, follow through) are all visibly there. The Helenius knockout, which looks physically impossible, is Alex's proof case: slowed down, Wilder does exactly what the framework predicts.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex frames every Wilder punch through the four distinct phases: **load** (loading the tissues either by lowering your center of gravity or through movement, priming them via the stretch shortening cycle — "just like you might dip down quickly before you jump or bounce a weight in the weight room"), **explode** (contracting those quickly stretched hip and leg muscles to power the hips into rotation), **accelerate** (the cascade of stretch shortening cycles up the body to maximize fist velocity at impact), and **follow through** (grabbing the fist at impact and transferring weight into and through the target). The anatomy in play is the cross-body chains: the spiral line (internal oblique of the hip moving back, external obliques of the hip moving forward, into the serratus anterior and shoulder blade) and the front functional line (abs to pec, crossing the shoulder joint).

The single detail Alex hunts for in every Wilder clip is **independent rotation between the hips and the torso**. Against Liakhovich, he points at the belt line: "his hips move just slightly before his torso and they move relative to his torso — so that motion right there is enough to stretch the cross body kinetic chains which he can then contract on to power the rest of the motion." On the Firtha left hook, the hip movement is much larger — "quite significant movement really stretching those cross body chains because those hips are moving independent from the torso, you can see the torso staying still" — and Alex notes how fast the fist and arm accelerate as a result, followed by weight transfer past the center line and a follow through. The order never changes: **hips, then cross body chain, then arm.**

On the long-range uppercut he walks the sequence frame by frame: weight comes down, hips start turning relative to the torso, the arm is *still* being stretched (elbow still extending), that picks up the tension, then the contraction begins and the elbow approximates — tension maintained in the chains through the rest of the motion, creating stability and involving the entire body in the punch. He is candid that it's "not the most concussive shot" — he shows it for the mechanics, not the result.

Alex also teaches what to do when you *can't* see the hip torque. On the big one-two against Fury, Wilder's trunks are high and he takes a big step, obscuring the belt line. Alex reasons it from anatomy instead: Wilder extends his hip (leg moving backwards relative to the hip), pushes off the side of the foot, and pushing the hip forward "inevitably causes stretch in the abs... if you're pushing the hip into rotation you are stretching those muscles and they have to contract or else you're going to be bending backwards." The cross-body contraction happens as a mechanism to maintain balance over the pelvis. This is his direct rebuttal to "some linear examples on YouTube where the hip is moving in lock step with the arm."

The **Robert Helenius knockout** is the fullest demonstration. From the overhead view Wilder's right leg moves inward and his hip comes forward as his arm is coming back — stretching the cross-body chains. From the second angle you can see the oblique striations, "you can see the lines that the oblique fibers follow," then he contracts that oblique; the upper pec fibers carry tension from where the arm is, then the tension spreads to the lower pec, and everything picks up the contraction and accelerates "just like throwing a ball" until impact on a solid platform with a follow through. Alex points out his shoulder does not move backwards at all. His prescribed drill for feeling this is the **barbell punch** on a landmine: keep the hips pointed forward and resist the rotation the descending weight wants to create by maintaining tension in the posterior chain (glutes, hamstrings, calves) — that stretches the cross-body chains, which you then contract to power the weight forward "like a spring or a slingshot." Alex's broader assessment: the Fury fights are "a good example of a much more skilled boxer beating a much more athletic guy."

## Common Mistakes
- **Moving the hip in lock step with the arm.** Alex explicitly calls out "some linear examples on YouTube where the hip is moving in lock step with the arm" — the hips must move *relative to* and *before* the torso to load the cross-body chains.
- **Throwing a swatting hook that follows jab mechanics.** Alex flags Wilder's own setup hook against Liakhovich as "not the best hook — just a swatting hook being used to set up the right hand."
- **Letting the arm accelerate too early.** On the Liakhovich finish, Alex notes "his arm looks like it starts to accelerate a little bit early here so it looks a little bit awkward" — the arm should be the last link in the cascade.
- **Assuming no hip torque just because you can't see it.** High trunks and a big step obscure the belt line; Alex teaches reasoning the sequence from anatomy rather than concluding the torque isn't there.
- **Neglecting the follow through.** Alex repeatedly ties impact to grabbing the fist at impact and transferring weight into and through the target — the amplitude of the follow through is a power variable, not an afterthought.
