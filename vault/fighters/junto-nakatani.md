# Junto Nakatani

## Summary
Junto Nakatani is, in Alex's framing, the pound-for-pound best boxer at long range — a tall southpaw whose long limbs and wide, low stance let him land from distances his opponents don't expect. What sets him apart isn't just the physical gift but how he maximizes it: a complete lead-hand game, a fundamentally perfect cross, and precise weight distribution from back foot to front that chains his long-range shots together. He also appears in Alex's Naoya Inoue breakdown as the opponent in the "impossible punch" sequence, where Inoue's hip hinge lets him fire from a seemingly off-balance forward-moving position.

## What Alex Teaches
Nakatani's stance is the foundation of everything. He fights southpaw with his feet wide apart — "right out the gate you see that long base." Despite being tall, he doesn't fight tall. He stays low with a wide stance, bending at the waist into a very bladed, neutral posture. Alex likes this specifically: it's easy for a taller fighter to stand tall and get hit by shorter fighters who know how to punch up. By staying low, Nakatani meets shorter opponents at their height level, neutralizing the overhand — typically the weapon of choice for shorter boxers when there's a big height difference.

The lead hand is his greatest skill. He uses a long guard — lead hand extended, elbows tucked in, and he uses the palm of the glove rather than the knuckles, giving a larger surface area and better control. The lead hand stays active, creating traffic to block, catch, or parry with minimal effort or commitment. Off that he throws two jab variations: a flicker jab, quick and snapping, to dictate pace and score on volume; and a vertical jab — a jab with a semi-upward motion, hand in a neutral or vertical position — which is particularly effective against orthodox fighters because it threads through the traffic created by the opponent's lead hand. He sets that vertical jab up with lead-hand traps, similar to Usyk: posture the lead hand high to bait engagement, then lower it to create the opening. Beyond jabs he mixes pushes, pulls, and posts — stepping back with a post to destabilize and blind. His relentless output of feints and probes with the lead hand serves as a measuring tool and reference point, telling him where he is and where his opponent is.

The cross is where the measuring pays off. If the lead hand can touch or get close to the opponent, he knows the cross will land — with proper rotation the cross reaches further than the jab. He'll measure with the lead, then throw the cross. He also uses the jab to set up his feet, stepping forward with it to blind or distract from the shot behind it, which makes it harder to read whether the cross goes head or body. He minds every technical detail and correctly rotates the knuckles at the last second to reach further and punch through the opponent's guard — a fundamentally perfect cross he can throw from inside or outside foot position.

His combinations rely on precise weight distribution. While throwing the cross he shifts weight from the back foot to the front, creating the momentum needed to follow up with a lead uppercut or swivel jab, which can also transition into a lead hook. That lead hook is thrown with different hand positions, vertically or horizontally. The horizontal hook is particularly effective against opponents who duck or move forward — the same technique Manny Pacquiao used, and which Nakatani himself explained to Naoya Inoue: the vertical lead hook can get evaded by ducking under it, but with the horizontal lead hook, when the opponent ducks, the trajectory can be adjusted downward to catch them with even more precision. He also excels at landing off the center line with rear hooks and overhands: he stays out of range on the back foot, then transitions weight from back foot to front while moving his head off the center line — a diagonal with the head while releasing the rear hand. Due to his long reach he stays extremely precise while remaining in a safe spot. He targets the body from long range with the same positional principles, stepping forward to land the rear hand downstairs.

The flaws come from the same source as the gifts. Body shots expose you more because you're lowering the hands, and his long reach amplifies that vulnerability — on rear hooks to the body especially, he risks exposing his head to a counter hook. And while he's nearly flawless at long range, he struggles on the inside. Being tall and long-limbed is a disadvantage at close range, and he compensates with volume and resilience rather than skill. His best close-quarters tools are uppercuts, cutting the action with a clinch, or spinning the opponent around — but he often stays and trades, which is dangerous against powerful inside fighters.

Separately, in the Inoue breakdown, Nakatani is the opponent against whom Inoue throws the "impossible punch" — a big right hand followed by a lead uppercut while moving forward in a seemingly off-balance position. Alex's point there is about Inoue's mechanics, not Nakatani's: Inoue loads the rear leg, launches off it to drive hips, torso, arm through the four phases (load, explode, accelerate, follow through), and lands in a clear hip hinge — "almost the same type of hinge you might have with a Romanian deadlift, a one-legged deadlift." Weight on the lead leg, knee bent, hip bent. From that hinge he has options, including the left hand.

## Common Mistakes
- **Standing tall because you're tall.** Taller fighters who stand upright get hit by shorter fighters who know how to punch up. Nakatani bends at the waist and stays low to meet shorter opponents at their level and neutralize the overhand.
- **Using the knuckles instead of the palm on a long guard.** The palm of the glove gives a larger surface area and better control for blocking, catching, and parrying.
- **Throwing the vertical lead hook against a ducking opponent.** It gets evaded under the trajectory; the horizontal hook lets you adjust the path downward instead.
- **Committing to a body hook with a long reach.** Body shots already expose you because the hands drop; long limbs amplify it, especially on the rear hook, leaving the head open to a counter hook.
- **Staying and trading on the inside when your skill set is long-range.** Nakatani's close-range toolkit is limited to uppercuts, clinching, and spinning the opponent — trading with a powerful inside fighter instead of using those tools is the risk.
