# Joe Frazier

## Summary
Joe Frazier’s left hook is one of the most iconic and destructive punches in boxing history — a shot powered by full-body torque, weight transfer onto the lead foot, and ruthless timing off his bob-and-weave defense. Alex respects it as an all-time great hook, ranking it second behind Tyson’s in his perfect fighter build, but acknowledges its devastating impact at the highest levels of heavyweight competition.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex presents Joe Frazier’s left hook as a masterclass in committing the entire body to a single, violent rotation. The punch was generated by slipping onto the lead foot, which simultaneously loaded the lead leg and positioned Frazier to twist his entire body weight through the target. This aligns with Alex’s broader principles: the weight shifts to the front leg, the hips initiate rotation, and the torso and arm follow in a kinetic cascade. Frazier wasn’t just arm-punching; he was using the torque from his lower body to maximize the stretch in the cross-body chains and then contracting everything into impact.

What makes the hook especially effective is how Frazier set it up. He mixed it with his signature bob-and-weave defense and relentless aggression, meaning he typically caught opponents right after they had thrown their own punches and left themselves open. By slipping onto that lead foot during his defensive movement, he could immediately fire the hook while his opponent was still recovering, off-balance, or exposed. That combination of timing and explosive full-body commitment turned the left hook into a fight-ending weapon.

Alex uses Frazier’s hook as a benchmark in the perfect boxer build. Initially placed at the top by Dan, Frazier’s hook was eventually overtaken by Mike Tyson’s after Jamie’s picks, settling at number two. This debate illustrates that while Frazier’s mechanics are historically devastating, Alex’s ultimate preference for Tyson’s hooks suggests he values an even more compact, efficient transfer of torque — one that doesn’t require quite as much devoted commitment to the lead leg without compromising power.

The results speak for themselves. Alex points to how both Jimmy Ellis and Bob Foster “melted to the canvas” once Frazier’s hook landed clean, and the punch famously floored the great Muhammad Ali. Even though Alex doesn’t break down every phase of the load-explode-accelerate cycle for Frazier in this video, the description — slip onto the lead foot, twist the entire body weight through, catch them off their own shot — mirrors the torque-first, cross-body-chain approach he champions. Frazier’s left hook is a violent, high-commitment version of the principle that the hips lead and the arm finishes.
