# Miguel Cotto

## Summary
Miguel Cotto was an aggressive pressure fighter who needed to close distance to land his shots, but his tendency to bounce on his toes made his movement predictable and left him vulnerable to perfectly timed attacks. After a devastating loss to Margarito, Cotto coped by getting tattoos—a psychological anchor Alex points to as an example of how fighters rebuild identity after a career-shaking defeat.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex uses Cotto to illustrate how specific mechanical habits can be exploited by a superior strategist. Cotto’s style demanded that he get close to land, while a fighter like Floyd Mayweather Jr. could extend his body and maximize reach to work safely from outside. Because Cotto couldn’t match that length, he was forced to enter Floyd’s kill zone repeatedly—and Floyd made him pay with jabs to the body, pull counters, and sharp lead rights.

A key vulnerability Alex highlights is Cotto’s habit of bouncing. He’d jump up and down, both feet leaving the ground, which meant there were windows where he literally had no options. Floyd timed those moments to land clean, especially with the jab to the stomach; when Cotto was airborne, he couldn’t move, couldn’t block, couldn’t counter—he was out of the fight for that instant. Alex shows frame‑by‑frame how Floyd waited for Cotto to rise, then speared him as gravity did half the work.

When Cotto did manage to trap Floyd against the ropes, Alex acknowledges Cotto threw a high volume of punches, but notes that almost none landed cleanly. Floyd smothered Cotto by leaning forward, then created just enough space to step back and fire crisp counters, proving that being on the ropes against an intelligent defender can still be a losing position for the aggressor. Alex emphasizes that Floyd’s strategy was cumulative; by consistently executing simple tactics, Cotto’s face got busted up and he slowed down considerably late in the fight.

Beyond the physical, Alex draws a psychological lesson from Cotto’s career. He points out that after the Margarito loss—which shattered Cotto’s identity as an unbeatable force—Cotto began getting tattoos, a visible attempt to regain a sense of personal agency. Alex frames this as a classic response to identity dissonance: external markers to anchor a shaken self‑concept. It’s a thread Alex weaves into his broader teaching about how fighters (and people) handle pressure, fame, and devastating setbacks.

## Common Mistakes
- Bouncing on the toes without a base — leaves you unable to react when you’re in the air, and a sharp opponent will time your landing to hit you.
- Trying to close distance against a longer, reach-extending fighter without feints or level changes — just walking into the kill zone lets them pick you apart.
- Throwing volume against the ropes without creating real punching space, allowing the opponent to smother and counter.
