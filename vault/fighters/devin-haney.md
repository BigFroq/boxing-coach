# Devin Haney

## Summary
Alex uses Devin Haney as his primary example of a technically skilled boxer who lacks punching power because of a specific mechanical flaw: throwing the overhand right with his hip and arm moving in lock step. Haney pushes the punch instead of using his kinetic chains, so his hip reaches its maximum forward position at the exact moment his fist lands — meaning there is no acceleration in the arm relative to the hip. Alex contrasts him directly with Manny Pacquiao, who creates torque first and then accelerates through every joint until impact.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex breaks down footage of Haney throwing overhand rights into a heavy bag with, in his words, "like zero power." The diagnosis is that Haney is moving his hips and his arm in lock step with each other. When the hip reaches the maximum forward position, that's when his arm is landing — and there's no acceleration in his arm relative to his hip. That simultaneity is the tell. A powerful punch is a sequence, not a single unified shove: first the motion in the hips from pushing off the ground with the legs, which creates a chain of events — the kinetic chains — where the hips create torque in the torso, which then creates acceleration in every joint until you land on the bag or on the person.

A critical distinction Alex draws is between pivoting and pushing off. Haney is pivoting on his back foot but not pushing off. He's pivoting on it to rotate his hips, but he's not creating torque — and if you don't have torque, you don't have acceleration. This is why Haney's punch looks rotational without producing anything. The rotation exists; the torque does not.

Alex frames the punch inside his general athletic-movement framework: a punch follows most other types of athletic moves, like throwing a baseball or swinging a racket in tennis. There's an explosion of stored elastic muscle energy through the stretch reflex in the hips, which creates torque in the torso, which creates acceleration through the kinetic chains into every joint until you make contact with the bat or racket, release the ball, or land the punch.

The Pacquiao comparison (Pacquiao vs. Ricky Hatton, a fight Alex attended) is the corrective image. When Manny throws his left, it's a chain of events — he doesn't make contact with his fist simultaneously with his hip reaching the end range. You can see his arm accelerating relative to the speed his pelvis is moving at the end range. In the Haney example, they both reach the end range at the same time. Manny creates torque and acceleration in every joint that peaks the moment he makes impact.

Alex then demonstrates both versions on the heavy bag. Mimicking Haney's mechanics is physically uncomfortable for him — "it's actually like uncomfortable for me to do because I'm so not used to it." Thrown properly, using the kinetic chains, it feels much better and lands with shearing force rather than a straight line with the arm — it feels much stronger and much more comfortable. Alex is explicit about why he makes these videos: partially so you don't fall into the same trap Devin Haney did, "getting some sus instruction, looking at the wrong videos, trying to copy the wrong things."

Haney also serves as Alex's reference case when diagnosing other fighters. Analyzing Tim Bradley's cross, Alex says: "look at his hip and his arm are in lock step, they're coming out at the same speed and his hip has reached the end point at the same time that he's landing — so this is exactly just like Devin Haney's punch." Tim Bradley "suffered from a similar problem as Devin Haney suffers from, which is a lack of punching power, and just like Devin Haney it comes down to the mechanics."

## Common Mistakes
- Moving the hips and arm in lock step, so the fist lands at the same moment the hip reaches its maximum forward position — no acceleration relative to the pelvis.
- Pushing the punch instead of using the kinetic chains to generate a chain of events (legs push off the ground → hips create torque → torso → acceleration through every joint → impact).
- Pivoting on the back foot to rotate the hips without actually pushing off — rotation without torque, and therefore without acceleration.
- Landing in a straight line with the arm rather than with shearing force.
- Copying the wrong models — taking instruction and video examples from sources that teach linear, lock-step mechanics.
