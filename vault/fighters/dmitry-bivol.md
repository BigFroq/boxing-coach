# Dmitry Bivol

## Summary
Alex uses Dmitry Bivol — specifically his majority-decision loss to Artur Beterbiev — as the clearest illustration of a world-class fighter whose *skill* is elite but whose *mechanics* leak power. Bivol has excellent movement, footwork, counter punching, body movement and the ability to fight from angles, but he rotates and leaps instead of creating torque, and he lands without stability in his crossbody kinetic chains. Alex's verdict is direct: on the occasions Bivol does create torque, the punches visibly bother Beterbiev and shift the fight — if he did it consistently, he would have won.

## What Alex Teaches
The central difference Alex draws between the two men is torque. Artur Beterbiev creates torque with nearly every punch — the hips rotate while the torso stands still, just a tenth of a second before the arm starts moving, so subtle you have to watch his belt line for two frames to catch it. Bivol, by contrast, "will occasionally use a back to front or rotating movement of his body to create momentum to power the punch but doesn't create torque and therefore loses out on a lot of potential punching power." Rotating the body is not the same thing as creating torque. Momentum behind a punch is not acceleration.

Alex names Bivol's default pattern the leaping or jumping style — "more of the Russian style, like he'll hop forward and then they pivot instead of pushing off." On these punches the arm moves in lock step with the hip, which kills the stretch and therefore kills the acceleration: "look at that hip moving with the arm, it's like the Tim Bradley hook, it's moving in lock step." He also flags Bivol lifting his heel off the ground on these shots. Because there is no hip-then-torso-then-arm sequencing, the punch has "no power on it" and "barely moves Artur." A right hand to the body thrown this way "just doesn't land with really any mustard."

The second failure Alex isolates is stability at impact — the crossbody kinetic chains. On one pivot-and-land right hand, Alex points to the shoulder moving forward in the socket: "look at his shoulder move forward in the socket, so he's not landing with really any stability... he's landing without stability in the chains that connect the shoulder to the body, the crossbody kinetic chains including the front functional line and the superficial front arm line." Without that tension, the weight cannot transfer through the opponent and there is no follow-through. This is the same architecture Alex teaches through Beterbiev, Pereira, Ilia and Fedor — the arm lags to create stretch, the chains hold tension, and the arm accelerates through the target after the torso stops. Bivol's version has neither the stretch nor the tension at landing.

Alex also flags commitment. On a right hand thrown while Beterbiev is covering up — a clear opportunity — "his right hip starts moving backwards before he's finished really making contact with his right hand." If he had landed with a little more stability and follow-through, that was an opening for strong shots.

Crucially, Alex shows the counter-examples: Bivol *can* do it. There are punches in the fight where the hips move before the arm, where Bivol manipulates his weight well — "he sets and then he's immediately able to push off" — and lands with real force. One such left hook "actually turns the fight around for a few moments," letting Bivol control the center of the ring, though he can't follow up and Beterbiev soon retakes it. Alex's admiration is genuine: Bivol's skill is excellent, he has a lot of experience, his counter punching is good, his body movement is good, he can fight from angles. He "just needs to tighten up his mechanics."

On the tactical side, Alex highlights Bivol's following tactic, which he uses routinely: Bivol follows Beterbiev's hand back and lands with his own jab. Alex notes the flaw even inside the successful counter — Bivol leads with the back of his hand and lands with a bent wrist, which glances off Beterbiev's face. And on the scorecards-versus-damage question: whatever the punch count said, "how effective were they? Artur Beterbiev doesn't really look like he's even been in a fight."

## Common Mistakes
- **Rotating the body instead of creating torque.** "Instead of creating torque he's just like jumping with a twist and his arm follows and just doesn't have anything on it." Body rotation adds some momentum; it does not produce the hip-lag-arm stretch that generates acceleration.
- **Arm moving in lock step with the hip.** No lag, no stretch of the crossbody chains, no stretch-shortening cycle — "like the Tim Bradley hook."
- **The leaping/jumping style — hopping forward and pivoting instead of pushing off.** Alex ties this to lifting the heel off the ground and to punches that land with no mustard.
- **Landing without stability in the crossbody chains.** The shoulder slides forward relative to the scapula, so weight cannot transfer through the opponent and there is no follow-through.
- **Pulling off the punch before impact is finished.** The right hip starting backwards before contact completes — abandoning a committed shot against a covering-up opponent.
- **Landing with a bent wrist and leading with the back of the hand** on the following-tactic jab, so the punch glances off rather than landing clean.
