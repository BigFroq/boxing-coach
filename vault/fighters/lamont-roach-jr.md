# Lamont Roach Jr

## Summary
Lamont Roach Jr is a technically sound, defensively responsible fighter who, as a 10-to-1 underdog, used precise distance management, unshakeable awareness, and an aggressive-yet-defensive style to shut down Gervonta “Tank” Davis. He walked Tank down all night, blocked or slipped his biggest shots, countered off those misses, and controlled the center of the ring. Alex emphasizes that if the knockdown had been called correctly, Roach would have won the fight, and notes that better punch mechanics and a touch more power would have made the victory even clearer.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex breaks down how Roach’s ring IQ and composure gave him an answer for everything Tank tried. Roach constantly maintained a distance that allowed him to bait Tank’s attacks, then slip or block them and immediately take back the center. He was “very aware of Tanks range and is able to maintain appropriate distance to bait slip and counter when appropriate.” That awareness let him consistently walk Tank down, cut off the ring, and force Tank to fight while moving backward — a position where Tank’s offense fell apart.

The core of Roach’s success was an aggressive yet defensive style. He applied relentless forward pressure, but never recklessly; every advance was covered by a high guard and alert head movement. Alex shows sequences where Tank would load up a big left hand, and Roach would casually block it or slip it, then answer with counters that prevented Tank from sitting down on his power. “Roach would just block or slip Tank’s big offensive shots with his great defense and awareness roach would also counter preventing tank from really throwing a power shot with abandon.” That defensive confidence allowed Roach to trap Tank on the ropes and corners, where Tank’s options were limited to running, clinching, or spinning out.

A key technical lesson Alex draws from the fight is how Roach systematically nullified Tank’s lead left hand. In the early rounds, Roach slipped a few lefts and threw a counter, but as the fight wore on, he got “very accurate and very consistent countering Tank’s left hand.” Alex highlights a slip-counter pattern: Roach senses the left hand coming, slips to his right putting weight onto his right foot, then uses that weight transfer to fire back with a right hand. This anticipation and clean weight transfer made Tank’s most dangerous weapon predictable and ineffective.

Alex compares Roach’s defensive approach to Winky Wright: “Lamont reminds me a lot of Winky uh good high defense very defensive and able to sneak punches in when the opponent doesn’t expect it.” Like Wright, Roach used a high, solid guard that handled most incoming fire while staying relaxed enough to counter. His calmness stood out — Alex points out that Roach looked “much more Cal and relaxed than Tank’s other opponents,” which came from confidence in his defense and his ability to stay attentive throughout the fight.

Even with all those defensive and tactical wins, Alex notes that Roach’s offensive mechanics left a little on the table. “If Lamont had a little bit better mechanics a little bit more power on his shots I think he would have had even more success and won a more decisive Victory.” This isn’t a criticism of his game plan, but a recognition that with sharper punch delivery, Roach’s well-timed counters and entries could have done even more damage against Tank’s guard.
