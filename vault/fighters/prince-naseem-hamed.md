# Prince Naseem Hamed

## Summary
Prince Naseem Hamed is one of Alex's favorite boxers of all time — very unorthodox, very exciting to watch, and a fighter whose fundamentals weren't really there but who could generate scary power. Alex uses Naseem's knockout of Augie Sanchez as a case study to demonstrate the four phases of the power punch: load, explode, impact, and follow-through. His compact, explosive delivery and strong legs make him a prime example of loading and exploding to generate knockout force.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex treats Prince Naseem Hamed as a case study in raw power generation despite unorthodox fundamentals. He acknowledges that once Naseem progressed to fighting "really A+ guys" (noting that Barrera was his next fight after Sanchez), he struggled a little bit — but Alex insists this doesn't take anything away from how he was able to generate power. A big part of that power came from his strong legs, which is where a lot of the power comes from, especially with the load phase and the explode phase.

The core of Alex's breakdown is the phases of the punch mapped onto a boxing swing. Naseem drops his weight down — Alex repeatedly calls attention to how you can literally see him drop his weight, sometimes onto his rear foot, sometimes onto his front foot in the case of the hook. When he's at the bottom of that load, he explodes out with his hips. Alex frames the explode phase as "the fall if you're considering a boxing swing" — he's transferring his weight forward, pivoting on the back foot, exploding with the hips, reaching the moment of impact, and following through. Crucially, Naseem does this incredibly fast and resets immediately, ready to go again.

Alex highlights the accelerating arc of the arm. When you rotate your upper body with your fist out from your body, the fist moves faster; but when you add the arc with your arm, it rapidly accelerates. You can see Naseem's arm accelerating faster than his body as the punch comes through the arc. On his best-connected shots, the follow-through is dramatic — Alex points out his fist goes "like a foot past Auggie's head." This deep follow-through is what creates the acceleration that finishes an opponent.

Alex emphasizes that this power does NOT require a big wind-up. As he puts it, "It's not like you bring your fist all the way back, cocking up and then swinging around in a wild arc. It's a short compact punch." The recipe he distills from Naseem is: loading your rear foot (or front foot in the case of the hook), loading your weight down, falling forward, exploding with your hips, and punching through the target with the follow-through. When it all comes together, Alex compares it to "a super move from Street Fighter."

Elsewhere, Alex notes Naseem's uppercut as a signature weapon — wild, flashy, and devastating when it landed. He threw it from awkward angles, sometimes leaping in, which made it unpredictable, and his reflexes allowed him to get away with the risky delivery, stunning opponents who never saw it coming.

## Common Mistakes
- Bringing the fist all the way back, cocking up, and swinging around in a wild arc — Alex contrasts this with Naseem's short, compact loading motion, which is fast and doesn't have to take a long time.
- Neglecting the follow-through — Naseem's fist goes about a foot past the opponent's head on his best shots, and that deep follow-through is what produces the finishing acceleration.
