# Manny Pacquiao

## Summary
Alex uses Pacquiao as his primary positive example of kinetic chain punching — the fighter to copy when you want to see torque, acceleration, and shearing force done correctly. In the Devin Haney breakdown, Pacquiao's overhand left against Ricky Hatton is the "what to do" reference against Haney's "what not to do." Pacquiao is also the panel's pick for the best combination puncher in boxing, and Alex credits him with the offset right/lead timing trick that changes the beat of a punch.

## What Alex Teaches
The core lesson Alex draws from Pacquiao is the kinetic chain. When Pacquiao throws his left, it's a **chain of events** — he doesn't make contact with his fist at the same moment his hip reaches end range. He creates torque with his hips first, which then creates acceleration in every joint, culminating with impact against the opponent's jaw. Alex says you can literally see his arm accelerating relative to the speed his pelvis is moving. This is the opposite of Devin Haney and Tim Bradley, whose hip and arm move **in lock step** and reach the end point at the same time — pushing the punch instead of using the chains. Alex uses the Pacquiao–Hatton knockout, frame by frame, to prove the difference.

A punch follows the same mechanics as other athletic moves — throwing a baseball, swinging a racket in tennis. There's an explosion of stored elastic muscle energy through the stretch reflex in the hips, which creates torque in the torso, which creates acceleration through every joint until you make contact with the bat, racket, or fist. Pacquiao is Alex's live-footage proof of this. Alex demonstrates both versions on the heavy bag: Haney's arm-and-hip-in-lockstep push, which he says is "actually uncomfortable for me to do because I'm so not used to it," versus Manny's — hips creating movement, torque in the torso, acceleration through every joint until peak acceleration meets the target. Manny's version lands with **shearing force**, not a straight line with the arm, and it feels both stronger and more comfortable.

Alex also identifies a specific Pacquiao tactic he calls the **offset right** (or offset lead right / offset right cross). Instead of a fluid cross where the arm follows the weight transfer, there's a beat — the weight transfers to the lead foot and the punch comes *after*. That offset timing means the opponent isn't expecting the punch in the way you're throwing it. Alex points it out in Ilia Topuria's win over Max Holloway and notes: "Manny Pacquiao does this a lot, and you'll see it — now that I mention it, you'll probably start seeing it a lot if you watch one of his fights."

On the skills side, Pacquiao is the panel's number one for **combination punching**, both in the audience vote and in the expert picks, securing the slot in the "technically perfect boxer." As a southpaw with blinding hand speed he didn't throw two or three punches — he'd land six or seven punch flurries from unpredictable angles, and they weren't wild: they were timed accurately and designed to overwhelm. He could dart in and out of range quickly, letting quick burst combinations go before moving out to the outside angle, and the 1-2-1 out of nowhere kept catching opponents off guard for years. His combinations were explosive and relentless — one barrage after another without pause, overwhelming defenses with sheer speed and awkward angles, turning every exchange into chaos.

Pacquiao also appears on the receiving end of the analysis. In the Tim Bradley breakdown, all of Bradley's flawed punches — the disconnected arm-punch jab to the body, the lock-step cross, the linear hook with no snap, the "dragon punch" uppercut — are thrown at Pacquiao, and Alex notes "none of these bother Manny at all." Juan Manuel Marquez's dramatic knockout of Pacquiao is cited as the epitome of counter-punching brilliance.

## Common Mistakes
- Throwing the overhand with hip and fist **in lock step** — both reaching end range at the same moment. That's pushing the punch, not using the kinetic chains. Pacquiao's arm accelerates *relative to* his pelvis.
- Pivoting on the back foot to rotate the hips instead of **pushing off** it. Alex: "there's a big distinction there... if you don't have torque you don't have acceleration."
- Copying the wrong footage. Alex makes these videos "partially so you don't fall into the same trap that maybe Devin Haney did — getting some sus instruction, looking at the wrong videos, trying to copy the wrong things."
- Landing in a straight line with the arm instead of with **shearing force**.
