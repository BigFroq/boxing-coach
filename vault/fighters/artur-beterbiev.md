# Artur Beterbiev

## Summary
Alex uses Beterbiev as the clearest modern demonstration of hip torque — creating torque with nearly every punch, not just some. In the Bivol fight, Alex frames the entire outcome as a mechanics story: Beterbiev's hip torque produced harder, more effective blows and ring control, while Bivol's rotation-without-torque left power on the table. The torque itself is subtle — a couple of frames of belt-line movement — but everything follows it.

## What Alex Teaches
The core contrast Alex draws between Beterbiev and Bivol is that **Artur Beterbiev creates torque with nearly every punch**. Bivol will occasionally use a back-to-front or rotating movement of his body to create momentum to power the punch, but doesn't create torque, and therefore loses out on a lot of potential punching power. Alex is explicit that this is the difference-maker in the fight: Bivol landed maybe more punches by the punch count, but the question is how effective they were — and Beterbiev "doesn't really look like he's even been in a fight" afterward, marked up only a little.

To read Beterbiev's torque, Alex tells you to **watch the belt line**. The hip movement starts before the arm starts moving — "just a tenth of a second before" — and then the hip finishes moving while the arm is still going. That's the signature: hips lead, hips stop, arm continues accelerating. Alex repeatedly notes how *subtle* it is — "it's very subtle and it only takes a couple of frames," "he has good genetics, subtle movement of the hip and everything follows." The torque powers the upper body, which powers the punch, and the weight transfers to the lead foot. This is the same hip-torso-arm sequence Alex shows in Pereira, Shavers, and Hearns, only compressed into two frames.

Alex also credits Beterbiev's **management of distance and ring control**. He shows Beterbiev slipping Bivol's right hand and landing his own very strong cross — "biggest punch of the fight so far" — and finishing with a jab. When Bivol does briefly turn the fight with a properly torqued left hook and takes the center of the ring, he can't follow up with strong shots, and Artur shortly thereafter regains control of the center.

On the other side of the comparison, Alex catalogs what Beterbiev has that Bivol lacks. Bivol throws hooks in a "leaping" or "jumping style" — hopping forward and pivoting instead of pushing off — with the arm moving in **lock step** with the hip, creating no acceleration and no torque, and lifting the heel off the ground. Alex calls it "like the Tim Bradley hook." He also shows Bivol landing without stability in the **cross-body kinetic chains** — the front functional line and the superficial front arm line — where the shoulder moves forward in the socket relative to the scapula, so he can't transfer his weight through his opponent. And on one right hand where Bivol could have committed, his right hip starts moving backwards before he's finished making contact.

Alex's verdict is that this is fixable, not fundamental: he likes Bivol, rates his skill, experience, counter punching, body movement, and ability to fight from angles — "just needs to tighten up his mechanics." If Bivol had used his kinetic chains and created torque a little bit better throughout, Alex thinks he would have won the fight.

## Common Mistakes
- **Rotating the body without creating torque** — a back-to-front or twisting movement puts some momentum behind the punch, but without hips leading the arm there's no torque and no real power.
- **Arm moving in lock step with the hip** — no lag, no stretch, no acceleration. Alex calls this "the Tim Bradley hook."
- **The jumping/leaping style** — hopping forward and then pivoting instead of pushing off; heel comes off the ground and the punch "barely moves" the opponent.
- **Landing with a loose shoulder** — the shoulder moves forward in the socket relative to the scapula, so there's no stability in the cross-body chains to transfer weight through the opponent.
- **Pulling the hip back before contact** — the rear hip starting backwards before the hand has finished making contact kills follow-through on a punch you could have committed to.
