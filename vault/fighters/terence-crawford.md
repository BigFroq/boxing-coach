# Terence Crawford

## Summary
Alex uses Terence "Bud" Crawford as the clearest living demonstration of integrated whole-body punch mechanics combined with elite positional readiness and ring IQ. Crawford's hip-driven jab, his ability to always land in a position from which he can immediately fire back, and his constant awareness of his opponent's body position are what Alex points to as the difference between a good fighter and an all-time performance. Alex considers him best pound-for-pound in the post-Floyd and Manny era of the last eight years or so.

## What Alex Teaches
The core mechanical lesson is the jab. In the Spence fight, Alex puts Crawford's jab next to Errol Spence Jr.'s side by side: Spence "throws it more like a push" with "not a whole lot of pelvic rotation behind the punch," essentially pushing it out there with his tricep. Crawford throws the same punch with "a lot of hip rotation" — "just a bit of hip pop," "that little hip flick." That rotational component is what elicits the stretch shortening cycle in the crossbody chains, which increases both speed and power of the jab and gives more stability at impact. Both men start their jabs at virtually the same time, and Bud lands first "by a pretty wide margin." Alex frames the margin precisely: one frame at 30 frames a second is 3.33 hundredths of a second, and in the F1 analogy a one-frame difference represents about a 14% speed advantage — "it might as well be a year difference." That earlier impact doesn't just land clean, it disrupts Spence's jab, makes him flinch, recoil, lose track of his target, and ruins the integrity of his punch. Compounded across a fight, that tiny differential is how Bud "basically pitched a no hitter."

The same principles show up in every power punch. On the uppercut that puts Spence down, the right hook that knocks him down, and the left cross, Alex traces the identical sequence frame by frame: weight comes down, the arm drops and lags back creating stretch in the cross body chains between the torso and arm, the hips rotate independently of the torso — "just a couple of frames right there, and that's all the rotation that you need" — then the torso picks up the contraction along with the arm, which accelerates until impact, with weight transferring past the center line for the follow through. Load, explode, accelerate, follow through. On one left hook Alex points to the visible striations in Bud's pec and oblique as the muscles pick up the contraction. All those chains create "a Cascade of stretch shortening Cycles which accelerates the punch."

Positional readiness is the other half, and Alex says it's arguably more important than raw power. Crawford is "always ready to strike" — his rear leg is behind him, his weight is neutral between his feet or on the back foot but still over it, so he can push off at a split-second's notice. When Spence pushes him back, Bud puts his legs back under him, "almost like gallops back," lands on that rear leg, absorbs the weight and immediately pushes off in a plyometric fashion to fire back. Alex calls this loading through movement: a small step back after an opponent's jab loads the tissues and immediately powers the return shot. When he gets too close to throw meaningfully, he takes a small step back to create space and avoid smothering himself, then fires with full hip rotation. Alex attributes the balance and the perpetual sprawl-like readiness to Crawford's wrestling background — someone comes in, you put your legs behind you and you can control the guy.

Against Canelo, this readiness becomes a whole strategy. Alex shows Canelo repeatedly throwing a big stepping hook to the body with no follow-up plan, turning his knee inward, then covering up with a high tight guard and his weight way back over his rear foot — a position from which he must first lower his center of gravity and move it forward before he can push off at all. Bud reads that moment of vulnerability every time and takes what Alex calls freebies, including "a beautiful little shovel hook that I almost never see anyone throw this anymore." Crawford also stays on "the edge of Canelo's bubble" — constantly repositioning, moving in and out with the upper body over neutral feet, inside to bait and outside to follow up. He uses the jab to interrupt, disrupt and control, keeping Canelo perpetually off balance and on his back foot, and fights southpaw virtually the entire fight, keeping great foot position and circling away from Canelo's right hand. When Canelo tries to walk him down to the ropes, Bud disrupts with a jab or lead left, makes room, and escapes to the weak side.

Alex emphasizes attention as much as position. Bud's eyes are always on his opponent — he never really shuts down even when getting tagged, and his awareness comes straight back on Canelo after taking a shot. Canelo, by contrast, has his vision blocked by his own high guard and his attention lapses every time he's hit. Alex also highlights Bud's baiting: the pull counter where he sticks his face just close enough for Spence to take the bait, and the feint where he looks at Spence's body so Spence reacts to a body jab and Bud goes to the head. When Canelo transfers his weight forward without intent to punch and his back foot leaves the ground, Bud reads it and disrupts him immediately — "giga Chad IQ."

## Common Mistakes
- Throwing a push jab with the tricep and no pelvic rotation, like Spence — no rotational component means no stretch shortening cycle in the crossbody chains, so no speed or power.
- Moving the hips and torso as one rigid unit (the linear pivoting base style) instead of sequencing hips, torso, arm. Alex measures a two-frame loss at 60fps in his own side-by-side demonstration — exactly the Spence-vs-Bud gap.
- Letting your center of gravity get too far back over the rear foot with a high tight guard. From there you must lower and move your weight forward before you can push off, so you can't respond to anything.
- Standing square to your opponent. A bladed stance gives more options; square, you can't throw a meaningful right hand without first lowering your center and stepping.
- Throwing high-commitment shots with no follow-up plan, like Canelo's stepping body hook — turning the knee inward limits movement possibilities and leaves you covering up with no head movement available.
- Staying in range trying to exchange big shots instead of working the edge of your opponent's bubble.
