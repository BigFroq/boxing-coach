# Naoya Inoue

## Summary
Naoya Inoue is Alex's showcase example of a small, fast fighter who generates knockout power through torque and the cross-body kinetic chains rather than size. Alex breaks Inoue's right hand and hook down through the four phases — load, explode, accelerate, follow through — showing how his hips explode into rotation first, stretching the spiral line and front functional line, which then contract to whip the torso and arm forward. Inoue is also Alex's case study in *tradeoffs*: the same extreme torque that ends fights sometimes transfers his weight into unsupported space and leaves him off balance.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex uses Inoue to teach the anatomy of the punch. Looking at a right hand against Nonito Donaire, he shows the hips rotating first, which creates a stretch in the cross-body kinetic chains — the spiral line (internal obliques in yellow, external obliques in orange, up into the serratus anterior in pink, which connects to the shoulder blade) and the front functional line (the abs and the pec, in blue). Both lines connect with the arm. Those muscles contract off the stretch the hip rotation created, powering the torso into rotation. As the torso rotates it moves away from the arm, creating a new stretch between arm and torso; the arm lines then contract off that stretch and power the arm into movement. "It's a chain of events" — not one robotic motion, and not the "linear style pivoting techniques that these guys are showing you." The hook works the same way. Alex is explicit that there isn't one kinetic chain: "Some people refer to the kinetic chain as a singular... however, there are several throughout the body that work together to create power."

Against the "you can't teach this" claim about Inoue, Alex pushes back hard: "I think that's one of the most defeatist attitudes I've ever heard in sports. They don't tell you that you're either born throwing a ball or swinging a tennis racket... If you can throw a ball, you can learn how to throw a punch with power."

On the mechanics of specific punches: the right hand against Jason Maloney is remarkably short. Alex points out that Inoue does not bring his fist back at all — even top boxers will draw the arm back a little — he starts by moving his pelvis, creating torque, and his hand moves out slightly to the side but almost all the movement is forward. Hips go forward, the torso is largely stationary, then the torso moves with the arm and accelerates through the target. On the straight punch against Luis Nery, Alex flags the same sequence with an added detail: Inoue drops his center of gravity right before firing, then immediately pushes the hip forward — load, explode, accelerate, follow through. Alex is candid that Inoue's torque is hard to film: "it was really difficult to find good examples of Inoue creating torque. He's small and he's fast and not a lot of views give you a good idea of how he creates torque" — but he assures viewers Inoue is creating torque on virtually all of his big punches.

The hook is where Alex draws the tradeoff. Unlike a rear hook, where the lead leg takes the weight transfer, on the lead hook Inoue "is transferring his weight to an unsupported position." Alex shows a short left hook with a ton of power behind it as proof that Inoue doesn't have to use so much torque that it throws his body off balance to land effectively. He shows a better version where Inoue stays on balance afterward and can immediately throw a second hook — body, head, head — something you can't do if you're torquing on your front toes or using the pivoting style, because you're never in a position to follow up. Then he shows the failure case against Donaire: Inoue torques so hard that his weight goes forward into empty space, there's no support for the transfer, and when he gets back on balance "he's got a fist in his face" — he eats a jab. Alex notes Inoue recovers and slips the follow-ups, and that a prime Donaire might have punished it more, but "something like this, eventually it could catch up with him."

In the "impossible punch" against Junto Nakatani — a big right hand followed by a lead uppercut thrown while moving forward in a seemingly off-balance position — Alex identifies the hip hinge as the enabling mechanic. Inoue lowers his weight, loads the rear leg, launches off it to drive his hips forward: hips, torso, arm. Then he lands in a clearly hinged position — "almost the same type of hinge you might have with a Romanian deadlift, a one-legged deadlift" — weight on the lead leg, knee bent, hip bent. From there he has options: he can throw the left hand because of his hip and leg position. Alex calls the hip hinge "a fundamental athletic organizational pattern for your body that's used in almost every sport," and vital to master for boxing. These are the biomechanical principles that let you throw with speed and power even from odd positions.

Finally, Alex uses Inoue's opponents as counter-examples. Paul Butler carries a very high, very tight guard with a lot of tension in the shoulders, which inevitably slows him down — "a relaxed muscle is able to contract more than a muscle that's tight." Because of that tight guard Butler can't use proper mechanics, and his jab is "virtually all powered by his tricep." Inoue, by contrast, "is always looking relaxed," and against him you can't afford the time it takes to unclench. Alex also highlights Inoue's adaptability and counter-punching against Marlon Tapales: Tapales throws a straight left, lands it slightly on a repeat, and Inoue gets smart to it — sensing the third attempt and intercepting him before he can throw it. "Little by little, starts from the outside, uses his speed, uses his timing to slowly break his opponent down until he gets closer on the inside and then ends them."

## Common Mistakes
- Torquing so hard on the lead hook that your weight transfers into unsupported space — you land off balance, can't follow up, and eat the return shot (Alex shows this happening to Inoue himself against Donaire).
- Creating torque from the toes of the lead foot, or using the pivoting style — it leaves you unable to throw a second hook off the first.
- Bringing the fist back before throwing. Inoue's power hand doesn't draw back at all; the movement starts at the pelvis and travels forward.
- Carrying a tight, high guard with tension in the shoulders (Paul Butler) — a tight muscle contracts less than a relaxed one, so you're slower, and the punch ends up powered by the tricep alone instead of the whole body.
- Treating the punch as one robotic motion, or as a single kinetic chain. It's a chain of events across several chains working together.
