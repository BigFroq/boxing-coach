# Earnie Shavers

## Summary
Earnie Shavers is Alex's case study in how a "thick boy" with big bones, long arms for his size, and loose, relaxed muscles generated devastating knockout power through kinetic chains rather than pure muscle. Shavers hits every one of Alex's fundamental principles automatically — load, explode, accelerate, follow through, with the stretch shortening cycle running through his crossbody chains. His limitation is skill, not power: a poor jab and heavy feet kept him out of the top echelon despite contender-level knockout ability.

## What Alex Teaches
The physical starting point is Shavers' build and tissue quality. He isn't huge, but he is wide, with big bones and long arms for his size — and crucially, loose, relaxed muscles. Alex's rule: when a muscle is relaxed, it can contract more than if it's tight, and that is potentially one of the reasons for Ernie's success. Watching Shavers in the ring, his tissues are not tense, and Alex flags that relaxation as a huge benefit and probably one of the reasons he's able to punch pretty hard.

The signature mechanic Alex isolates over and over is the hips turning while the arm is still moving backwards. On the compact short left hook against Ken Norton, Shavers' hips start to turn as his arm is moving back, stretching all those crossbody chains between the hip and the arm so they're able to contract from a fully stretched position for a more emphatic contraction and a faster acceleration. Alex notes Ernie even picks up the contraction a little earlier than expected — it's versatile. The same pattern drives the body shot against Ali: Ernie lowers his center of gravity, pushes off with his left leg, the hip goes forward as the arm moves back, then everything comes forward and the stretch is contracted on, creating rapid acceleration until impact.

The uppercut is the concept Alex says was one of the hardest or weirdest things for him to conceptualize during his own development: the uppercut is powered by hip rotation. Shavers throws it like a spiral — almost like a hook, except he's bent to the side. It's not a linear push, it's an oblique hook, which is how most of these guys get power in their uppercut. Before the big uppercut against Norton, Ernie's weight comes down and he then straightens out his lower back, going from what appears to be a flexed spine to an extended spine. Alex ties this back to his squat video: in a flexed position you're not going to be able to recruit the glutes as well as possible. Going flexed-to-extended stretches the glutes and hamstrings, allowing them to explode with contraction to turn the hips, turn the torso, and power the uppercut.

On the straight punches, Alex uses Shavers to define the cross–overhand spectrum. The overhand right against Larry Holmes shows lateral sidebending and rotation, and because of Ernie's distance from Larry it's necessarily an overhand and not a cross — Larry is moving backwards, so Ernie has to loop and extend to reach him. The degree of the loop determines whether it's an overhand or a cross: a slight loop is more of a cross, more loop makes it an overhand. On the cross that lands on Ali, Shavers is already in a coiled position with his tissues loaded, and the hip coming forward inherently stretches the crossbody kinetic chains with hip extension. There's not a whole lot of lag with Ernie — his arm is already starting to go forward as the hip drives. And the weight transfer is huge for Ernie: all that weight he's pushed forward continues going forward behind the shot.

The limitation Alex is explicit about: Shavers has a poor jab — he doesn't snap it out, he doesn't use hip turn to power it — and he's very heavy on his feet. Alex is careful to say you can punch hard and still be able to move well, as we've seen a lot. Against lower-tier fighters Shavers absolutely dominated, but against the highest tier he had a lot of trouble. He lacks mobility against guys who are really good boxers and able to adapt. He's not exactly a one-trick pony — he has good boxing skills, just not highest echelon — and Alex places him in the range of a Shannon Briggsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgsgs, Hasim Rahman, or David Tua: great knockout power, contender power, contender skill, just missing that extra little bit of skill the very top echelon have. That's what cost him the ultimate success of reigning world champion and multiple title holder against Larry Holmes, Muhammad Ali, Jerry Quarry and Tex Cobb.

## Common Mistakes
- Throwing the uppercut as a linear push instead of a spiral. Shavers' uppercut is an oblique hook powered by hip rotation, not a straight lift.
- Punching from a flexed spine. In a flexed position you can't recruit the glutes as well as possible — Shavers goes from flexed to extended spine before the uppercut to stretch the glutes and hamstrings.
- Throwing the jab without hip turn. Alex flags this as Shavers' own flaw: he doesn't snap it out and doesn't use hip turn to power it.
- Punching with tight, tense muscles. A tight muscle contracts less and slower — Shavers' loose, relaxed tissues are part of why he hits so hard.
- Assuming power requires being heavy-footed. Alex is explicit that you can punch hard and still be able to move well; Shavers' heavy feet are a limitation, not a requirement of power.
