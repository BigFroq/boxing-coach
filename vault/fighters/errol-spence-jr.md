# Errol Spence Jr.

## Summary
Alex uses Errol Spence Jr. — specifically his 2023 fight with Terence "Bud" Crawford — as the primary real-world demonstration of what happens when a world-class fighter throws with linear, disconnected mechanics instead of integrated whole-body mechanics. Spence throws his jab "more like a push," using his tricep without meaningful pelvic rotation, which costs him a one-to-two-frame speed disadvantage against Crawford's hip-driven jab. Alex is explicit that this isn't a knock on Spence as a fighter ("I love Spence, I think he's a great boxer") — it's proof that at the championship level, tiny mechanical margins compound into shutouts.

## What Alex Teaches
The core lesson is the jab comparison. When Spence throws his jab, Alex points out you won't see a whole lot of pelvic rotation behind the punch — "it looks like he's just pushing it out there with his tricep." Crawford, by contrast, uses "just a bit of hip pop" and a "little hip flick" to elicit a stretch shortening cycle in his crossbody kinetic chains, which increases both speed and power of the jab. Alex shows sequence after sequence where both men start their jab at virtually the same moment and Crawford lands first — "by a pretty wide margin," "it might as well be a year difference." In one sequence Alex concedes Spence shows a little bit of hip rotation, but "it doesn't come close to the amount that Bud is using and it's not enough to elicit the type of stretch shortening cycle that Bud is getting."

Alex then quantifies the gap in frames, because that's where the lesson becomes actionable. Bud lands one frame before Spence — at 30 frames a second, that's 3.33 hundredths of a second. A one-frame difference represents about a 14% speed advantage. Alex is emphatic that this margin is far more important than it sounds: when Bud lands first, he doesn't just land clean, he disrupts Spence's jab entirely. Bud's earlier impact "causes Spence to flinch, recoil, lose track of his target, and it ruins the integrity of his punch." It turns Spence's jab into a glancing shot with less power, or makes him miss outright. Alex frames the jab as the most valuable, most important punch in boxing, and this fight as a showcase of exactly that.

To show how a small margin compounds, Alex uses the Formula 1 analogy. If Lando Norris is 1% faster around the track, that might be one second per lap — already huge, the difference between starting first and starting tenth on the grid. Over a race, that's a minute-plus gap. Translate the one-frame punch advantage into F1 terms and it's roughly 14 seconds a lap: "It's light years difference, and it puts the faster car in a totally different league." That's why Alex says the speed differential let Crawford "basically pitch a no hitter" — Bud lands clean every time and never lets Spence complete his shot. As the fight goes on, Spence gets busted up, slowed down further, becomes tentative with his offense, and gets completely shut down. "This tiny margin leads to much wider margins as the fight goes on."

Alex validates the finding on himself. In his side-by-side demonstration, he throws the linear pivoting base style — where the hips and torso move as one rigid unit — against the integrated whole-body style, hips, torso, arm in sequence. From a stationary position, the jab comes in two frames faster at 60 frames a second on the integrated version: "That's exactly the speed difference between Spence and Bud that I showed you in the clip." He notes he deliberately didn't sandbag the linear version, and that some independent hip-torso rotation still leaked into his linear demo because it's automatic for him now — and the integrated version was still significantly faster anyway.

Throughout the fight breakdown, Spence functions as the reference point against which Crawford's mechanics are measured — Bud's pull counter baits Spence in, his feint gets Spence to react to a body shot before going to the head, his fast hip-powered jab interrupts Spence's left cross mid-flight, and the big right hook and uppercut that put Spence down each follow the load, explode, accelerate, follow through sequence. Alex closes with genuine respect: "I love Spence, I think he's a great boxer and I'm a big fan. Terence is just on another level."

## Common Mistakes
- **Pushing the jab out with the tricep** instead of powering it with hip rotation — the arm does the work in isolation and never gets the stretch shortening cycle.
- **Moving hips and torso as one rigid unit** (the linear pivoting base style) rather than sequencing hips, then torso, then arm — this is the mechanical root of Spence's speed deficit.
- **Using some hip rotation but not enough** — Alex shows a Spence jab with a little hip movement that still fails to elicit a meaningful stretch shortening cycle. Partial rotation isn't integrated mechanics.
- **Dismissing one- and two-frame differences as trivial** — at the championship level, greatness is defined by tiny gaps, and those gaps compound into shutouts.
