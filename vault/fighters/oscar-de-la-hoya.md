# Oscar De La Hoya

## Summary
Alex uses the 2007 Mayweather–De La Hoya buildup as the primary case study in how pre-fight psychology decides fights. Oscar was a phenomenal fighter — multiple titles across multiple weight classes, a laundry list of outstanding opponents — but Alex shows how Floyd systematically took him "from aggressor to survivor" before the first bell. The technical consequence was concrete: Oscar abandoned his jab, the most important punch in boxing, and fought emotionally instead.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex sets the stage with the state of both careers. Floyd came in off wins over Sharmba Mitchell, Arturo Gatti, Zab Judah and Carlos Baldomir — "not the cream of the crop," but a lot of momentum, and he was one of the top pound-for-pound fighters at the time. Oscar had reached the pinnacle and was starting to get mixed results against the really top guys at the higher weight classes: losses to Tito Trinidad, Shane Mosley twice, and Bernard Hopkins, with recent wins over Fernando Vargas and Ricardo Mayorga. Floyd was Oscar's fourth fight in about four years since the 2003 Mosley loss — not terribly active. But Oscar was still a dangerous fight: naturally the bigger guy, a great champion, and Alex says Floyd "would really need every advantage he could get."

The tactics Alex identifies are three: overt displays of confidence through speech and physical posturing; the circus atmosphere Floyd created to draw attention onto himself and make a mockery out of Oscar; and offensive verbal aggression — smack talk — which tested Oscar's resolve and kept him on the defensive. Alex tracks Oscar's arc across the press tour. Oscar opens strong and no-nonsense — "come May 5th when I touch you you're going to hurt for a week" — but it doesn't last. Floyd gets in his face, calls him a chicken, accuses him of stealing his training gear, and Oscar visibly goes inside himself, stewing, subdued, his charismatic personality deflated.

A key structural point Alex makes is that Oscar had no flexibility. "Oscar has no flexibility to be anyone but his golden boy persona because that's who he is." Floyd was transitioning from Pretty Boy into Money Mayweather — the heel, the villain — a role that paid him twice over, since people either loved him or loved to hate him. Oscar couldn't go on the offensive without turning his own audience against him, so Floyd kept "driving in the screw" with impunity.

Alex reads the mentality gap directly from the quotes. Floyd compares himself to Sugar Ray Robinson and Muhammad Ali; Oscar calls himself "just an old dog trying to survive in this game here." Floyd routinely talks about being willing to die in the ring; Oscar's "I'm willing to punch as hard as I can as fast as I can until I can't lift my arms" comes out as a question rather than a conviction. Oscar claims Floyd will freeze on the big stage in front of a crowd bigger than the two or three thousand he's used to — which Alex flags as plainly wrong at that point in Floyd's career, and possibly Oscar just trying to justify things to himself. Alex contrasts the backstage scenes: Floyd relaxed, dancing, trading dance moves with his team; Oscar's dressing room looking "like a funeral."

The fight consequence is the technical payoff. Freddie Roach said the plan was to get under Oscar's skin, and Alex notes that a fighter who wants to punish or hurt his opponent stops doing the best boxing job possible. Alex ties it back to Roach's read on Chico Corrales against Floyd — "he just wants to fight too much, not using his jab" — and says if you rewatch Floyd–De La Hoya you'll see Oscar abandon his jab for a long stretch. Floyd came on in the later rounds as he usually does, and that sealed a split-decision win. Alex is careful to say it's impossible to know how the fight goes without the mind games, but he thinks Floyd had to do these things to maximize his chances.

## Common Mistakes
- Abandoning the jab to try to punish or hurt an opponent who got under your skin — Alex's central diagnosis of Oscar's fight.
- Letting an opponent's provocation convert your game plan from boxing into fighting. Freddie Roach's read on Corrales — "he just wants to fight too much, not using his jab" — is the same failure.
- Letting your body language and mood betray your internal state. Oscar's subdued press conferences and funeral-like dressing room gave Floyd information; Floyd's dancing and goofing around gave none.
- Building yourself up with claims you can't support ("he'll freeze on the big stage") instead of confronting the actual matchup.
- Framing yourself as a survivor. Once Oscar said "old dog trying to survive," the psychological fight was decided.
