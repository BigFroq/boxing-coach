# James Toney

## Summary
Alex teaches James Toney as the master example of linking defense directly into offense — the ability to slip a punch, keep your center of gravity over your base, and explode immediately into a fight-changing shot. Toney is also Alex's reference for straight-punch mechanics: the right hand is not thrown in a straight line, but in an arc with a downward trajectory that lands with shearing force, recruiting far more musculature than a bench-press-style push.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex uses the Toney–Michael Nunnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnn fight as his showcase for linking defense with offense. Nunn was outboxing Toney and Toney desperately needed a knockout to win. Alex points out that you can turn on virtually any James Toney fight and see him showcase this type of skill effortlessly — but he chose this sequence specifically because it dramatically turns the fight around and gives Toney a win in a fight he was losing, which emphasizes that this is a vital skill to learn. In the sequence, Toney slips to the right to avoid Nunn's left hand, and the key detail Alex highlights is body position: Toney's body is over his leg, his center of balance is not too far to one side or the other, it's right over his leg. From that defensive position he can explode with a big right hand that rocks Nunn, then follow up with a big left hook to finish the sequence. Alex frames it as big head movement to get out of the way, then snapping right back with a big right hand while maintaining balance — center of gravity over his base that he can then explode from.

On mechanics, Alex breaks down Toney against Iran Barkley — a great example of a shorter fighter beating a taller fighter, with Barkley around four inches taller (Barkley 6'1", Toney 5'9"). The technical lesson is the straight right: Toney does not throw his straight right in a straight line, because it's not a planar movement. There is an arc to it. The reason is muscle recruitment — when you throw it as an arc, you recruit more muscles than just the pecs and triceps like a bench press.

Alex explains the anatomy in detail. Toney lands with shearing force, meaning his punch is moving forward and down at impact. Extending the arm while moving it down is like a straight-arm pulldown — it works the lats, the pecs, the teres major, and for stability the abs and obliques. On screen Alex highlights teres major in blue, latissimus dorsi in red, obliques in light blue, the pec in yellow, and the long head of the triceps in green. Beyond individual muscles, throwing in an arc engages the kinetic chains — the spiral line running from one shoulder down to the opposite hip, including the glutes the lats connect to. Landing with stability recruits all of these.

The sequence Alex describes for the punch itself: Toney dips back, pushes off with his leg, turns his hips, uses the kinetic chains, and at the moment of impact all of those muscles are engaged for stability, weight transfer, and follow-through. Alex notes this is exactly what his Power Punching Blueprint teaches — using kinetic chains to recruit these muscles for more stability, better weight transfer, and better follow-through at impact — including band work like the band straight punch to feel those muscles firing when you land with shearing force.

## Common Mistakes
- Throwing the straight right in a purely straight, linear path — it's a planar movement that limits you to pecs and triceps, like a bench press, instead of recruiting the lats, teres major, and obliques.
- Losing your center of gravity to one side during head movement, so you can't explode out of the defensive position into a counter.
- Treating defense and offense as separate actions rather than linking them — Toney's slip and his right hand are one sequence, not two.
