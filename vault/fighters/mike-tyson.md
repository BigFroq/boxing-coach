# Mike Tyson

## Summary
Alex holds Tyson up as the reference standard for full-body punching mechanics — the fighter who best demonstrates torque, acceleration, and follow-through in his framework. Tyson's low center of gravity, loose hips, and relaxed musculature let him skip the load phase entirely, because he's "already loaded up all the time." Alex repeatedly uses Tyson as the benchmark against which he measures other fighters' mechanics, most notably Usyk's under-used head movement and Jake Paul's arm-driven punches.

## What Alex Teaches
Tyson's structural advantage is his body itself. Alex points out that Tyson's center of gravity is so low, his hips so flexible, and his muscles so functional that he can skip the load phase most fighters need — "he's already loaded up all the time... when they're relaxed, they're able to contract very significantly." This is why Tyson can throw from anywhere without the big telegraphing step that lesser punchers require. In the Michael Johnson fight (which Tyson called his best ever), Alex frames the mechanics as a sprinter analogy: "it's like a sprinter coming out of the starting blocks... look at him drop his weight down onto that leg, explodes off a leg, up the chain into the fist. The fist transfers the energy right into Johnson's head."

The sequence Alex traces in Tyson is always the same chain: weight shift loads the leg, push off the back foot, hips rotate first, torso follows, arm arcs behind it, acceleration at impact, then follow-through. On the left hook that dropped Johnson, Alex narrates: "his movement to the left, shifting the weight to the left, loads that foot up, loads that leg up and he's able to explode out of it... you see his hips rotating, of course. That's moving the whole kinetic chain. You got that arm going in the arc, the arm's accelerating, snap." He grounds it in the physics — "what's power, it's mass times acceleration" — noting Tyson accelerates through his punches *and* carries mass behind them.

Follow-through is the piece Alex singles out as making Tyson devastating rather than merely powerful. "Mike's got great follow through, which is why it's such a devastating puncher." On the knockout shot he measures it literally: "Look how far Mike's fist moves through Johnson's head... it goes a foot past Johnson's head. So that's a perfect follow through — acceleration and follow through." He pairs that with weight transfer: pushing off the back foot and transferring energy through the target, not to it.

Tyson's head movement is the other thing Alex teaches from him, and he uses it as the direct contrast to Usyk. "When Tyson used his head movement, moved from side to side, he would get a big stretch in his lateral hips, in his obliques, and he would use that to snap back and forth to potentially use as a platform to launch a punch." The key is base position: against Reggie Gross and Trevor Berbick, Alex points out that Mike's legs, hips, and center of gravity stay underneath him, so he's got weight on the loaded leg and "can explode from that position if he wanted to." Moving to the right loads the right leg and stretches the tissues on the left side, which he then releases into a big right hook — "load up his entire body like a coiled spring and then release that with a big right hand." Alex calls this using defensive movement offensively, and says it's "a huge skill you can practice." The prerequisites are the Tyson package: good low center of gravity, loose hips, relaxed muscles.

In the Jake Paul breakdown, Alex uses 58-year-old Tyson as the mechanical standard even in decline. "Mike has lost his step for sure, he's not the guy he was 30 years ago, however he still looks quite dangerous up close and doesn't waste any punches or movement. He still looks fluid and uses good mechanics." On the training-footage left hook to the body: "look at his hips moving first, then his body, he's creating acceleration and landing with quite good impact." From the open workout Alex notes "good head movement to get inside, not a lot of wasted movement, very economical with his movement and all of these punches count." Alex also lists Tyson among the athletic fighters — with Roy Jones Jr., Ali, Prince Naseem, and Floyd — who have "incredible freedom of movement... fluid, balanced, and able to generate explosive speed and power, and never look stiff or mechanical." In the technically-perfect-boxer build, Tyson takes both hooks and uppercuts: the uppercut works because of his small size, low center of gravity, and ball-like physique — he loads the weight on his legs and explodes, with a compact delivery and less distance for the shot to travel.

## Common Mistakes
- Moving the head side to side without keeping the legs and center of gravity underneath you — Alex's criticism of Usyk. Tyson's base stays under him so he can explode from the loaded position; Usyk's center of gravity is too high relative to his legs, so the movement is "sort of like going through the motion."
- Head movement without ballistic intent — the side-to-side has to be fast enough to elicit the stretch shortening cycle. "You just got to do it."
- Carrying background tension. Alex teaches that Tyson's power depends on relaxed muscles being able to contract significantly; a tense fighter loses speed, power, and rhythm.
- Needing a big step to generate rotation (Jake Paul's problem) — Tyson creates torque up close, on demand, without wasted movement.
