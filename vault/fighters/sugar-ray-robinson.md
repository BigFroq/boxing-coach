# Sugar Ray Robinson

## Summary
Sugar Ray Robinson's perfect left hook is the textbook benchmark for punch mechanics. Alex uses Robinson as the blueprint for the four-phase punch sequence—loading, exploding, accelerating, and impact—showing how elite fighters transfer weight, rotate the hips, and channel energy through the kinetic chain. Robinson also exemplified counter-punching mastery, blending timing with devastating power that could end fights instantly.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex breaks down Sugar Ray Robinson's signature left hook against Gene Fullmer as the definitive example of textbook punch mechanics. Every phase is on display: Robinson loads by dropping his weight and letting his arm dip slightly, then explodes with his hips rotating while his back foot pivots. During acceleration, the arm comes forward in a spiral arc as the hip rotation drives the motion. At the moment of impact, Robinson transfers his weight immediately, and because the punch had so much snap, he doesn't need excessive follow-through. Alex emphasizes that Robinson hits the tip of the chin—the farthest point from the spinal axis—which causes maximum head rotation and brain acceleration inside the skull, the direct mechanism of knockout.

The loading and exploding sequence mirrors what Alex teaches for the hook on the heavy bag: dropping weight to the side, the arm loading, then snapping with a flick of the hips. Robinson pivots on his back foot rather than the front foot, which Alex advocates because it enables a faster reset. Pivoting on the front foot wastes movement and time turning back around, whereas pivoting on the back foot keeps you ready to push off and fire again—the same principle Alex identifies in Floyd Mayweather Jr. and Roy Jones Jr.'s rapid multi-hook combinations.

Beyond the hook, Alex cites Robinson as one of the greatest counter-punchers ever. Robinson could slip a shot and fire back with a lightning-fast hook or cross that ended fights instantly. His ability to punish opponents in between their punches kept them hesitant. Fullmer never saw the knockout punch coming, which Alex highlights as a critical advantage—if you can hit an opponent without them anticipating it, you have an enormous edge. Robinson made counter-punching both an art and a weapon.
