# Phillip Ndou

## Summary
Phillip Ndou is the opponent in Alex's Floyd Mayweather case study — the seventh round of that fight is the source material for demonstrating load, explode, accelerate, follow through in a live boxing context. Ndou functions in Alex's teaching primarily as the *counter-example*: his C-shaped lumbar-flexed posture is contrasted directly against Floyd's proper hip hinge, and his head movement on impact is the visual proof of what follow-through does to the brain. Alex uses the fight to show both what a technically perfect puncher looks like and what happens to the fighter who isn't organized that way.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex walks through the seventh round of Floyd Mayweather vs. Phillip Ndou as a full case study in punch mechanics. He introduces Floyd as "a perfect boxer" — "What is boxing? It's hitting and not get hit. Hit and don't get hit." Floyd isn't known as a knockout puncher because he's a master of distance: he starts far enough away and out of range, jabs and punches until "their head starts wobbling around," and that's when he gets his knockout. Alex tells you exactly what to look for in the round: "You're gonna see a great jab. You're gonna see great straight rights. You're gonna see him, how he loads, how he transfers weight, how he follows through, and then the knockout."

The weight-shift pattern against Ndou is the core lesson. Alex shows Floyd loading — "load, load, load, load, load, load" — moving his weight back onto his back foot before exploding forward. He compares it to fencing: "it's like a sword guy, you know, with a fencing is basically what he's doing." The front foot is positioned so Floyd can reset immediately, transferring his weight backwards onto his back foot and returning to range — "of course before Phillip Ndou could throw anything, he'd already be out. He'd already be too far back to be hit." Alex also highlights Floyd's counter habit: he catches Ndou's punch, moves his head back, loads his weight back, and comes right over the top with the straight right. "So his weight's transferring forward, he's accelerating, boom, right into Ndou's head. And it's so violent." Floyd "almost leaps through the air to punch you to some extent."

On the straight right, Alex makes the range argument explicit: "Floyd's straight, right? Really it only has a distance of maybe six to eight inches further to go than his jab. That's why he's so effective with it. That's why you know, Ali was so effective with it. That's why anyone can really be so effective with it." It takes only a fraction of a second longer than the jab and you get more weight behind it.

The knockout itself is Alex's demonstration of follow-through. By the finishing sequence Floyd has closed the gap — he's much closer to Ndou because Ndou has already been knocked down in a previous round, so Floyd isn't afraid of him anymore. Alex reads Ndou's body language: his upper body is "kind of curled up," so he's not in a position to be as offensive as he could be. Then the impact: "look how far Ndou's head moves. So that's creating acceleration in the brain and that's what causes a knockout. It's 'cause of the follow through. You wanna spin that head and you don't do that by just touching it. You do it by following through." Alex says you can definitely see the concussion in the clip, and that it's more dramatic at real speed.

The stance comparison is the most direct thing Alex teaches *about* Ndou himself. Discussing the hip hinge — lowering the body so the glutes are loaded and slightly stretched, ready to explode for rotational and back-to-front power, with a straight spine as the axis of rotation — Alex puts the two fighters side by side: "You can see the difference here between Floyd and Phillip Ndou. Look at Floyd having a pretty traditional hinge position right here. And Ndou has that C-shaped curve with a lumbar flexion which is going to affect his mobility and explosive speed and power." The same fight also supplies Alex's footwork example: Floyd loading his tissues, dropping his center of gravity, pushing off from back to front with a rotational component in the hips to stretch the cross-body chains — "it lands on Phillip Ndou and he's right back out of range, ready for another shot."

Alex's prescription is repetition of the underlying drill: "there's that weight shift exercise back and forward. Basically just practice that. And then you add in the component of the arc. You launch that fist forward and you practice transferring the weight... you do it a few thousand times, which should only take you, you know, a couple of weeks of working out. You do any of the exercises a few thousand times and you will have it burned into your neurology, burned into your muscles."

## Common Mistakes
- **C-shaped spine / lumbar flexion instead of a hip hinge** — Ndou's rounded lower back is Alex's picture of the wrong loaded position. It kills mobility, explosive speed and power, because there's no straight spine to rotate around and the glutes aren't properly loaded and stretched.
- **Curling the upper body up under pressure** — Alex reads Ndou's curled posture late in the round as the reason he can't be as offensive as he could be. Defending by folding in removes your own offense.
- **Being wild and reckless without defending** — "We know you have to be wild and reckless to get your offense off, but you just can't allow the guy to paint you constantly with clean shots. You gotta defend yourself to some extent."
- **Touching the target instead of following through** — a punch that stops at the surface doesn't spin the head, so it doesn't create the brain acceleration that causes a knockout.
- **Not resetting after the punch** — Floyd's front foot placement lets him transfer weight straight back to his rear foot and get out of range before Ndou can answer. Landing and staying there is how you get countered.
