# Floyd Mayweather Jr.

## Summary
Floyd Mayweather Jr. is one of Alex's favorite boxers of all time and his primary case study for what boxing actually is: hit and don't get hit. Alex uses Floyd to demonstrate that his own punching mechanics — load, explode, accelerate, follow through, weight transfer off the back foot, hooks thrown off the lead leg without pivoting — are what elite fighters already use, even when commentators claim otherwise. He also uses Floyd's Oscar De La Hoya and Ricky Hatton promotions as the definitive study in pre-fight mind games.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex frames Floyd first and foremost as a boxer, not a power puncher. "What is boxing? It's hitting and not get hit. Hit and don't get hit. That's boxing. Floyd does his best to not get hit." Floyd is a master of distance — he starts far enough away and out of range of his opponent, jabs and picks, and only once he's opened someone up and their head starts wobbling around does he get his knockout. He doesn't go for it right off the bat. Floyd's strategy is to land single punches early to break the opponent down, then increase output as the opponent slows, gets broken down, or gets more defensive. He doesn't force combos — he throws them when the opportunity is there, allowing the condition and capacity of his opponent to dictate when he strings punches together.

Mechanically, Floyd uses almost specifically the mechanics Alex teaches — a little more liberal and loose with them, sometimes putting himself at danger, but he's experienced and knows how to use the tools appropriately. The quintessential example is the jab to the stomach: a big step from out of range to close the distance, pushing off the back foot, really extending the body to get the most length possible, landing with body weight, catching the momentum on the front leg, and then pushing himself back out of range. Alex describes the setup as fencing — "it's like a sword guy, you know, with a fencing is basically what he's doing." The reason you step when you punch is to catch your weight so you can bring it back and be in a position to throw again. Floyd's front foot is positioned so he can reset immediately — he transfers his weight backwards onto his back foot and he's at range before the opponent can throw anything. On the pull counter, Floyd's back foot is in an "on your mark" position, he baits by leaning forward, takes a quick step back onto his rear leg, then pushes off just like you would to start a race.

On hooks, Floyd loads his weight off a flat foot, powers his hips forward into rotation, arm lagging back, torso moving, arm, impact, follow through. Alex is emphatic: "if you try pivoting on a hook, you're never going to be able to put two together like this." Floyd goes head, body, and is immediately in position to follow up again — he simply wouldn't be able to bring his weight back down to the left that quickly if he were pivoting. Alex directly rebuts George Foreman's commentary that "Mayweather hasn't tried to sit down on any shot yet." He finds it amusing: obviously Floyd is landing with power, he's just following more favorable and advantageous mechanics — weight transfer, pushing off the back leg, throwing hooks with the entire body off the lead leg, pushing the hips into rotation. "So, you don't really need to like quote unquote sit down, which I think is sort of an erroneous saying anyway. It gets you thinking the wrong way about punching."

Floyd's tactical toolkit is small and repeated: jab to the stomach, lead right, lead left hook, jab to the head, and the pull counter off his opponent's jab. He stays on the edge of his opponent's bubble, using a slight forward lean to bait and elicit punches he can get away from with upper body movement, then counters. He uses feints to set up shots and reads his opponent to throw leads when they're not ready. He makes constant micro adjustments to maintain the distance he likes — just outside his opponent's reach. Against Cotto, Alex shows Floyd timing jabs to land while Cotto is bouncing up with both feet off the ground, unable to do anything about it. Against Hatton, Floyd fought from the Philly shell on the ropes, blocking with his right fist and left shoulder, weight loaded on his right leg, then firing that right hand off the loaded leg. When he couldn't immediately create distance and disengage, he'd smother or tie up — and Alex notes Ricky had a large hand in those clinches himself.

On the mental side, Alex's Master Mind Games series covers Floyd's pre-fight tactics as deliberate technique: overt displays of confidence through speech and physical posturing, a circus-like atmosphere that brought attention onto himself, offensive verbal aggression or smack talk, and relentless ridicule and complete dismissal of the opponent's skills. Against Oscar, this is where Pretty Boy became Money Mayweather — the heel, the villain persona. Floyd took Oscar from aggressor to survivor, deflated his charismatic personality, minimized him, made him feel small. Oscar had no flexibility to be anyone but his golden boy persona. The result: Oscar abandoned his jab — "the most important punch in boxing" — for long stretches and just tried to fight. Against Hatton, Floyd played matador to Ricky's bull, landing flicker jabs, straight rights and check hooks until Ricky's head was a bobble head, knocking him out in the tenth. Alex's core point on mind games: "the whole point of mind games is to take your opponent out of the present." Fighting with anger is counterproductive — it takes your head out of the immediate moment, and holding negative emotion makes you stiff and tense. Tense muscles are already partly contracted, so they have less contractile range available to generate power, and tension tires you out faster.

Alex also holds Floyd up as a model of internal identity for young fighters. Floyd's work ethic, his discipline outside the ring — avoiding drugs and alcohol — his goals to be the best fighter of all time and stay undefeated, were his own decisions. "It was his decision to become Money Mayweather. It was his decision to pursue perfection in the ring." That gave him a concrete self-identity that was unshakable against external pressure. He was flawed, sure, but he operated from the inside out, which is why he reached the heights he reached.

## Common Mistakes
- **Pivoting on the hook.** If you pivot, you can't bring your weight back down to the lead leg quickly enough to put two hooks together or follow up. Floyd loads off a flat foot and pushes his hips into rotation instead.
- **Trying to "sit down" on punches.** Alex calls this an erroneous saying that gets you thinking the wrong way about punching. Floyd lands with real power without it, using weight transfer and hip rotation.
- **Abandoning the jab.** Oscar dropped his jab under the pressure of Floyd's mind games and just tried to fight — Alex calls the jab the most important punch in boxing, and abandoning it sealed the loss.
- **Fighting angry.** Ricky came in enraged wanting to hurt Floyd. Anger takes your head out of the present moment, makes you stiff and tense, reduces the contraction available for power, and tires you out faster.
- **Failing to step back to a third-person view of an opponent's antics.** Ricky should have had someone in his camp tell him to take a step back, acknowledge the tactics Floyd was using, and put Mayweather in a box — instead his camp fed the "shut this guy up" narrative, which was detrimental to him.
