# Tim Bradley

## Summary
Tim Bradley is Alex's primary case study in what happens when a fighter's arm is disconnected from his kinetic chains. Bradley suffered from the same problem as Devin Haney — a lack of punching power that comes down entirely to mechanics, not genetics. Across his jab, jab to the body, cross, hook, and uppercut, Bradley moves his body from back to front but throws his arm on a separate, disconnected track, producing arm punches with no acceleration relative to his hips.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex opens the breakdown by naming the problem directly: "Tim Bradley suffered from a similar problem as Devin Haney suffers from, which is a lack of punching power, and just like Devin Haney it comes down to the mechanics that Tim Bradley is using." He then works through four punches — a jab to the body, a right hand, a hook, and an uppercut — showing the same fault in each, and demonstrates the corrections live on the heavy bag.

On the **jab to the body**, Bradley does move his body from back to front, but his arm is not connected to that motion. The arm comes out at an angle that isn't in line with his upper body, so he can't use his weight transfer effectively. Alex notes Bradley "kind of catches himself before the arm lands" — when you throw a punch with your entire body, the weight comes forward at the same time that you step. That's the falling step: you step to catch your weight, to catch your momentum, so you can push yourself back. Bradley instead is "already low, he's pushing and he's pushing forward, but he's not transferring his weight into his opponent — he's using his arm like that, he's just stepping with it." Alex compares it to how Winky Wright jabs: essentially pushing the punch. The fix Alex gives is a wall drill — find a wall and push all your weight from your back foot into it. That's what weight transfer feels like: the weight goes into the wall and you have to step or you'll fall down. It can be very fast because you're falling into it. And you don't have to paw with the hand down — the arm is connected to your torso with muscles and tissue, so as you move forward that momentum transfers into your opponent at the moment you catch your weight with the front foot.

On the **cross**, Alex freezes the frame and points out that Bradley's hip and his arm "are in lock step — they're coming out at the same speed, and his hip has reached the endpoint at the same time that he's landing." This is exactly Devin Haney's punch. The **hook** shows the same lock-step fault: the upper arm and the body move at the same speed, the arm is not accelerating relative to his pelvis or his upper body, so there's no snap. It's a linear motion. Alex explains the correction in terms of the arc: with a hook you're creating an arc, the arm moving in a circle, and the way to create acceleration is to *reduce the radius of the arc*. Power the hips into rotation, the arm comes out, the cross-body chains are stretched, it comes around — and then you snap it by grabbing your fist and using the muscles in the arm to shorten that arc. The moment of impact is the moment you pass the center line. On the bag he demonstrates both: moving linearly versus reducing the radius to create acceleration and snap.

The **uppercut** Alex calls awkward — Bradley "pushes it up rather than having his hip drive his torso, create torque and acceleration in his kinetic chains." Mimicking it on the bag, Alex shows Bradley pivoting on the back foot and pushing the arm up "like a Shoryuken, like a dragon punch," and notes this is extremely common: "this is actually pretty common, I see a lot of guys doing it." The correct uppercut is mediated just like most other punches — push the hips into rotation, stretch the cross-body chains, arm comes out, then it accelerates — except instead of coming from the side you're coming up. You're opening or closing the hip from the back, opening the chest, which creates the stretch you then capitalize on with a contraction. Alex points to old footage of Mike Tyson as the model. From a lead uppercut, you're loaded onto the front leg, pushing off — "just like a hook mechanic."

Alex does find one good shot. Bradley throws a hook where you can actually see acceleration relative to his hip: the hip has finished moving, and then the arm comes, with the point of weight transfer clearly visible. "If someone was in front of him right there... and then he followed through, that person is going to have a pretty bad day." But the shot puts him badly off balance, because he isn't used to practicing balance and using his kinetic chains. Alex reaches for the baseball analogy: "think of a guy swinging a baseball bat — the greatest hitters, they're very compact, they have a compact concentrated swing, it's powerful but they stay on balance. Think Josh Hamilton. Other guys, they'll swing it big and be off balance — that's Bradley right here." His weight transfers past his midline, which is oblique to where Manny is, so the apex of the hook isn't on target. To throw it effectively he'd have to tighten it up and practice it so he's on balance after he throws.

Alex closes against the HBO commentary claim that "you can't grow power overnight, you have it or you don't, and to some degree it's a mystery why some fighters have power." His rebuttal: it's not a mystery. There are principles power punchers use to generate power, and there are simply things that weak punchers don't do that prevent them from punching with power.

## Common Mistakes
- **Arm disconnected from the body's back-to-front motion.** The body steps forward but the arm comes out at an angle not in line with the upper body, so the weight transfer can't be used. Result: a pure arm punch.
- **Lock step between hip and arm.** The arm moves at the same speed as the hip and finishes when the hip finishes. Without the arm accelerating relative to the pelvis and torso, there is no snap — it's a linear motion.
- **Pushing the jab instead of transferring weight into the opponent.** Stepping with the jab but not letting the weight go into and through the target. Alex's fix: the wall push drill from the back foot.
- **Pawing with the hand down.** Unnecessary — the arm is already connected to the torso by muscle and tissue.
- **Throwing the jab in the air.** Landing the punch and landing with the feet at different times makes it all arm — "like a weird bicep and shoulder maneuver," not the cross-body kinetic chains.
- **The "dragon punch" uppercut.** Pivoting on the back foot and pushing the arm straight up instead of scooping and snapping off hip rotation and cross-body stretch. Alex flags this as very common among amateurs.
- **Not staying on balance through the shot.** Even Bradley's one good hook leaves him way off balance, because the weight transfer happens past his own midline rather than on target.
