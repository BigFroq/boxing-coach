# Ramon Dekkers

## Summary
Alex uses Ramon Dekkers as a case study in kinetic chains — chains of muscle that run through the body and produce movement through sequences of stretch and contraction rather than isolated contractions. Dekkers loaded elastic muscle energy through his hip flexors and cross body chains on nearly every strike, then contracted emphatically for devastating kicks and punches. Alex breaks his roundhouse kick into the same four phases as the punch — load, explode, accelerate, follow through — and shows Dekkers applying them even at close range where there's no room to step.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex frames Dekkers through kinetic chains: "Movement isn't done with isolated muscle contractions. Movement is performed through sequences of stretching and contracting chains of muscle." In most athletic movements — think golf swing or baseball pitch — that stretch-contract cycle is done quickly to maximize speed and power. Create a fast stretch in a muscle and you can contract it with more force. That's the stretch shortening cycle: a quick stretch followed by a powerful contraction, loading the muscle with elastic muscle energy that gets released for a faster, more explosive, more ballistic movement.

For the roundhouse kick, Alex names the specific chains involved: the deep front line (hip flexors including the psoas and iliacus, plus the adductors), the cross body chains — the front functional line connecting the rib cage to the pelvis to the lower legs, and the spiral line connecting the hip to the opposite ribs — and the quads, which cross both the hip joint and the knee joint. The four kick phases mirror the four punch phases with minor changes: load, explode, accelerate, follow through. In the load phase you step with the lead leg, which flexes the lead hip and extends the rear hip. That step loads the glutes and hamstrings on the stepping leg and puts the rear kicking leg into extension, stretching the hip flexors. The forward hip motion also stretches the cross body chains including the abs and obliques. Alex adds a bridge between phase one and phase two he'd initially left out: a slight bounce off the rear toes, loading the rear calf, which initiates the forward motion that everything else follows.

The close-range leg kick is Alex's key Dekkers example. There isn't room to take a big step to stretch all the hip flexors and cross body chains — but Dekkers still lets his kicking leg hang back as his hips move forward. Alex points to the arch in his back that then flattens, indicating contraction of the cross body chains: it goes from lumbar and right hip extension to flexion in both cases, whipping the leg around. The rear leg travels from cocked to impact in barely three frames — "it's like two and a half frames." The leg lags back, cross body chains stretched, hip flexor on the kicking leg stretched, they start contracting and get the leg into motion, the lower leg lags behind naturally, and then with the quads he extends the knee and snaps the kick into his opponent. No room for a big step, still a big emphatic contraction with a good impact.

On the hands, Alex shows Dekkers ripping a hook to the body off a flat foot — something he does frequently to double the hook, going body-head. He sets it up with a little throwaway to the head just to keep the opponent's guard high, then loads his lead leg on the left, twisting his body toward his lead leg side. Alex flags a technique fault here: when Dekkers pushes off the foot and turns his hips into rotation, opening the hip, he lets the knee collapse. "Which is not great technique in my opinion. You should try to keep your knee pointed forward." Despite that, he rips it to the body, maintains good position, and resets quickly enough to throw another to the head — very mobile.

The big right hand follows all four phases with independent hip and torso rotation. His weight comes down, his arm comes back, his hips begin the motion while the arm is still lagging and getting into position, and then everything explodes — hip, torso, arm — with a big shot. Alex notes Dekkers is able to commit more to the punch here because his opponent is in a very defensive position, not really able to see exactly what he's doing: "it's all about is your opponent in a position to be hit, right?" He uses this to answer the recurring comment that hip rotation or hip drive causes telegraphing — despite all the pros on his channel doing it. Alex's overall read: Dekkers had "a lot of stretch in those cross body chains and in his hip flexors and he's really able to contract upon them for explosive movements every time," and his punches "almost always had very bad intentions behind them."

## Common Mistakes
- Letting the knee collapse when pushing off the foot and opening the hip into rotation — the knee should stay pointed forward.
- Assuming you need a big step to load a kick. At close range there's no room, but letting the kicking leg hang back while the hips move forward still stretches the hip flexors and cross body chains.
- Believing hip rotation or hip drive to power a punch causes telegraphing — Alex gets this comment frequently despite all the pros on his channel doing it. What actually matters is whether your opponent is in a position to be hit.
- Forgetting the bounce off the rear toes that bridges load and explode — the rear calf gets loaded too, and that's what initiates the forward motion everything else follows.
