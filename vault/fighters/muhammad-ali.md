# Muhammad Ali

## Summary
Alex treats Ali primarily as a reference point for two things: the bounce step off the back foot that launches a lead right hand, and as the opponent in the Earnie Shavers footage he uses to break down kinetic-chain mechanics. Ali's footwork and movement are shown as rhythm-based, light, and constantly dancing — which both frustrated power punchers like Shavers and, at times, created the openings they needed. Alex also cites Ali alongside Floyd Mayweather as proof that the straight right, thrown from a jab-like position, is effective for anyone willing to learn the mechanics.

## What Alex Teaches
Ali is Alex's go-to example for the **bounce step** — the drill he uses to teach launching off the back foot. In the footwork video Alex has you stand with feet about shoulder-width apart, take a step back, drop your weight down, and push off the ball of your foot. You can't do this if you're scrunching up your toes. Done right, you should feel like it's propelling you forward enough that your weight naturally wants to go forward. "That's how you launch off your back foot, bounce off of it to throw a lead right hand just like Ali did, just like Floyd did, Roy Jones Jr." The end position is the same as if you were about to launch into a sprint.

In the Mayweather vs. Ndou case study, Alex uses Ali to make a point about the economics of the straight right. Floyd's straight right "only has a distance of maybe six to eight inches further to go than his jab. That's why he's so effective with it. That's why you know, Ali was so effective with it. That's why anyone can really be so effective with it." The right hand takes only a fraction of a second longer to deliver than the jab, and you can get more weight behind it. This is the same "anyone can learn this" framing Alex applies elsewhere — the mechanics are teachable, not innate.

The bulk of Ali footage Alex breaks down comes from the **Earnie Shavers fight**, where Ali is on the receiving end. Alex notes the fight "was mostly a lot of right hands. Ali was dancing around a lot," and that Ali's constant movement meant Shavers didn't get the big uppercuts and hooks he landed against a hurt Norton. But Alex repeatedly shows how Ali's movement *created* openings: "Ali moves to the right creating an opportunity for Ernie to land another hook. Despite turning in with his knee, he's still in a position to land another hook because of Ali's movement. If Ali was still in front of him, it would have been more difficult." Ali's lateral movement fed the angle rather than escaping it.

Alex walks through several Shavers punches landing on Ali as illustrations of the load-explode-accelerate-follow-through sequence. On the cross: Shavers is already in a coiled position, tissues loaded, hip comes forward which inherently stretches the cross body kinetic chains, everything follows behind — "it nails Ali in the jaw and gets him moving backwards and it certainly bothers him." Alex reads Ali's reaction directly: "you can see by that facial expression, he's pretending like it didn't bother him, but it almost certainly did." On the punch that may have put Ali down, Alex shows the full chain — weight comes down, arm relaxed, push off, cascade of hips, torso, arm — and is honest about the limits of the footage: "Ali's head moves slightly after that. It could be him just avoiding the punch, but then he goes down right afterwards. His legs are not really in the best position to balance his entire body. That may be a factor... Yeah, that looks like it m in fast speed. It looks like it misses. Yeah, probably a good call by the ref."

In the best-boxer-by-area rankings, Ali appears at #4 for footwork. His footwork "was based on rhythm. He danced around heavyweights with light bouncing steps that defied physics for a man his size. His movement let him fight at range, dictating the pace entirely on his own terms." The Ali shuffle is named as his hallmark and "a perfect example of just how quick and fluid his feet were" — his ring mobility made him "both untouchable and iconic."

## Common Mistakes
- Scrunching up your toes when practicing the bounce step — Alex is explicit that you can't push off the ball of the foot if the toes are curled.
- Moving laterally into an opponent's punching angle rather than away from it — Alex shows Ali moving to the right and handing Shavers the opening for a second hook.
- Being caught with your legs out of position to balance your body — Alex flags this as a possible factor in Ali going down against Shavers, independent of whether the punch landed clean.
