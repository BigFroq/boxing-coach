# Ilia Topuria

## Summary
Alex uses Ilia Topuria as a showcase of elite, fundamentally sound boxing biomechanics inside MMA — full kinetic chain integration, load-explode-accelerate-follow-through sequencing, and the "offset right" timing trick borrowed from Manny Pacquiao. But Alex also uses Ilia as a cautionary case: his left hook comes off the ball of his lead foot, leaving him off balance and unable to follow up, and against Justin Gaethje he abandoned the boxing that made him great in favor of hunting a predicted knockout.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex treats Ilia's mechanics as textbook examples of what he teaches on his channel. Against Max Holloway, Ilia's jab to close the distance shows a lowering of his center of gravity, an explosion forward, slight hip rotation, and his entire body catapulting the arm forward to go from out of range to landing. His jabs to the body use his entire weight — front leg lifted in the air, pushing off the back leg, all the weight coming forward into Max's stomach — instead of the arm punch Max throws while traveling backwards. On the right hand, Alex walks through the four phases: load (dipping down, knee bending, tissues dropping), explode (hip moving up and forward into rotation off the pushing leg), accelerate (arm accelerating relative to the hip, which has stopped rotating while momentum keeps carrying forward), and follow through (fist continuing into and through the opponent). Ilia loads his hips and his cross-body chains simultaneously — the right hand dips down in an arc, stretching the pec, front functional line, spiral lines, and superficial front arm line, so he can contract violently off that stretch via the stretch-shortening cycle and land in a shearing motion with stability at impact.

Alex highlights a "moment that he creates torque" — pointing at the Venom print on Ilia's trunk to show the twist over two or three frames. "Twist means torque." On the fight-ending left hook, the hip rotation takes roughly a tenth of a second — two to three frames at 30 frames per second — with the arm coming right behind, cross-body chains getting utilized, big landing.

Tactically, Alex credits Ilia with more than fundamentals. He knows how to set shots up with faints and is dangerous at any range. Alex identifies two specific tactics from his GGG-Canelo video: **following** — waiting for the opponent to throw and coming right back over the top as he lazily brings his arm back, which Floyd Mayweather does to perfection in almost every fight — and **complimenting** — reading the opponent manipulating his weight in a way that indicates he's going to throw, then preempting it and landing at the same moment the other guy expects to. Alex also names Ilia's signature timing trick the **offset right** (offset lead right / offset right cross): the weight transfers to the left foot and the right hand does *not* follow the weight transfer — it comes after, a little beat off. It changes the timing so the opponent isn't expecting the punch the way it's being thrown. Manny Pacquiao does this a lot. Alex says you can practice this yourself.

The weakness Alex returns to most is the left hook. Ilia pivots on his lead foot and throws off the ball of his foot, which transfers his weight to an area with no support. Alex acknowledges Ilia is creating torque anyway — the hip moves first and everything follows — but the problem is position: look where his rear foot is relative to his opponent. If that hook hadn't put Max down, he'd have to unpivot his hips and pivot on his back leg just to get in position to push off and throw a powerful right hand. He's closing his hip to power that lead hook instead of opening it, which would allow a better follow-up position. Alex has watched years of Ilia's training footage; the technique has improved but the uppercut and hook are "still not what I want to see." He thinks Ilia needs to unlearn it and learn to push off a more flat foot. This is endemic in gyms because people teach pivoting on the front foot — Ilia has found a way to lift his heel and still create torque, but most guys can't. Alex frames the stakes as marginal gains: "small percentages across a large amount of techniques adds up to a large percentage overall. That's the difference between someone who's great and someone who's all time."

Alex also reads Ilia psychologically. Like Tom Aspinall, Ilia carries an aura of dominance — his opponents buy into the mystique and fight reactive, anxious, jittery. But at the Oliveira weigh-in Alex saw Ilia break frame: smirking like a kid caught red-handed, looking down, eyes not fixated on Charles, breaking the staredown first — a man who still has something to prove, moving up in weight, defending an aura. Against Gaethje that framing became the whole story. Ilia publicly predicted a first-round knockout under two minutes, "got lost in the sauce," "got high on his own supply," and came out to fulfill the prediction rather than boxing and letting the knockout come to him. He carried his weight on his back leg with his feet too close together — a position with very limited options — while Gaethje played the matador and busted him up with the jab. Alex's fix: keep a normal boxing stance, bait the jab, use head movement to take his head off the line as he launches, and land. Ilia has done exactly this in other fights. He never truly adapted.

## Common Mistakes
- **Pivoting on the lead foot for the left hook.** Throwing off the ball of the foot transfers weight to an area with no support. Ilia has to reset his stance afterward, is off balance, and would have to unpivot and re-pivot on the back leg before he could follow up with a right hand. Alex wants a flatter foot, pushing off to create torque, opening the hip rather than closing it.
- **Retreating straight backwards.** Effective for Ilia in the past, but against a fast, aware fighter with great grappling it's a problem.
- **Carrying weight on the back leg while walking an opponent down.** Alex: "You need that back leg behind you in order to increase your options." Ilia brought it in close with his weight — sometimes his torso — over it, and got his feet too close together. From that position he could do "zip."
- **Abandoning your boxing to chase a knockout.** Ilia predicted the KO, walked Gaethje down, and stopped using the head movement, counters, and faints that made him great. The damage compounded with swelling and fatigue.
- **Not taking the head off the line.** When Ilia timed Gaethje's jab, slipped, and threw the cross, it landed and let him close distance. He never truly adapted to keep doing it.
