# Michael Nunnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnn

## Summary
Michael Nunnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnn appears in Alex's teaching as the *opponent* in the James Toney case study — the fighter who was winning the boxing match but got caught by a perfectly linked defense-to-offense sequence. Alex uses the Toney–Nunn knockout to show that the ability to slip a punch and explode straight back with power is a fight-winning skill, not a stylistic flourish. Nunn's role is the cautionary side of the lesson: outboxing an opponent doesn't protect you from a fighter who can convert defense into offense in one beat.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex sets the scene: "Michael Nunnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnnn was outboxing James Toney in this fight, and James Toney desperately needed a knockout in order to win." That framing is the whole point of the clip — Toney was losing on the cards, and the single skill Alex is teaching in the *Ready to Strike* series is what reversed the result. Alex chose this particular sequence over the many available Toney fights specifically because "it dramatically turns the fight around and gives James Toney a win in a fight that he was losing, which just emphasizes the point that this skill is a vital skill to learn."

The mechanics of the finish: Nunn throws his left hand, Toney slips to the right to avoid it. Alex points to what happens to Toney's body in that defensive position — "James Toney's body is over his leg, right? His center of balance is not too far to one side or the other. It's right on his over his leg." Because the center of gravity stays stacked over the base, Toney can explode out of the slip immediately. It's the right hand out of that slip that lands, rocks Nunn, and sets up the left hook that puts Nunn down. Alex calls it "the beginning of the end for Nunn."

The key detail Alex returns to on the second and third viewing is that big head movement and readiness to punch are not in conflict. "James Toney uses big head movement to get out of the way and snaps right back with a big right hand, maintaining balance, center of gravity over his base that he can then explode from." Most fighters pay for evasion by ending up leaning, weight dumped to one side, unable to fire. Toney doesn't — the defensive movement leaves him loaded rather than emptied.

Alex frames the whole thing as having the mechanics "in the bag in case you need them" — the ability to use your entire body behind your punch for more fluidity, more speed, better balance, and power on demand, so that when you're behind and need a knockout, the tool is there.

## Common Mistakes
- Assuming that outboxing an opponent on the scorecards means you're safe — a fighter who can link defense and offense can reverse a fight in one sequence.
- Moving your head off the center line so far that your center of gravity ends up outside your base, leaving you unable to explode back with a counter.
- Treating defense and offense as separate phases instead of one continuous action.
