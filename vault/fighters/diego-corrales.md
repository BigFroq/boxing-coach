# Diego Corrales

## Summary
Diego "Chico" Corrales is the fighter Alex uses as the primary case study for how a pure boxer dismantles a come-forward brawler, specifically in the Floyd Mayweather Jr. fight. Alex is fond of Corrales — he came into the Mayweather fight as "an absolute killer" with stoppage wins over Angel Manfredy, Derrick Gainer and Justin Juuko — but Floyd "makes him just look ridiculous." Corrales is the illustration of what happens when an upright, brawling stance meets a flexible, in-and-out boxer who accumulates single shots until the opponent breaks.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex frames the Mayweather–Corrales fight as a stylistic mismatch rooted in stance and movement. Chico has a pretty upright stance and wants to get closer so he can really brawl and fight. Floyd is more of a pure boxer, his movement is much more flexible, he likes to be in and out, he moves laterally very well, and he's quick and agile on his feet. That difference is what the whole breakdown hangs on — Corrales's upright, forward-wanting posture gives Floyd exactly the target he wants at exactly the range he prefers.

The fight is Alex's cleanest example of Floyd's core strategy: land single punches early to break the opponent down, then increase output as the opponent slows or gets more defensive. Alex says it directly of Corrales — Floyd lands a punch here, a punch there, eventually they accumulate, and on Chico they accumulate enough where Floyd gets him out of there. Floyd doesn't force combos; he waits for the opponent to be broken down enough to string punches together while minimizing risk. Alex points to the round where Corrales gets knocked down three times as the moment Floyd is finally "opening up with some more combos," stringing punches together head and body — that's the beginning of the end for Corrales, and Alex is explicit that Floyd opened up *based on the condition and state of his opponent*, not on a preset plan.

The mechanics Alex shows against Corrales are his standard four punch phases — load, explode, accelerate, follow through. On the jab to the stomach, Floyd throws his weight off his back foot, takes a big step from out of range to close distance, lands with body weight, and pushes himself back out of range. Alex stresses the reason you step when you punch is to catch your weight, so you can bring it back and be in a position to throw again. On the left hook that puts Corrales down, Floyd loads his weight off of the flat foot, powers his hips forward into rotation — hips move, arm lags back, torso starts moving, arm, impact, follow through. Alex emphasizes Floyd is not pivoting on that hook; if you pivot you can never put two together like that (head, then body, then in position to follow up again), because you can't bring your weight back down to the left quickly enough.

Corrales is also where Alex rebuts the George Foreman commentary that "Mayweather hasn't tried to sit down on any shot yet." Alex finds this amusing: Floyd is obviously landing with power, he's just not traditionally sitting down. He's using a weight transfer, pushing off the back leg, throwing hooks with his entire body off the lead leg and not pivoting, pushing his hips into rotation, using his entire body. So you don't really need to "sit down" — Alex calls it sort of an erroneous saying anyway, one that gets you thinking the wrong way about punching.

Alex also shows Floyd's fainting against Corrales: a faint that gets Chico to move his arms up to protect his head, then Floyd goes right downstairs, weight on his left leg, and pushes it back right back out of range. And in the De La Hoya video, Alex references Freddie Roach's assessment of Corrales versus Floyd — that Chico just wants to fight too much, not using his jab — and draws the parallel to Oscar abandoning his jab for a long period against Floyd.

## Common Mistakes
- Fighting too much and abandoning the jab against a boxer — Roach's read on Chico, and the same error Alex tracks in De La Hoya against Floyd.
- Coming forward upright and inflexible against a fighter who moves in and out and laterally, which lets him dictate range all night.
- Pivoting on the hook. Alex uses the Corrales knockdown hook to show the opposite: load off the flat foot, power the hips forward into rotation, so you can throw head then body and still be in position to follow up.
- Thinking you need to "sit down" on punches to hurt someone. Alex calls this an erroneous saying that gets you thinking the wrong way about punching — Floyd hurt Corrales with weight transfer and full-body rotation instead.
