# Canelo Alvarez

## Summary
Alex uses Canelo Alvarez as a two-sided case study: his jab mechanics are an excellent, professional-grade example of hip rotation and kinetic chain sequencing, while his hook shows correctable flaws and his performance against Crawford illustrates the concept of poor positional readiness. Canelo ranks at the top of Alex's panel lists for body shots and near the top for counter punching, but in the Crawford fight his squared stance, back-foot weight, and unplanned stepping hook to the body left him unable to respond. He's the clearest example Alex has of a great fighter losing because of body position rather than skill.

## What Alex Teaches

**The jab is the showcase.** Alex opens the Canelo mechanics breakdown by pushing back on the robotic-jab-and-hook instruction he sees online — "guys showing you how to do these punches robotic style jab hooks where they're pivoting on their front foot" — which he calls completely wrong. Canelo's jab is the counter-example. Watching it at full speed, Canelo is opening his hips, moving his right hip (the rear hip relative to the target) backwards to rotate his trunk and torso, and he also has back-to-front movement, catching his momentum with his lead foot to bring his body back into position to explode with another punch. Two planes of motion for the hips. Alex maps the specific chains: the superficial front line with the quads to push the rear hip back, the lateral line with the lateral hip muscles and TFL for the side-to-side motion, the spiral line (internal oblique into external oblique into serratus into the shoulder) for the crossbody rotation, and the front functional line, which starts with the adductor, goes into the abs, then into the big pec on the opposite side — the chain used big time in any sort of throw or a tennis serve. The principle: the more kinetic chains you utilize, the more power you generate, whether it's throwing a ball, swinging a baseball bat, or swinging a tennis racket.

**The hook is useful but flawed.** Alex is explicit that Canelo's lead hook is not a picture-perfect example. First flaw: he lets his heel come off the ground when he could generate more power keeping it flat. The most important thing is creating hip extension to power the hip rotation, and you can generate more hip extension and more power pushing off a flat foot. Alex suspects Canelo lacks some hip flexibility, which also causes the knee to come in — something you should avoid. Second flaw: he's trying way too hard. "Canelo here looks like he's trying to muscle the punch." Alex contrasts this with guys who throw powerful shots that look effortless because they're really utilizing their kinetic chains. What Canelo does right: he does not pivot on his front foot — he pushes off it to rotate his hips, powered first by the glutes through the back functional line, which extends the hip front-to-back just like pushing off in a sprint. The rear hip coming back creates stretch in the crossbody chains, and the lat's job is largely to stabilize the arm while it's moving. It's a cascade of events, not one singular robotic movement with a pivot. Alex also notes Canelo lands with his front knuckles rather than his main knuckles — landing a little prematurely — though the palm-facing-you landing on the body hook is correct.

**Positional readiness is where Canelo loses.** In the Crawford breakdown, the recurring diagnosis is that Canelo's weight is too far back on his back foot with a high guard, and from there he simply cannot fire. Alex explains the mechanics repeatedly: if your center of gravity is over your back foot, you have to move your center of gravity forward — or down and forward — before you can push off that back foot. The pelvis needs to get more neutral relative to the two feet, then you can push off to drive the hips forward and power a shot with the upper body. He also flags Canelo being too square: "if you get into that position where you're square like Canelo is and try to throw a right hand, you can't throw a meaningful one." A more bladed stance would give him more options. Alex invites the viewer to test it themselves — get in Canelo's position and try to launch something. The result of the bad position is that Crawford's shots become "freebies," because Canelo is not in a position to respond.

**The stepping hook to the body with no follow-up plan.** This is what Alex calls probably one of Canelo's biggest mistakes, and he never adapts to it all fight. The big high-commitment stepping hook to the body, thrown with the knee turning inward — which adds limited movement possibilities to an already very high-commitment punch — leaves him in a very poor position with few prospects of follow-up. He gets into positions where he could follow up with a right hand but doesn't, and instead goes defensive without head movement, just putting the guard up. He can't even use head movement in that situation. Bud, who is perpetually ready to strike, capitalizes every time — including with a beautiful little shovel hook that Alex says he almost never sees anyone throw anymore. Alex adds a lack of head movement as a compounding issue: Canelo doesn't use much head movement in this fight, which would have been appropriate against a guy like Bud, and one reason he's forced onto his back foot so frequently is that Bud keeps him off balance with the jab. When Canelo gets tagged, his attention lapses — he looks down, his arms go up, his high-tight guard blocks his vision, and his only hope is to cover up.

**Where Canelo ranks.** On the panel lists, Canelo is the top pick for body shots: explosive and compact, blended with perfect setups, targeting the liver with thudding left hooks, often disguising them behind head shots — few current fighters blend timing, power, and technique to the body as effectively. He ranks second for counter punching: his counter punching is textbook perfect and he can counter from any position — off a roll, off a slip, or off a pullback in a combination. While his combinations often steal the limelight, his counters are often the key to his fights, making opponents hesitant to throw. Alex's closing verdict on the Crawford fight is that over time, Canelo has lost some of his boxing ability — poor head movement, and he wants to come in all the time and bang.

## Common Mistakes
- **Weight too far back over the rear foot with a high guard** — from this position you cannot push off. You have to move your center of gravity down and forward until the pelvis is more neutral before you can launch anything, which costs time your opponent uses against you.
- **Being too square to the opponent** — you can't throw a meaningful right hand from a squared position. A more bladed stance gives you more options.
- **Throwing a high-commitment body hook with no plan of follow-up** — Canelo does this all night against Crawford and gets countered almost every time, then covers up instead of responding.
- **Turning the knee inward on the stepping hook** — this adds limited movement possibilities to a punch that is already high commitment.
- **Letting the heel come off the ground on the hook** — you generate more hip extension and more power pushing off a flat foot.
- **Muscling the punch instead of using the kinetic chains** — powerful shots should look effortless; forcing it means you're leaving chains unused.
- **Covering up with a high, tight guard that blocks your vision and attention** — once your attention is off your opponent you can't see the follow-up coming.
