# Max Holloway

## Summary
Alex treats Max Holloway as a good boxer whose specific technical gaps got exposed in his fight with Ilia Topuria — most notably a near-total absence of head movement and a slow, lazily retracted jab. In the breakdown, Max's height and range management kept the first round close, but as Ilia closed distance with footwork and head movement, Max became increasingly nervous and readable, and the fight ended with a big right hand and a finishing left hook. Alex uses Max primarily as the counter-example that makes Ilia's mechanics and tactics work.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex frames the fight as a good boxer being solved. He calls it "an excellent performance from Ilia against a good boxer in Max." At the start Ilia set the tone by taking the center of the ring and daring Max to engage; Max used his height in the first round to keep Ilia at distance and made it a close round. Ilia spent much of the first round feeling out Max's range and was able to adapt to the larger fighter with footwork and head movement to close the distance and start landing very effective right hands. Max had moments, but as the fight went on he "appeared to become increasingly nervous and wary of Ilia's pressure and effective blows."

The single technical flaw Alex isolates in Max is head movement. When he points out Ilia starting to bob and weave, he says plainly: "one thing that Max doesn't do well is move his head and that opens him up later on to some big right hands." Alex is explicit that this is basic stuff — "just some bobbing and weaving, pretty basic stuff" — but that these things Ilia does and Max doesn't "plays a pretty big role in the fight." The head movement isn't just defense; it's what lets Ilia close the gap and it's what leaves Max standing there to be hit.

The second flaw is a lazy jab that invites **following** — the tactic Alex covers in his GGG–Canelo video, where you look for the other guy to throw a punch and then come right back as he's bringing his arm back. Max throws a slow jab, and Alex notes "how lazily he's bringing it back, how lazily he's throwing it out and bringing it back," which Ilia capitalizes on by throwing right over the top. Alex names Floyd Mayweather as the man who does this in almost every fight to perfection — it's one of his go-to maneuvers.

The third is weight and commitment on his own punches. When Ilia goes downstairs with the jab to the body, Alex contrasts the two directly: Max is traveling backwards, "and it's just a little arm punch," whereas Ilia is using his entire weight behind that jab to the body — front leg in the air, pushing off the back leg, all that weight coming forward into Max's stomach. Max is also shown reacting predictably: he lifts his hand to parry, and Ilia immediately goes downstairs, sliding right under Max's hook. That's **complimenting** — manipulating your opponent into showing you what he's about to throw, then preempting it so you land at the same moment he expects to.

Finally, Max was beaten by timing he didn't expect. Alex highlights the **offset right** — a cross with a beat in it, where the weight transfers to the lead foot and the right hand comes *after* rather than fluidly with the transfer. "It changes the timing of your punch and so the opponent is not really expecting it in the way that you're throwing it." Manny Pacquiao does this a lot. Ilia uses it repeatedly from the second round to the finish, and Alex notes each one gets through. The end comes from a huge right hand that "puts Max on Queer Street," followed by the left hook where Ilia's hips snap in roughly two to three frames — a tenth of a second — before the arm follows. Twist means torque.

## Common Mistakes
- **No head movement.** Standing available. Basic bobbing and weaving is what stops an opponent from closing distance and landing clean right hands.
- **Lazy jab retraction.** Throwing the jab out slow and bringing it back slower invites the opponent to follow — to come right back over the top as your arm returns.
- **Arm punches while retreating.** Punching with the arm alone while traveling backwards, instead of pushing the hips forward and putting body weight behind the shot.
- **Predictable defensive reactions.** Lifting the hand to parry the same way every time tells the opponent exactly when to go downstairs.
