# Andre Ward

## Summary
Alex teaches that Andre Ward is perhaps the boxer with the highest ring IQ — a fighter who dominated not with power or speed but by outthinking his opponent and sticking to the game plan. Ward embodies the "be smart" mantra, using a long guard, adaptive stances, and strategic clinch work to control every aspect of a fight. Without being the most athletic, he was able to dominate through technique, strategy, and intelligence, making him maybe the best boxer to learn from in study.

## What Alex Teaches
Ward fights primarily from an orthodox stance but changes to southpaw to give opponents different looks and create openings. His foot positioning is bladed with his feet wider apart, similar to a Philly shell — but instead of using the shoulder roll, Ward employs a long guard, putting traffic in front of his opponent by parrying and blocking all incoming punches. Alex emphasizes that the long guard is a major part of Ward's game and that Ward is one of the boxers who really knows how to make the most of his lead hand to control the opponent. He uses various lead hand control techniques: probing to measure distance, handles and pins to pivot and pull his opponent the opposite direction, and posts — especially when retreating — to derail his opponent and blind them, making it much more difficult to get on the inside. However, Alex points out that Ward's greatest asset is his ability to adapt to what the fight demands, adopting different stances depending on the situation.

Offensively, Ward's lead hand work is impressive, starting with his stiff jab which he throws to both head and body. Alex reiterates how underrated and underused the jab to the stomach is — it steals air from the opponent and stops their momentum going forward. Because Ward uses it so frequently, it creates openings upstairs. Alex explains the tactical sequence: "I can take you here and I've hit you there three or four times. Fake, boom, come with the hook. Or if I still want to beat you up with the jab, I can think — boom — come with the jab." Ward also lands by ending combinations with the lead, catching opponents by surprise, and employs a swivel jab — riding the momentum of the rear hand motion and ending with the lead in a hybrid between a jab and a hook. This works because the opponent expects Ward to fall short after the rear hand, but the lead allows him to gain reach and catch them off guard. His counter-punching includes a catch-and-shoot motion: catching the jab, countering with the straight, and immediately minding his defense by blocking the hook.

Alex defines ring generalship as a boxer's ability to control the action and put himself into position to land clean punches. Ward's ring generalship begins with his feet — the first line of defense. Alex teaches that you don't need great feet, you just need to know how to use them and when to use them: "If a guy charges me and I take two steps backwards, that punch just fell short." Ward is equally comfortable pressing the action or boxing backwards. Many associate ring generalship with a pressure style, but Alex highlights Ward's fight against Abraham where Ward boxed backwards most of the time yet was clearly in control. Ward utilizes feints to analyze opponents, create openings, tire the opponent, and control the pace. Alex quotes the principle: "You can make a person tired of just fighting."

Ward's clinch work is something Alex breaks down into two components: knowing when to clinch and working in the clinch. Ward uses the clinch to stop an opponent's momentum — when they land clean and have momentum, Ward cuts it off immediately by clinching. He also uses it to frustrate counters, especially against power punchers, by unloading a combination and clinching right after — hitting and nullifying the option to get hit back. In the clinch, Ward does not wait for a break. He knows the clinch is still part of the fight and takes full advantage offensively to get his punches off. Alex notes a defensive flaw: Ward's lead hand, when used for posting, probing, or pushing, creates openings on the lead hand side that opponents with similar or longer reach can exploit.

## Common Mistakes
- Getting comfortable in the clinch and waiting for a break — Ward takes full advantage offensively instead of resting
- Leaving the lead hand out too long when using lead hand control techniques (posting, probing, pushing), which creates openings opponents with similar or longer reach can exploit
