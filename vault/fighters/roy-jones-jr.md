# Roy Jones Jr.

## Summary
Roy Jones Jr. is Alex's case study for how subtle, athletic weight-shifting turns into explosive punching power. In the Clinton Woods fight, Alex uses Jones to demonstrate the full progression — loading the rear foot, dropping the center of gravity, exploding off the ground, and rotating the hips to drive the cross-body chains into the arm. Jones is also Alex's reference for the bounce step and for how fast a fighter can reset the hips to fire again.

## What Alex Teaches
Alex opens the case study on Jones' straight lead right hand: "he's gonna follow all the progressions." Jones loads his rear foot, his stance is on the mark, his rear heel is off the ground, and he loads his tissues — the whole chain loads simultaneously, "including that oblique that goes into the serratus, stretches it all as he shifts his weight back." Then he drops down with gravity, loads the right leg, and you see that leg straighten out. At the same time the energy travels up the side of the body — the lateral line — into the oblique, into the serratus, into the shoulder, and through the shoulder into the punch. Alex notes Jones puts so much force behind it that "he just propels himself forward and then out."

Part of the loading phase is relaxation. Alex points out that when Jones drops his weight down, he also relaxes his arm: "his arm is real relaxed and this is part of the loading phase." The shoulder moves back, the torso moves back — so when he explodes, "that whole torso can come forward." Alex's takeaway on this is a teaching point about mastery: "when you get good at it, you'll be able to load all the tissues simultaneously."

Jones' movements are what Alex calls subtle, not exaggerated — "he's just that fit, that athletic." All fighters shift their weight back and forth, but Jones does it in such a way that he's ready to explode with a punch at any moment. Because he's so fast and disguises the load with his rhythm, opponents don't expect it: "he could be moving forward just as his rhythm or he could be moving forward with a punch. And you keep people off balance that way."

The hooks show the hip movement most dramatically. Alex tracks the sequence: weight transfers to the left leg, the knee bends, and Jones extends that leg to power the rotation of the hips. The pelvis explodes into rotation, the rear hip comes back, "those cross body chains are all getting used," and the arm comes around. Alex highlights the accelerating arc — "the arm starts a little bit wide, but then he rapidly accelerates it" as the bicep gets involved and the arc becomes smaller — followed by follow-through, and then the immediate reset. On the reset Alex is emphatic: "look how quick his reset is. He moves his hip so, so fast he can move him back and be ready to go with another shot immediately." After the weight transfer the lead leg is loaded and he comes right back into a neutral stance, ready to go again.

Alex also uses Jones as a name in the bounce-step lineage. Teaching the bounce step off the back foot — step back, drop the weight down, push off the ball of the foot — Alex says that's "how you launch off your back foot, bounce off of it to throw a lead right hand just like Ali did, just like Floyd did, Roy Jones Jr." Alex adds one caution from the Woods fight commentary about the right hand: "this is where you can really start to hurt that right hand if you're not careful how you turn it over."

## Common Mistakes
- Turning the right hand over carelessly — Alex notes this is where you can hurt that right hand if you're not careful how you turn it over.
- Exaggerating the weight shift. Jones' movements are subtle, not exaggerated; the load should be disguised inside your normal rhythm so the opponent can't read whether you're stepping or punching.
- Tensing the arm during the load. The arm stays relaxed as the weight drops — tension in the loading phase blocks the simultaneous loading of the tissues.
- Failing to reset. Alex emphasizes that after follow-through, the weight lands on the lead leg and the fighter returns to a neutral stance ready to fire again — no reset means no second shot.
